2. Supplier Power / Infrastructure Concentration

Adobe does not have a traditional supply chain. Its "suppliers" are:

  • Cloud infrastructure providers (AWS, Azure, GCP) — multi-cloud by design [dossier note]. No single-source. This is a deliberate operational hedge, not an afterthought.
  • Content licensors for Adobe Stock — distributed, not concentrated.
  • OpenAI / Google models integrated into Firefly app — Adobe now relies on third-party foundation models as part of its AI delivery. This is a new supplier dependency, but it is a strategic hedge (if your own model underperforms, you route to a competitor's). The dependency is bounded and multi-source.

The one genuine supplier-adjacent risk: AI training data copyright class action [dossier: threat 2]. Adobe marketed Firefly as "trained on Adobe Stock + public domain only." A class action is pending over alleged use of pirated books for SlimLM. If this proceeds to class certification, it could: (a) constrain training data access, (b) impose licensing costs, (c) damage the "safe AI" brand differentiator. However, this is a legal/reputational risk, not a supply chain choke point.

Adobe Inc. (ADBE) — Feynman Explanation

Silicon Council Teaching Memo | Price: $239.31 | Date: 2026-04-28

1. What This Company Actually Does

Adobe builds the tools that professionals use to make things look good — and increasingly, the infrastructure that enterprises use to measure whether those things worked.

Think of Adobe in three rings.

The innermost ring is Creative Cloud: Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere, InDesign, After Effects. These are the tools that designers, photographers, filmmakers, and marketing teams use every day to create the visual material of the modern economy. 41 million people pay for these tools every month. They do not buy them once — they rent them on subscription, which means Adobe collects a recurring toll on every creative project being produced anywhere in the world.

The second ring is Document Cloud: Acrobat and PDF. When someone says "just PDF it," that is Adobe's moat announcing itself. PDF is a 30-year-old format that became the language of legal, government, financial, and business document exchange. Adobe invented it, owns the premium tooling around it, and now sells AI features on top of it — Acrobat AI Assistant helps you summarize, search, and query documents. 200 million people use Acrobat's free reader daily. A meaningful portion pay for the full suite.

The third ring is Experience Cloud: enterprise marketing software. This is Adobe competing with Salesforce and HubSpot — tools for running digital marketing campaigns, measuring customer journeys, and serving personalized content at scale. This segment ($5.9 billion of revenue, 72% gross margin) often gets ignored because it lives in the shadow of the creative franchise. Right now its AI platform, Adobe Experience Platform, is growing 30% year-over-year. That is the sleeper in this story.

Total revenue: $23.8 billion in FY2025. ARR: $25.2 billion. 96% of revenue is subscription — money that shows up every month before Adobe sells a single new seat.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is what analysts call a Clean Analytical problem. That means the standard tools work. You run a discounted cash flow, you calculate owner yield, you check ROIC, you read the 10-K — and the answer is unambiguous. The problem is not figuring out whether the business is good. It obviously is. The problem is figuring out why it is priced as though it is failing.

The answer is narrative siege. Adobe is surrounded by a story that sounds plausible: AI image generators (Midjourney, OpenAI GPT Image, Stable Diffusion) are going to make Photoshop obsolete. Figma has already taken the UI/UX design market. Canva is eating the casual-creator market. Subscription fatigue is real. A new CEO is coming. The market has heard these arguments, believed them, and marked the stock down 66% from its 2021 peak.

But here is the thing: the cash flow disagrees with the narrative. Revenue grew 11% last year. Subscription revenue grew 12%. Free cash flow is $10.32 billion. ROIC — the best single measure of whether a business is actually compounding — rose from 33.5% to 62.1% over three years. That is not a company being disrupted. That is a company accelerating.

This creates a specific type of opportunity that Munger would call a fear-discounted compounder: a genuinely exceptional business whose stock is depressed not because the business is deteriorating, but because the market is convinced the deterioration is coming. When the feared deterioration fails to materialize in the cash flows — quarter after quarter — the discount slowly collapses. The patient investor collects a 10% FCF yield while waiting.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Here is the mental model, stated plainly: when the narrative says "this company is dying" and the cash flow says "this company is thriving," trust the cash flow until it lies to you.

Narratives move fast. Cash flows move slow. Photoshop being disrupted by Midjourney is a story that can spread across Twitter in a day. Revenue declining because of that disruption takes years to show up in an annual report — and in Adobe's case, it has not shown up at all. Subscription revenue is growing 12%. That means customers who were supposed to be fleeing are still paying.

The cleanest historical analogue is Adobe's own history in 2012 to 2014. In that era, Adobe announced it was abandoning perpetual software licenses and forcing everyone onto Creative Cloud subscriptions. The reaction was fury. Designers revolted publicly. Tech journalists wrote obituaries for Adobe's business model. The stock went nowhere for two years while the transition played out.

Then the subscriptions scaled. Margins expanded. Revenue compounded. The stock went from roughly $30 in 2012 to over $600 by 2021 — a 22x return in nine years, funded entirely by the subscription model that the market had feared.

The pattern is: dominant platform encounters genuine disruption threat — absorbs it, integrates it, monetizes it — while the market prices the fear rather than the outcome. Microsoft in 2009-2014 (mobile killing Windows), Salesforce in 2014-2016 (Slack killing CRM), and now Adobe in 2023-2026 (AI killing Photoshop). In each case, the platform survived because switching costs were organizational, not just habitual. You cannot leave Photoshop the week before your ad campaign launches. The files are in PSD format. The whole team knows the tool. The muscle memory is baked in. The disruption would have to be so dramatically better that it overcomes all of that friction simultaneously — and that has not happened.

The practical application: when you see a high-quality compounder with rising cash flows trading at a fear-driven discount, the question to ask is not "what if the bears are right?" but "what evidence would the bears need to see in the numbers before I should worry?" For Adobe, that evidence would be subscription revenue decelerating below 5% for two consecutive quarters. Until then, the narrative is noise.

4. How They Make Money

Adobe's economics are almost embarrassingly good.

Creative Cloud and Document Cloud together generate 95% gross margins. That means for every dollar of subscription revenue, Adobe keeps 95 cents after the direct cost of delivery. Experience Cloud runs at 72% gross margins. Blended: 89%. For context, most industrial businesses would be thrilled with 40%.

The subscription model means revenue is visible and sticky. ARR — annualized recurring revenue — stands at $25.2 billion and is guided to grow 10% in FY2026. The company does not need to re-sell its existing customers every year. It just needs to not lose them.

FCF is $10.32 billion on $23.8 billion of revenue — a 43% free cash flow margin. After paying employees, running infrastructure, and investing $4.3 billion in R&D, Adobe still converts nearly half its revenue to cash. That cash is being used aggressively: $11.28 billion in share buybacks in FY2025 alone, reducing the share count by 6.4% in a single year. And in April 2026, the board just authorized another $25 billion of buybacks through 2030 — at a price 41% below where they were buying last year.

ROIC at 62.1% means that for every dollar Adobe has invested in its business, it generates 62 cents of return per year. That is exceptional by any standard. The S&P 500 average is roughly 12-15%. The relentless rise — from 33.5% in 2022 to 62.1% today — tells you the moat is widening, not eroding.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Three layers of protection, each reinforcing the others.

File format lock-in. PSD, AI, INDD, PDF — these are not just file types. They are the lingua franca of professional creative production. Ad agencies, publishers, film studios, law firms, and government agencies have decades of files in these formats and workflows built around them. Switching does not just mean learning new software. It means migrating your entire archive, retraining your team, and rebuilding your production pipelines. That cost is measured in years and millions, not clicks.

Photoshop and PDF as verbs. When a brand name becomes a verb — "Photoshop it," "PDF the contract" — it has achieved something extraordinary. It means the category and the company are the same thing in users' minds. There are perhaps five companies on earth with this status. Adobe has two. Verb status means the default is always your product, even when alternatives exist.

Professional workflow integration. Creative Cloud is not just a collection of apps. It is an integrated system where Photoshop files open in Illustrator, get laid out in InDesign, get cut in Premiere, get scored in Audition, and publish through Behance or Adobe Stock. Each tool in the ecosystem increases the switching cost of every other tool. And now Adobe is threading AI through the whole system — Firefly Generative Fill inside Photoshop, AI-powered recolor in Illustrator, AI summaries inside Acrobat. The new capabilities are features in existing workflows, not separate products requiring a new habit.

This is the textbook response to disruption: absorb it as a feature, keep the switching costs, and charge a premium for the upgrade.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

Shantanu Narayen has run Adobe for 18 years. He is the architect of the subscription transition, the Figma deal (blocked, but disciplined — he did not overpay once regulators pushed back), and the AI integration strategy. He owns $118 million of stock. He is leaving once a successor is named.

This is the only risk that every expert in the Silicon Council agreed on.

The base rate for new technology CEOs is troubling. A new leader, facing pressure to make their mark, often reaches for the M&A playbook. If Adobe's next CEO decides to spend $15 or $20 billion acquiring a company to accelerate growth, it would consume the capital that is currently going into buybacks at $239, absorb management attention, integrate a new culture into a precise engineering organization, and likely overpay for something the market is already valuing. That sequence — announcement, dilutive deal, integration drag, multiple compression — is how great businesses lose a decade.

The $25 billion buyback authorization announced in April 2026 is partially a tripwire: if the new CEO deploys that capital toward a large acquisition instead, it is the signal that the disciplined capital allocation playbook has ended.

The business itself does not depend on any one person. The file formats are durable. The switching costs exist independent of who is in the corner office. But the multiple the market assigns to the stock depends heavily on whether it trusts management's capital allocation judgment. A succession announcement that includes a clear commitment to buybacks and continued AI integration is bullish. One that signals M&A ambition is the reason to reduce the position.

7. The Price Tag Problem

The best way to understand the price is to start with what Adobe is worth and work backward.

Conservative DCF, growing free cash flow at 12% for ten years then slowing: $545 per share. Owner earnings method: $514 per share. Munger's Franchise Floor — 14 times owner earnings, appropriate for a franchise-quality business with 62% ROIC and 96% subscription revenue: $329 per share. Graham Floor, assuming literally zero growth forever: $179 per share.

Current price: $239.

The current price is below the absurdly-cheap floor of $329. It is below what the business is worth if it never grows again, as long as it maintains its current profitability. It is 56% below the conservative DCF. The market is pricing Adobe for negative growth while it is actually growing at 11% with expanding margins.

The FCF yield at $239 is 10.7%. The 10-year Treasury yields 4.3%. Adobe is offering 6.4 percentage points of premium over the risk-free rate, for a business that has been compounding ROIC upward for three consecutive years, has 96% subscription revenue, and has $25 billion authorized for buybacks at this price. That configuration — high yield, high quality, growing — exists only in moments of narrative-driven fear.

The path to fair value does not require anything heroic. Adobe growing at 9-10% (its current guide) for three years, while the multiple rerates from the current compressed level toward historical norms, lands the stock between $400 and $500. That is a 70-110% return from $239 over three to five years, with a 10% FCF yield as the cushion while you wait.

The best analogy is a fire sale on the best house in the neighborhood. The house is not on fire. The neighborhood is fine. Someone told a compelling story about a flood that has not arrived, and priced the property accordingly. Eventually, the flood fails to materialize, the fear fades, and the price reflects the house.

Adobe at $239 is that house.
