Global Crossing (1997–2002): The Fiber Overbuild Death Spiral

Market position: Built 100,000 miles of fiber-optic network across 27 countries. First-mover in global bandwidth infrastructure. Gary Winnick raised $20B+ and was celebrated on magazine covers as a visionary.

The numbers that match Nebius today: - Revenue: ~$3.8B (2000) vs. capex of $4B+ annually - Capex/Revenue ratio: ~1:1 — Nebius is running 7.7:1 ($4.1B capex vs. $530M revenue), which is worse - Market cap at peak: ~$47B — Nebius is at $37B pre-profitability

What killed them: The thesis was correct — bandwidth demand would explode. It did. But 97% of laid fiber went dark because every competitor had the same thesis and laid the same fiber simultaneously. When supply flooded ahead of demand, pricing collapsed 90%+ in 18 months. Global Crossing filed Chapter 11 in January 2002 with $12.4B in debt.

What management said right before collapse (2000): "We are the backbone of the new economy." "Demand is insatiable." "First-mover advantage in global infrastructure is insurmountable."

Timeline from peak to bankruptcy: 26 months.

Exodus Communications (1998–2001): The Hosting Company That Believed Demand Was Infinite

Market position: Premier managed hosting and internet data center provider. $1B+ revenue run rate. Stock hit $63 in March 2000. Ellen Hancock, CEO, was on every conference stage.

Nebius's parallel risk: if AI application layer companies over-extrapolate GPU demand, Nebius's occupancy rates collapse simultaneously with the market that created them.

What management said (Q1 2001): "We remain confident in the long-term fundamentals of internet infrastructure." They were right about the long-term. They were dead by September.

Timeline from warning signs to bankruptcy: 18 months.

Genuity / GTE Internetworking (1999–2002): Scale Didn't Save Them

2. SURVIVOR ANALOGUES — The Adapters

Amazon Web Services (2006–2014): The Canonical Survivor

Why it survived what hosting companies didn't:

  1. Vertical integration from day one. AWS did not rent someone else's infrastructure — it built proprietary hardware (Nitro chip, Graviton), proprietary networking, and proprietary abstractions that lock customers in at the software layer, not just the hardware layer.

  2. The flywheel economics. Revenue from retail subsidized infrastructure investment. AWS could price below economic cost for 7 years because Bezos had a different P&L accounting for it. Nebius has no such subsidy engine.

  3. Timing relative to market maturity. AWS launched in 2006 — before enterprise cloud was mainstream. The market grew into the infrastructure. Nebius is launching into a market where hyperscalers already have $635B committed.

  4. Proprietary moat deepened with use. Every AWS customer who stored data in S3 became progressively harder to move. Nebius's GPU compute is by definition more substitutable — a model runs on H100s regardless of whose data center they're in.

Key differentiator Nebius lacks: The software lock-in layer. AWS's moat is IAM, S3, Lambda, RDS — not the servers. Nebius's moat is the servers.

Equinix (2000–2012): The Colocation Company That Found Its Actual Moat

Why it survived when Exodus didn't:

Equinix pivoted from "we host your servers" to "we are the neutral interconnection point where every network meets every other network." The physical location became the moat — not the compute inside it.

The $2.4B market cap in 2009 grew to $70B+ by 2023 — because interconnection has genuine network effects. Adding one more customer makes the exchange more valuable to every existing customer (BGP peering, latency reduction, financial exchange co-location).

The Nebius question: Can Nebius find its Equinix pivot? Is there a product layer — a framework, a dataset marketplace, a European AI sovereignty platform — that creates genuine network effects rather than commodity compute rental?

Rackspace (2008–2016): The Managed Services Wedge

Rackspace survived the early commodity wars by going upmarket: "Fanatical Support" — managed services with guaranteed SLAs. This created switching costs that pure commodity compute providers couldn't match.

The parallel for Nebius: Managed AI infrastructure for European enterprises (GDPR-compliant, sovereign AI) could be the Rackspace wedge. The $27B Meta deal suggests Nebius is capable of strategic partnerships, but Meta doesn't need managed services — they need raw GPU throughput.

Rackspace's failure mode (2016-2020): When Microsoft Azure and AWS both offered managed services at hyperscaler scale, the wedge collapsed. Rackspace went private, tried again, re-IPO'd, then fell 95% from its 2021 peak. The lesson: managed services buys time, not survival.

THE NO TEST — Focus (Or Lack Thereof)

When I came back to Apple, I killed 70% of the product line. Not because those products were bad. Because focus is not about saying yes to the good things — it's about saying no to almost everything.

NBIS has four business units: AI cloud, autonomous driving, data labeling, education. This is not a product vision. This is a portfolio.

Volozh is essentially running Yandex-in-exile — taking whatever survived the Russia exit and dressing it in AI clothes. Avride (self-driving cars), Toloka (human labeling), TripleTen (bootcamps). These have nothing to do with each other. A GPU cloud company does not need an autonomous vehicle division. A managed inference platform has no business running a coding bootcamp.

The honest question: is Nebius Cloud the product, or is it the umbrella brand for four disconnected bets?

Apple's 4-quadrant grid — Consumer/Pro, Desktop/Portable — forced every product to answer: "Why does this exist?" NBIS cannot answer that question for three of its four units in relation to the others. That is the Open Box problem. You open the box and you don't know what this company is.

Verdict on Focus: FAIL. The cloud unit has potential focus. The conglomerate structure does not.

THE LOVE METRIC — Insanely Great or Merely Good Enough?

"Capacity fully sold out" is the closest thing we have to a love signal here, and it is a real signal. When people are lining up to give you money faster than you can take it, something is working.

The 34% cost advantage over CoreWeave matters — but I want to be careful here. Price is not love. Price is desperation. Customers who come for price leave for price. The question is: are developers choosing Nebius because the experience is insanely great, or because it's the cheapest GPU they could find this quarter?

Volozh's quote — "not adapted from general-purpose cloud, but designed for what developers actually need" — is the right instinct. It sounds like what I said about the original Mac. General-purpose computing is just a typewriter that costs more. But a Mac is designed for humans.

The problem is I don't have NPS data. I don't have churn. I don't know if Token Factory has the kind of "I can't go back" retention that iCloud created. When customers are "trapped" in your platform, that looks like love from the outside. When they genuinely love it, it looks the same — but only one of those survives commoditization.

Token Factory (managed inference) is the most interesting product here. If it truly abstracts away the horror of deploying and scaling LLMs, and developers build workflows on top of it rather than just through it, that is a sticky product. The Physical AI Data Factory is also intriguing — synthetic data pipelines for robotics and autonomous systems could be genuinely differentiated. But "intriguing" is not "insanely great."

Verdict on Love: INCOMPLETE. The sold-out signal is real. Without churn and NPS, I cannot tell if this is iPhone or iPod nano — a product people like, not a product that changes their lives.

THE INTEGRATION — Do They Own the Whole Widget?

This is where NBIS is most interesting to me.

The full-stack claim — NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs (Aether) through platform software to managed inference (Token Factory) to serverless AI — is the right architecture. I always believed that the person who controls the whole widget controls the experience. Intel controlled the chip and Microsoft controlled the OS and Dell controlled the box and the customer got a beige nightmare. We controlled everything and gave people something worth owning.

The NVIDIA partnership across the entire stack is a meaningful differentiator — today. It means they get early access to Blackwell before anyone else. It means their software is optimized for the hardware in a way that generic Kubernetes deployments cannot match. That is real.

But here is what keeps me up at night about this integration story: they don't own the chip. Apple owns the silicon. NBIS rents it from NVIDIA. When NVIDIA decides to go vertical — and they will, they are already doing it with DGX Cloud — the "integration" advantage disappears. NBIS becomes a reseller with a nice UI.

The real integration question is: do they own the developer relationship? If Token Factory becomes the place where developers build their inference pipelines, and those pipelines are tightly coupled to Nebius abstractions, then switching costs are real even without chip ownership. That is the Stripe model — Stripe doesn't own the payment network, but nobody leaves Stripe because the API is so good that switching costs are an act of self-harm.

Verdict on Integration: PROMISING BUT PRECARIOUS. The stack is real. The dependency on NVIDIA is a structural fragility. The software layer needs to become the moat before NVIDIA closes the window.

THE NEXT BIG THING — Creating the Future or Renting the Present?

Is this the iPhone moment or the dot-com server farm moment?

I think this is the most important question and I think most people are answering it wrong.

The dot-com server farms were necessary for the internet. They were not wrong investments — they were just not the right businesses to own. The value accrued to the application layer: Google, Amazon, eventually Facebook. The Exodus Communications and AboveNet server farms went bankrupt. The picks-and-shovels metaphor breaks down when the shovels get commoditized and the gold goes to whoever built the map.

NBIS is selling infrastructure for an AI moment that is real and enormous. The demand is not manufactured. But infrastructure in a commoditizing market is not a product with a soul — it is a service with a margin.

The Physical AI Data Factory is the one product that could be genuinely "next big thing" territory. Synthetic data for robotics, physical simulation, autonomous systems — if AI is going to move from software into the physical world (and it is), the bottleneck will be training data for physical environments. That is a problem nobody has solved elegantly. If NBIS solves it, that is not a server farm. That is a new kind of foundation.

Avride is interesting for the same reason — autonomous driving generates the physical world data that trains the models that improve autonomous driving. A flywheel. But Avride competing against Waymo and Tesla FSD with a ~$37B market cap parent that has three other unrelated businesses is not a bet I would take.

Verdict on Next Big Thing: PARTIAL CREDIT. Cloud infrastructure rides the wave but doesn't create it. Physical AI Data Factory could be genuine market creation. But it needs to be the center of the product story, not an asterisk.

SOUL VERDICT

NBIS has fragments of greatness and the structure of a conglomerate.

Volozh clearly has the instinct — "designed for what developers actually need" is exactly the right question to be asking. The full-stack integration play is the right architecture. The NVIDIA partnership is the right relationship. Sold-out capacity is the right problem to have.

But a company with four unrelated business units, uncertain whether it is a platform or a rental service, dependent on a chip supplier who is becoming a competitor, without visible NPS or churn data to prove the "love" story — this is not an insanely great product. This is a talented team executing well in a hot market.

Hot markets hide sins. When the market cools or the next architecture shift happens (and DeepSeek is already showing us that compute efficiency can collapse demand overnight), the companies that survive are the ones with products people love, not products people use.

The cloud business alone, stripped of the conglomerate weight and focused entirely on Token Factory as the developer platform, would be a product I could get excited about. As currently structured, this is a company that is very good at several things and great at none of them.

SOUL VERDICT: Sugared Water with a drop of something real.

The cloud unit has the soul of a product. The company has the soul of a holding company. Those are two very different investments, and right now you can only buy the holding company.
