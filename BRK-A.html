Warren Buffett on Berkshire Hathaway (BRK-A) — Self-Assessment

Analyzing BRK.A as of 2026-06-22. Data source: refined_dossier.md. I am the founder, largest shareholder, and Chairman — I will be ruthlessly objective.

1. ANTI-FRAGILE MOAT — Chaos Test

The $1B-Competitor Test

Could a well-funded competitor replicate what we've built? Ask yourself: could someone raise $1B today and build the float engine, the railroad, the regulated utility network, and 60 years of seller trust? Obviously not. But that's the wrong question. The real question is whether the economics of those assets remain defensible under chaos — recession, catastrophe, credit crunch, AI disruption.

Float engine: genuinely anti-fragile. ~$176B [CALC] in float is our most misunderstood asset. In a crisis, insureds pay premiums before claims arrive. We collect cash when others are bleeding. BHRG's combined ratio was 87.1% [SEC] in FY2025 — we are being paid to hold $176B. That is not replicable at any price in a short period. A $1B competitor in reinsurance would face adverse selection, no track record, no rating, and no relationships. They would write the worst risks at the worst prices. We cherry-pick.

However, the SEC language is precise and I wrote it myself: insurance has "virtually no barriers to entry." [SEC Item 1] What I mean is that starting an insurance company is legally simple. What is not simple is doing it profitably for six decades. The moat is not in the license; it is in the underwriting culture, the float deployment machine, and the balance sheet that lets us write catastrophe risk that others must refuse. Those take generations, not capital.

Chaos test result: In 2022, GAAP net income was −$22.76B [CALC] — a paper catastrophe that would have destroyed leveraged competitors. Our operating earnings remained positive. In Q1 2026, operating earnings hit $11.35B (+18% YoY) [SEARCH per Insurance Business/Forbes] when macro uncertainty was elevated. Anti-fragile confirmed.

Pricing Power Assessment

Honest accounting: pricing power is segmented, not uniform.

  • Float/reinsurance: Reinsurance rates continued softening at April 2026 renewals — abundant capacity, low 2025/Q1 cat losses. [SEARCH per Howden/Reinsurance News] This is genuine pricing headwind for BHRG. Social inflation and litigation funding ("nuclear verdicts") create a casualty-reserving crisis [SEARCH] that pressures the cost side even as premium rates soften. Net: reinsurance pricing power is cyclical, currently in a soft market, not structural.
  • GEICO: Rates are often the cheapest in the market — pricing power means we can raise rates without losing volume, which GEICO has demonstrated in the telematics-era recovery. Q1 2026 underwriting after-tax $1.7B vs $1.3B prior year [SEARCH]. But Progressive's telematics advantage means GEICO competes partly on price rather than on product differentiation — weaker pricing power than I'd prefer.
  • BNSF: Duopoly pricing with Union Pacific — the STB's proposed repeal of Part 1144 intramodal rules (Jan 9 2026) and UP's May 2026 switching-rate complaint vs BNSF [SEARCH] are modest headwinds. Reciprocal switching requirements would let single-served shippers route to a second carrier. This is not existential — rail still has enormous cost advantages over truck — but it nibbles.
  • BHE regulated utilities: Formula returns, not discretionary pricing power. Oregon PUC denied PacifiCorp's interim 2.8% rate hike (May 29 2026). [SEARCH per OPB] Regulators will not let us quickly socialize wildfire costs. That is the correct answer to "unlimited pricing power" — it does not exist here.

Verdict on anti-fragility: The float engine and AAA balance sheet are genuinely anti-fragile. The operating businesses (insurance underwriting, rail, utilities) have durable but not unlimited pricing power, with specific soft spots currently in reinsurance and regulated utilities.

2. STRATEGIC EVOLUTION — Feature Absorption and AI

AI as Incumbent Tool

The industry consensus in 2026 is clear: "incumbents combining InsurTech capabilities with scale and underwriting discipline OUTPERFORM pure-play disruptors." [SEARCH per LinkedIn/InsurTech 2026] This is the correct pattern. We are not being disrupted by AI in insurance; we are absorbing it. GEICO's DriveEasy now competes with Progressive's Snapshot in usage-based insurance. We were late, but we are in the game. [SEARCH]

Commoditize the complement: Our strategic pattern for 60 years has been to own the scarce assets and let others race to commoditize everything around us. Rails are scarce (you cannot build a new Class-I in America). Float is scarce (60 years of underwriting discipline to build). Regulated utility franchises are scarce (you cannot build a competing power grid in Iowa without regulatory approval). AI commoditizes underwriting analytics — a complement to insurance, not the insurance itself. We should benefit as AI makes our underwriting cheaper, not less valuable.

The $10B Alphabet AI-infrastructure placement [SEARCH] is Abel's first signal of how he thinks about this. It is a capital-markets bet on AI infrastructure, not a strategic integration. I would have done the same thing. The real question is whether BHE can use AI and new energy technology to strengthen the regulated utility moat or whether distributed solar/batteries erode it. Net-metering policies being dismantled across states in 2026 (Maryland program ends Jul 2027) [SEARCH] reduce the rooftop-solar threat — regulators are protecting our utility franchises, at least temporarily.

Combs Departure — Strategic Bench Risk

Todd Combs has departed Berkshire [SEARCH] — described as "the most significant news" in Abel's team announcement. Combs and Ted Weschler each managed ~$15B. With Combs gone, the investing bench is thinner: Abel (operating/capital allocation), Weschler (public equities), Ajit Jain (insurance, though stepping back). This is the most operationally significant personnel event of the transition. The 13F now shows 29 holdings (concentrating), and the portfolio is more dependent on fewer decision-makers. I am not alarmed — I ran everything myself for decades — but it raises key-man concentration in the post-Buffett phase.

This is not a failure of capital allocation — it is its highest expression. The cannibal instinct requires patience: buy when the stock is cheap, not because you have cash to deploy.

The $380B Cash Position

~$380B in cash and T-bills [SEARCH per FT/Morningstar] at a $1.05T market cap is ~36% of market cap sitting at Treasury yields. The operating earnings CAGR since 2018 is ~15.1% [SEARCH]. The Q1 2026 buying/selling ratio was $16B in / $24B out — net reduction of public equity exposure. Abel is building the fortress higher.

The honest assessment: this is correct strategy (preserve dry powder for dislocation) but it creates genuine drag on returns. ~$380B at 4–5% T-bill yields earns $15–19B/yr. The same capital deployed at 12–15% ROIC into an operating business earns $45–57B/yr. The opportunity cost is real and roughly $27–38B/yr in forgone earnings. That is the price of patience. Whether it is worth it depends on the quality and size of the next acquisition — which remains unknown.

Strip these out and look at operating businesses: BNSF ~30% EBT margin [SEC], GEICO CR 87.1% [SEC], BHRG generating positive underwriting margins. Those are not 5.5% ROIC businesses.

4. YELLOW PAGES TEST — Permanent Moat vs. Decay

What would a Yellow Pages obituary look like for each segment?

Float engine: Permanent. The mechanism — collect premiums before paying claims, invest the spread — is not subject to technological disruption. It is a structural feature of insurance contracts, not a product. The float grows as long as the insurance business grows. Probability of a Yellow Pages moment: essentially zero within 20 years.

BNSF: Durable but with concrete threats. - UP-NS merger: STB accepted revised application May 28 2026; supplemental info due Jul 27 2026; ruling ~2027. [SEARCH] If approved, this creates the first single-firm US transcontinental — a scaled competitor that BNSF cannot match geographically. BNSF currently serves the western half of the US; a merged UP-NS would have true coast-to-coast reach. Berkshire Hathaway: A Plain-English Guide

Based on the Silicon Council analysis, June 22, 2026 | Price: $733,610/A-share

1. What This Company Actually Does

Imagine someone bought 70-odd toll booths — a railroad, a car-insurance company, a power utility, a furniture chain, a candy business — and then kept the profits in a giant savings account earning interest instead of paying dividends. That is Berkshire Hathaway. It is not a software company or a manufacturer: it is a machine that uses insurance premiums collected today to fund permanent ownership of excellent businesses, and has done so for 60 years under Warren Buffett and now, since 2025, under his chosen successor Greg Abel.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a clean analytical problem with a capital-allocation and succession overlay.

Here is why that classification matters for valuation. Most companies have one cash stream — revenue goes in, costs come out, and you can project that forward in a spreadsheet (called a discounted cash flow, or DCF). Berkshire has roughly seven distinct cash streams: insurance underwriting, railroad freight, utility tariffs, manufacturing earnings, investment income, a $263 billion equity portfolio, and $380 billion in cash sitting in Treasury bills. Each of those behaves differently. Stacking them into a single DCF model the way you would for Apple or McDonald's produces mathematical nonsense — the analysis team's pipeline spat out a "fair value" of $193,000–$265,000 per share and then immediately flagged its own answer as structurally invalid.

The right question is not "what is the one cash flow worth?" It is "what are all the parts worth individually, and what are we paying for the bundle?" That is called sum-of-parts analysis, and it is the only honest tool for a holding company.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Sum-of-parts and price-to-book. Book value is the accountant's tally of net assets — what everything is worth on paper minus what is owed. At $733,610 per A-share, Berkshire trades at roughly 1.44 times book, meaning you are paying $1.44 for every $1.00 of underlying asset. That premium reflects the trust and skill Buffett built over six decades. A useful shorthand: book per share is about $506,000, and $380,000 of that is essentially cash and Treasury bills.

Ignore GAAP net income; watch operating earnings. An accounting rule (ASU 2016-01) forces Berkshire to mark its $263 billion stock portfolio to market every quarter. When stocks fall, GAAP net income collapses; when they rise, it balloons. Neither number tells you whether the underlying businesses are healthy. Operating earnings — what the insurance, railroad, utility, and manufacturing operations actually earn — is the honest number. In Q1 2026 those operating earnings came in at $11.35 billion, up 18% year-over-year. That is the signal.

The founder-to-operator succession pattern. History offers a useful template. When Steve Jobs handed Apple to Tim Cook in 2011, the natural fear was that Cook — an operations genius, not a visionary product designer — would let the company drift. Instead, Cook deepened every structural advantage Jobs had built: supply chain, retail, ecosystem lock-in. Apple compounded beautifully. Greg Abel is Berkshire's Tim Cook. He is an operator, not a poet. He bought $15 million of BRK stock with his own after-tax salary in his first year and pledged to repeat annually. He has not chased bad deals. The succession risk is real but the template suggests operators who internalize the founder's discipline often outperform the romanticized expectation.

4. How They Make Money

Insurance float. This is Berkshire's superpower and the hardest concept to grasp. When you pay your car-insurance premium in January, GEICO holds that money until — if ever — it pays a claim. That pool of money, currently $176 billion, is called the "float." Berkshire gets to invest those funds as if they were its own, but technically they belong to future policyholders. If Berkshire underwrites well (combined ratio below 100%, meaning premiums exceed claims), it earns investment income and a profit on underwriting. GEICO's combined ratio in 2025 was 87.1%, meaning for every $100 in premiums, only $87.10 went to claims and expenses. The float effectively gives Berkshire free leverage at a negative cost.

BNSF Railway. Burlington Northern Santa Fe is one of only two Class-I railroads serving the western United States. Moving freight by rail is four times more fuel-efficient than truck; once the tracks are laid, competitors cannot replicate them. BNSF earned $7.2 billion in pre-tax earnings in 2025 on $23.5 billion of revenue — a 30% margin that most industrial companies would trade their headquarters for.

Berkshire Hathaway Energy (BHE) and utilities. Regulated utilities earn government-approved rates of return. It is boring, predictable, and durable — with the notable exception of PacifiCorp, a western utility facing wildfire litigation discussed below.

The investment portfolio. Berkshire owns $263 billion in publicly traded stocks (Apple is the largest) and holds $380 billion in cash and Treasury bills. Those T-bills alone generate roughly $15–19 billion per year in interest income just by sitting there.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Scale is the first answer: a $176 billion float means Berkshire can absorb a catastrophic hurricane season that would bankrupt competitors, and then write more business afterward because it is still standing. The balance sheet has $380 billion in cash against only $46 billion in debt — an 8.3x coverage ratio that makes even a severe recession look manageable.

Regulated monopolies are the second answer: BNSF's tracks and BHE's transmission lines cannot be duplicated. The government sets the rules but also guarantees the returns.

The brand is the third answer, and it is underappreciated. When a family business owner in Ohio wants to sell their company and retire, they have two choices: auction it to private equity (who will fire people, load it with debt, and flip it in five years) or call Berkshire (permanent ownership, no interference, management stays). Berkshire's reputation lets it buy excellent private businesses at below-auction prices. That acquisition pipeline is a competitive advantage with no line item on any income statement.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The single most important risk is not a wildfire or a railroad competitor — it is $380 billion that earns Treasury-bill rates forever. If Greg Abel cannot find a large, intelligent place to deploy that capital — a credit crisis, a sector dislocation, a forced seller — then Berkshire's return on capital gradually drifts toward the T-bill rate, and investors holding Berkshire would have been better off in an index fund. This risk is compounded by the recent departure of Todd Combs, one of only two investment lieutenants Buffett had trained to identify large opportunities; with Combs gone, the bench for executing a $200 billion deployment event is thinner than it has ever been. The PacifiCorp wildfire litigation (an $8 billion expected liability still winding through courts through 2028) and GEICO's ongoing effort to catch Progressive's telematics technology are real concerns, but they are containable. The cash-drag scenario — the Teledyne precedent, where a brilliant conglomerate drifted for eight years after its founder stepped back — is the one risk that would not destroy Berkshire, but would quietly make it a mediocre investment for a decade.

7. The Price Tag Problem

When you buy a house, you do not just ask "what is it worth?" You ask "what am I paying versus what a fair buyer would pay in an arm's-length sale?" If comparable homes sell for $500,000 and the asking price is $725,000, you are paying a 45% premium and need a compelling reason to do so.

Berkshire today works similarly. The council's best estimate of the underlying asset value — operating businesses plus the cash plus the equity portfolio — reconstructs to roughly $1.09 trillion. The current market cap is about $1.05 trillion. You are paying essentially fair value, with a thin premium for the Berkshire brand and the optionality of that $380 billion deployment. There is no bargain here. The fresh good news is that Q1 2026 operating earnings came in at $11.35 billion, up 18% year-over-year — that reverses the narrative that Berkshire was declining without Buffett, and it is genuinely important.

The council's add-zone — the price where a margin of safety actually exists — is $556,000 to $658,000 per A-share, which corresponds to 1.1–1.3 times book value. At those levels you are paying a 10–30% premium to a fortress balance sheet that contains $380,000 per share in near-cash. Two things could get you there: a broad market correction of 15–25% that drags Berkshire down with it (without impairing the business), or market impatience with Abel's deployment pace compressing the multiple over time. The current price at $733,610 is not a price to initiate a large position. It is a price for existing owners to hold and wait.
