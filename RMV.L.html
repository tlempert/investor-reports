THE QUESTION THAT MATTERS: DID THE DOMINANT VARIABLE CHANGE?

I do not reverse a "Too Hard" judgment for sport. In April we put Rightmove on the Too Hard pile, and we did it for exactly one reason: we feared a May 2026 CMA Strategic Market Status designation under the DMCCA 2024. That was not a business risk you could price — it was a regulatory regime decision, a binary act of the state that would have repriced a 437%-ROIC franchise to utility economics overnight. When the load-bearing variable is "what will a regulator decide," fundamental analysis tools structurally cannot touch it. That is the textbook definition of TOO UNCERTAIN, and we were right to invoke it.

The forensic block now tells me that feared binary did not materialize, and not by luck. The CMA's first SMS designations went to Google Search (>90% share, £3-4bn of UK supernormal profit) and an SMS investigation into Microsoft's business software. The CMA's own 2026/27 Annual Plan names Google, Apple, and Microsoft — mobile, search, cloud. There is no SMS investigation into Rightmove, and none into UK property portals at all. The uncalculable regime variable that drove the April verdict has resolved, and it resolved favorably. That is not me hoping; that is the CMA having spent its designation ammunition elsewhere and published its priorities.

But a disciplined man does not declare victory because his old fear evaporated. A new variable has crystallized in its place: a live £1.5bn class action by UK estate agents (lead claimant Jeremy Newman) at the Competition Appeal Tribunal, alleging Rightmove abused a dominant position through "excessive and unfair" fees. The court date is set. The honest question for this entire memo is whether the new variable is the same kind of uncalculable as the old one — in which case nothing has changed and we stay on the Too Hard pile — or whether it is a different, bounded kind of risk that fundamental analysis can actually weigh. That distinction is the whole ballgame, and I will resolve it in the Decision Logic below.

Rightmove (RMV.L) — A Feynman-Style Explanation

1. What This Company Actually Does

Imagine every single house for sale or rent in Britain had to be listed in exactly one place, and that place was a website called Rightmove. Not because the government said so — just because that's where all the buyers go. And because all the buyers go there, every estate agent has to list there too. And because every agent lists there, all the buyers go there. Around and around it goes.

That is Rightmove. It is the place where Britain buys and rents property. Not one of the places — the place. Eighty-three percent market share. When a British person thinks "I want to look at houses," they do not say "I'll search for property online." They say "I'll have a look on Rightmove." It has become a verb-level brand, like Hoovering a carpet or Googling something. That linguistic gravity is not an accident. It is worth a lot of money.

The mechanics are straightforward. Estate agents — the human beings who help you buy and sell homes — pay Rightmove a subscription fee to advertise their listings. Rightmove calls this ARPA: Average Revenue Per Advertiser. In 2025, that number was £1,621 per agent per year. There are roughly 19,000 agents paying that fee. Rightmove does not own any houses, does not employ any agents, does not arrange any mortgages (though it is starting to try). It simply owns the marketplace where the whole industry has to show up.

The result is a business that generates £425 million in revenue and keeps nearly 70 pence of every pound as operating profit. For context: a typical retailer keeps 5-10 pence. A typical tech company keeps 20-30 pence. Rightmove keeps 70 pence because its "costs" are essentially electricity, servers, and the salary of a few hundred software engineers. No inventory. No trucks. No factories. Revenue fell into the company like rain.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

Problem Type: Clean Analytical — with a legal asterisk that used to be something far worse.

This is the classification that requires the most careful teaching, because Rightmove's problem type has actually changed over the past few months, and watching that shift teaches you something reusable for every investment you will ever consider.

In April 2026, Rightmove was classified as Binary/Event-Driven. The British competition regulator — the CMA — had the power to designate Rightmove as a "Strategic Market Status" firm under new 2024 legislation. Had that happened, it would have meant government-imposed rules on how Rightmove could price and operate, essentially converting a private monopoly into something closer to a regulated utility. The franchise economics — that beautiful 70% margin — would have been repriced overnight. You cannot use a spreadsheet to evaluate a regulator's mind. The dominant variable was not financial; it was political. That is textbook Binary/Event-Driven: one external decision changes everything, and you cannot calculate your way to the answer.

The CMA designated Google and investigated Microsoft. It did not touch Rightmove, and its published 2026-27 priorities make no mention of property portals. The binary resolved. The gun was put away.

But a new risk crystallized to replace it: a £1.5 billion class action lawsuit filed by estate agents, alleging that Rightmove abused its dominant position by charging "excessive and unfair" fees. A court date is now set.

Is this the same kind of problem as the CMA designation? No. And here is the crucial distinction.

A regulator's designation is a regime act — a political-administrative decision with no established precedent, no negotiated outcome, no damages range. It is Knightian uncertainty: you cannot assign a probability to it in any meaningful way.

A class action lawsuit in an established court, by contrast, is bounded litigation. It has precedents. It has a legal test (dominance alone is not illegal in UK law; you must also show abuse, which is a high bar when the agents keep paying and keep renewing their subscriptions at near-record retention rates). It has a range of outcomes you can actually estimate: dismissal, small settlement, large settlement, fee cap. Each of those outcomes has a financial consequence you can model.

The problem type shifted from "uncalculable regime risk" to "bounded, estimable legal risk layered onto a clean analytical foundation." That is a very different animal — and it is why the April verdict was "Too Uncertain" and the June verdict is "Buy."

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

The reusable framework here has two parts: how to identify when a binary resolves, and how to evaluate bounded litigation on a quality business.

Part A: Watching for resolved binaries

Binary/Event-Driven situations create artificial uncertainty that suppresses price. When the binary resolves — when the feared event does not happen — the market is slow to update. It took the market weeks to internalize that the CMA was not coming for Rightmove. Prices did not instantly reprice to fair value. This lag creates opportunity.

The discipline: maintain a watch list of companies in "Too Uncertain" status because of a specific binary, and monitor for resolution. When the binary resolves favorably, re-evaluate the fundamentals as if you are seeing the company for the first time. Do not simply assume the old verdict was wrong — verify that the fundamentals were always sound. If they were, the suppressed price is a gift.

Part B: Sizing litigation risk on a quality business

When a monopoly faces a class action, the instinct is to panic. The instinct is wrong, for a simple reason: the lawsuit exists because the moat is strong, not because the moat is collapsing. You do not sue a company for excessive pricing if you have an alternative. The agents suing Rightmove are still paying Rightmove. They are still renewing at the second-highest retention rate in a decade. They are suing because they have no choice, and a court will notice that.

The framework: ask three questions about the litigation.

  1. What is the worst-case outcome, and does it impair the existence of the business or merely constrain the rate of extraction? A fee cap is painful; a shutdown is fatal. These are categorically different.

  2. Can I bound the financial damage? For Rightmove: £1.5 billion claim, but claims in competition law routinely settle for a fraction. Even a £450 million settlement (30% of claim) is manageable against a business generating £230 million of free cash flow per year with near-zero debt.

  3. What does the legal precedent say? In UK and EU competition law, dominance itself is not illegal. You must prove abuse. Agents paying voluntarily and renewing at record rates is evidence that works for the defendant.

None of this guarantees a win. But it moves the question from "uncalculable" to "estimable." And estimable risk, priced into a monopoly at 14.9x earnings, is the kind of risk an investor is paid to bear.

4. How They Make Money

The model is a toll road. Not a metaphor — a literal economic toll road.

Every estate agent in Britain who wants buyers to see their listings must pay Rightmove a subscription. Not for each listing — a flat annual subscription, renewed whether the market is hot or cold, whether they sell a lot or a little. That subscription was £1,621 per member in 2025, up 6% from the year before. It has gone up every year for years.

There are about 19,000 agents paying. Total revenue: £425 million. Operating profit: £298 million. Free cash flow: £230 million. The company has essentially no debt and no inventory. Its fixed costs are servers and engineers. Once you have built the platform and established the audience, each marginal pound of revenue costs almost nothing to collect.

The company returns almost all of this cash to shareholders: £90 million in buybacks and £50 million in dividends in 2025 alone — over £220 million total returned that year. They cannot find enough things to spend money on, which is the highest compliment you can pay a capital-light business.
