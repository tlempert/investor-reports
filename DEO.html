  • [CALC] GAAP net income: $2.35B in FY25 — down −39% from $3.87B in FY24 and −47% from $4.45B in FY23.
  • [CALC] Operating margin: 11.6% FY25 vs 19.1% FY24 vs 21.6% FY23. In three years, operating margin was cut nearly in half.
  • [SEARCH per SEC 20-F filing] DEO's "organic" net sales metric strips out FX, acquisitions, and exceptional items. Reported operating profit FY25 declined 1.2% — approximately in line with organic. The gap is clean; this is not a "headline organic beats but GAAP bleeds" story. Credit where it is due.

The adjustment I'm watching: Exceptional charges. When a company takes repeated "exceptional" restructuring charges over multiple years, they become operating reality, not one-time items. The dossier notes real exceptional charges are present but doesn't quantify the FY25 exceptional total separately. That is a blind spot. At $24.4B net debt with a shrinking earnings base, every "exceptional" dollar is cash out the door at a premium cost of capital.

Verdict on earnings quality: Not fraudulent. But the margin collapse from 21.6% → 11.6% in 36 months is not cyclical noise — that is structural operating leverage working in reverse. High fixed costs of aged-spirits production (barrels committed years ahead) meeting flat-to-declining revenue.

Diageo (DEO): A Feynman-Style Explanation

1. What This Company Actually Does

Diageo is the world's largest premium spirits company. If you have ever seen a bottle of Johnnie Walker Scotch, ordered a Guinness at a bar, or mixed a cocktail with Don Julio tequila or Captain Morgan rum, you have touched a Diageo product. Think of the company as owning the trophy shelf at the world's bar — not every bottle, but reliably the one in the center. They sell in 180 countries, and roughly one in every three bottles of Scotch sold anywhere on earth carries one of their labels.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a cyclical/timing problem with a narrative overlay — and understanding the difference between those two things is the entire intellectual job.

Here is what that means. A cyclical problem says: the business is fundamentally healthy, but its earnings are swinging through a predictable phase. The phase will end. The question is not "will it survive" but "when does the cycle turn, and what is the right price to pay now?"

A narrative overlay is something different and more dangerous. A narrative is a story that the market believes might be permanently true even though the evidence is still thin. The narrative here is GLP-1 drugs — medications like Ozempic and Wegovy that reduce appetite and, in some studies, reduce alcohol cravings. The bear argument is: if tens of millions of people take these drugs, global alcohol volumes could fall structurally and permanently. That would not be a cycle. That would be a regime change — and regime changes require completely different analysis.

Why does the distinction matter so much? Because if this is purely cyclical, you are being handed the chance to buy a century-old franchise at a temporary trough in earnings. The brands are intact, the warehouses are full, the distribution network spans 180 markets — and the market is pricing the stock as if the bad earnings will last forever. That is where money is made.

But if the narrative is partially correct — if GLP-1 adoption turns into a structural demand shift, or if the current generation genuinely drinks less permanently rather than just differently — then the cycle does not fully reverse, and those trough earnings become the new normal. "Trough multiple on trough earnings" is not cheap at all. It is fair value for a business in long-term decline.

The evidence in the data here is ambiguous. Gen Z alcohol participation actually rose from 66% to 73% between 2023 and 2025, which cuts against the sober-curious narrative. Guinness 0.0 (non-alcoholic) is growing 40%, which means the brand is capturing the moderation trend rather than losing to it. On the other side, GLP-1 users report roughly 40% reduced alcohol cravings — and the prescription base is still small but growing. The verdict on the narrative is: probably overstated today, real and worth monitoring over a five-year window.

What that means practically is that you should analyze DEO primarily as a cyclical recovery story, while holding a small discount in the back of your head for the possibility that the narrative becomes real at scale.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Here is a reusable mental model for any cyclical business with a narrative threat:

Step 1: Separate the cyclical from the structural. Ask: is the revenue decline happening to every company in the industry, or just this one? Pernod Ricard — Diageo's main rival — also printed -3% organic revenue. Brown-Forman is struggling too. An industry-wide $22 billion aged spirits inventory overhang is documented. This is not a Diageo problem. It is a spirits industry cycle, and cycles end.

Step 2: Normalize the earnings. Never value a cyclical business at peak earnings or trough earnings. Peak earnings make it look cheap (low P/E, high ROIC). Trough earnings make it look expensive or impaired. Neither is the right anchor. Use the mid-cycle average — the earnings the business generates across a full cycle. For Diageo, mid-cycle free cash flow is roughly $3.3-3.5 billion per year, versus the current trough of $2.7 billion. That gap is significant when you multiply it by 15 turns.

Step 3: Find the cycle's timing signal. Cycles turn when inventory is burned off and real demand resumes. The specific signal to watch here is US organic revenue. North America is the largest and most important segment. Management declared destocking "largely resolved" in late 2025 — yet US volumes are still running -7%. That means the residual weakness is real consumer demand, not just warehouse cleanup. Until that number turns flat or positive for two consecutive quarters, you cannot declare the cycle over.

Step 4: Price the narrative into your discount rate, not into your base case. Do not build GLP-1 into your revenue forecast as if it is already happening. It is not happening at a scale visible in the numbers. Instead, apply a modest discount to the multiple you are willing to pay — buy the business at 14x mid-cycle earnings instead of 16x, precisely because there is a tail scenario where the narrative becomes real. This is the intellectual discipline: narrative risk adjusts your price, not your thesis.

Step 5: Identify the catalyst, then wait. Cyclical recoveries happen. But they happen on a timeline, and holding an expensive cyclical for two years while the cycle turns is not the same as getting a bargain. The right move is to know exactly what price and what news combination triggers a buy, and then wait patiently for that combination to appear. Patience here is not passivity — it is precision.

4. How They Make Money

Diageo makes money the old-fashioned way: they buy or build a famous brand, manufacture the product at scale (or age it in barrels, which costs almost nothing and takes 12-18 years to mature), and then sell it to distributors who sell it to bars, restaurants, and retailers, who sell it to you at 3-5x the production cost.

The business has a flywheel: famous brands attract premium shelf placement, premium shelf placement drives volume, volume funds marketing, marketing sustains the fame. The Scotch segment adds a layer that is almost unfair — Diageo commits to aging whisky today that will be sold in 2044. Their competitors cannot simply decide to make 18-year-old Scotch tomorrow. The inventory on the balance sheet is not a liability; it is a factory no one else has built.

Revenue is roughly flat at $20 billion a year across three years. The problem is not revenue — it is margin. Operating margin has collapsed from 21.6% in 2023 to 11.6% today. That is where the earnings pain lives: not in sales disappearing, but in the cost of carrying excess inventory through a soft-demand period.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Three layers of moat, nested inside each other.

The innermost layer is regulatory. You cannot legally call a spirit "Scotch whisky" unless it was distilled and aged in Scotland. The European Union and UK trade agreements protect this. There is no workaround — not capital, not technology, not marketing. If Diageo owns Johnnie Walker, no one else can make Johnnie Walker. This is permanent.

The middle layer is culture. Guinness is a 265-year-old brand that drinkers order by name at bars in 150 countries. Johnnie Walker's tagline "Keep Walking" is recognized across six continents. These brands have become verbs in their categories. Cultural verbs take decades to build and are nearly impossible to dissolve.

The outer layer is distribution. Diageo has relationships with the major distributors, the major retailers, and the on-trade accounts (bars and restaurants) across 180 markets. A new entrant with a great product still has to spend a decade negotiating shelf space, back-bar placement, and listing agreements that Diageo has cemented over a century. The distribution moat is not exciting to talk about, but it is the one that makes the brand moat cashable.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The most dangerous single scenario is that the US -7% volume decline, which management attributed to post-COVID destocking, turns out to be real and ongoing demand destruction. If two years from now US organic volume is still running negative — after the inventory excuse has fully expired — then the cycle thesis was wrong. That would mean a structural shift: either GLP-1 is working at scale, or Gen Z's moderation is genuine and durable, or Diageo's tequila brands (Don Julio, Casamigos) lost cultural relevance and never recovered. In that case, you are not holding a temporarily impaired franchise — you are holding a business whose earnings have permanently step-changed down. At $81 a share with $24 billion in debt, there is no margin of safety for that scenario. The math goes from "wait for recovery" to "where does this thing earn at steady state," and the answer is not pretty.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Imagine you want to buy a house you love — a grand Victorian on the best street in town, century-old bones, beautiful gardens. You know the neighborhood. You know the house. But the asking price is $820,000, and your honest analysis says the house is worth $460,000-530,000 in a normal market. The sellers point out that the house once earned $45,000 per year in rental income and will again. They are probably right about that. But today it is earning $27,000, the roof needs $25,000 of work, and there is a lawsuit about whether the garden's irrigation system was installed legally.

You love the house. You will not burn it down. But you are not paying $820,000 for a house worth $530,000 just because you like the address.

That is exactly where Diageo sits. The council's fair-value range, using mid-cycle earnings (not the trough, not the peak), is $46-53 per ADR. The stock trades at $81.88. That is 54% to 104% above what the math says the business is worth today, using reasonable and not pessimistic assumptions. The business is real. The moats are real. The Scotch aging in Scottish warehouses right now will be poured at a bar in Tokyo in 2042. None of that is in dispute.

But Warren Buffett's most important line is not "buy wonderful businesses." It is "buy wonderful businesses at fair prices." Quality and price are two separate questions, and you have to answer both correctly. At $53, Diageo is interesting. At $81, you are paying for a recovery that has not yet happened, a CEO who has not yet been hired, and a tequila franchise that is in the middle of a legal fight about whether its flagship product is actually what the label says. That is not a bargain. That is hope priced as certainty — which is the one thing disciplined investors never pay for.

The moment to act is when the price tag closes the gap: either the stock falls to the $40-53 range, or the underlying business improves enough (two quarters of US stabilization, a permanent CEO, a clean tequila lawsuit result) that the fair value rises to meet the current price. Right now, the gap is too wide. The Scotch will wait. So should you.
