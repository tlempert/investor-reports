I. MOAT TRIBUNAL RESOLUTION

Let me start where I always start: is this a good business, and is the good part durable? Zero severe flags means I do not invoke the "too hard, throw it out" reflex. So let me be honest about both what the moat is and what it isn't.

The moat here is real but it is a layered thing, and you have to weigh the layers separately. The bottom layer — the one Morningstar sneers at when it stamps "commodity industry, unfavorable moat" — is underwriting pricing power, and Buffett is exactly right that this is cyclical, not structural. It works in a hard market and evaporates in a soft one. The cycle just turned: commercial rates softening for the first time since Q3 2017, national carriers "broadening appetite," Berkshire's own E&S book down 12.4%. So I refuse to capitalize that layer as if it were permanent. Anyone who pays 2.5x book on the assumption that 20% underwriting-fed ROE is forever is making the classic error of extrapolating the peak.

But there are two layers above that which are structural. First, the A+ float engine: $30.5B invested at superior discipline throwing off a record $404M/quarter of net investment income, +12.2% YoY. That is a genuinely higher earnings floor than WRB had in prior soft cycles — Futurist's best point. Float compounds whether or not the underwriting market is hard. Second, the decentralized 60-niche underwriting culture — Anthropologist's MODERATE flag and the heart of the matter. The moat doesn't live in a logo; it lives B2B, in 60 P&L-accountable underwriters close to their niches, trusted by brokers, armed with A+ paper, socialized over 59 years to say no to underpriced risk.

That second layer is precisely where the founder's death on June 9, 2026 lands. William R. Berkley built the place and owned 21% of it. Now consider the Tribunal's own honesty: Psychologist notes Rob Berkley Jr. has run operations since 2015 and held a sub-93 combined ratio through Q1 2026 — culture not visibly degraded. Anthropologist concedes the culture "was already transitioning pre-death, may be more institutionalized than feared." Sherlock and Historian both note WRB behaved like a survivor archetype in every prior cycle. So the succession risk is a MODERATE, not SEVERE, flag — and crucially it is a slow-acting flag (calcification or talent bleed over 3-5 years), not a cliff. No ceiling cut on the moat. The Quality Floor stands — but I price the business on its book and normalized ROE, the only honest insurer lens.

1. DEAD ANALOGUES (Casualties)

Reliance Group Holdings — The Soft-Market Reserving Blowup (bankrupt 2001)

Reliance was a large commercial/specialty insurer that collapsed after aggressively pursuing premium growth into the soft market of the late 1990s. The mechanism: writing business at inadequate rates to maintain volume, then releasing reserves to prop up reported earnings while adverse development compounded silently in long-tail casualty lines (general liability, workers' comp, errors & omissions). Management maintained a confident tone well into 1999–2000, citing "disciplined underwriting" even as reserve deficiencies mounted. What killed them: (a) a gross reserve shortfall of ~$1.5B that surfaced only when state regulators forced accelerated development recognition; (b) no capital cushion to absorb the shock — Reliance had levered up via holding-company debt; (c) a liquidity crunch when reinsurers disputed recoverables.

Decline timeline: The fatal decisions were made in 1997–1999 (the soft market). External stress (Hurricane Floyd, workers' comp adverse development) began surfacing in 2000. Bankruptcy filed July 2001. From peak to zero: ~4 years from when the problem was planted to when it manifested. Management's public statements as late as Q2 2000 described "adequate reserves" and "a fortress balance sheet." [SEARCH per AM Best / Insurance Journal historical archives]

What it tells us about WRB: Reliance is the archetype of the CASUALTY trajectory. The warning signs are: (1) volume growth into a softening market, (2) favorable prior-year development masking accident-year deterioration, (3) management narrative that describes discipline while competitive pressures compromise it. WRB's dossier shows management flagging the cycle turn honestly — which is a materially different behavioral fingerprint.

HIH Insurance — Aggressive Growth, Cheerful Reserving, Total Collapse (Australia, bankrupt 2001)

HIH was Australia's second-largest insurer and one of the largest insolvencies in Australian corporate history (~A$5.3B shortfall). The parallels to what a poorly managed soft-market casualty insurer looks like: HIH expanded aggressively into U.S. general liability and workers' comp at inadequate rates; management used reinsurance arrangements that obscured the actual reserve position; favorable prior-year development was booked proactively to meet earnings targets. The CEO (Ray Williams) was publicly confident in the company's financial position months before collapse. When the reserves were forced to proper levels, the entire equity base and more was wiped.

What killed them: Under-reserving in U.S. liability lines + aggressive acquisition of FAI (another poorly-reserved insurer) + reinsurance recoverables that proved uncollectible + a governance structure that rewarded premium growth over underwriting discipline. Decline timeline: Reserve deficiencies accumulated through 1997–2000. Collapse: March 2001. From the turning point to insolvency: ~4 years. The "point of no return" came when HIH booked an unexpectedly large reserve addition in late 2000 — by that point capital remediation was impossible.

What it tells us about WRB: HIH's exact mechanism — reinsurance-obscured reserve positions and favorable development that reversed — is the tail risk flagged in WRB's forensics (S&P explicitly models "material adverse reserve development" as the downside scenario [SEARCH per S&P]). WRB's balance sheet is incomparably stronger (HIH had thin capital; WRB has 15.5x fixed-charge coverage, minimal near-term maturities [SEC]).

Late-1990s / Early-2000s Casualty Cohort — The Soft Market Graveyard

The 1997–2001 soft market destroyed Kemper (underreserved workers' comp and GL), Frontier Insurance (asbestos/environmental mass tort reserves exploded), Stirling Covent Bridge, and parts of what would later become OneBeacon's legacy liabilities. The shared fingerprint: all chased premium volume as rates declined, all reported favorable development through the soft market, and all saw reserve blowups concentrated in long-tail casualty lines where the lag between underwriting decisions and claims payment is 5–10+ years. W. R. Berkley — A Feynman-Style Explanation

For someone who has never studied WRB before

1. What This Company Actually Does

W. R. Berkley is a commercial insurance company — which means businesses pay it money today (called a "premium") in exchange for a promise to cover losses tomorrow. The brilliant trick is what happens in between: WRB collects all those premiums up front, holds them in a giant pool called float, invests the pool in bonds and stocks, and pays out claims as they arise. It is essentially a money-management firm disguised as an insurance company. As of early 2026, that pool is roughly $30 billion, generating $404 million every single quarter in investment income — even before it writes a single new dollar of business.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a cyclical/timing problem — and that label changes everything about how you analyze the company.

Here is what "cyclical" means in plain English. There are businesses where the earnings are mainly driven by how good the business is — Apple's margins are high because iPhones are hard to replicate, year after year, regardless of the macro calendar. And then there are businesses where the earnings swing up and down with an industry tide that no one controls. Insurance is the second kind.

The P&C (property and casualty) insurance industry runs on a roughly 7-to-10-year "hard/soft" cycle. In a hard market, there is not enough capacity (insurers are cautious, capital is scarce, recent catastrophes have burned people), so pricing is strong, everyone is profitable, and combined ratios — the single number that tells you how profitable underwriting is — fall below 95. In a soft market, capital floods back in, national carriers get greedy, pricing erodes, and combined ratios creep toward or above 100, meaning the underwriting operation alone loses money.

WRB is an excellent business inside that cycle. Its 60 specialized underwriting units have held an Insurance combined ratio below 93 even into 2026. But — and this is the critical point — its current return on equity of roughly 20% is peak-cycle, not mid-cycle. Commercial P&C rates began softening in Q1 2026 for the first time since 2017. Management itself described a "shift from fear to greed" and "national carriers broadening their appetite." Berkshire Hathaway's own specialty unit saw premiums fall 12.4%. The tide is turning.

Why does this matter for analysis? Because an investor who does not recognize the cycle will look at WRB's glittering 20% ROE, project it forward at 4% growth, run a DCF, and conclude the stock is a screaming buy. That investor is about to make the same mistake as someone who buys a luxury hotel chain at the peak of a travel boom and acts surprised when occupancy falls. The error is capitalizing a peak number as if it were a permanent number. With cyclicals, the one thing you can be most confident of is that peak earnings are not permanent — that is precisely what a cycle means.

The secondary problem layered on top is a succession event: WRB's founder, William R. Berkley, who built the firm over 59 years and owned 21% of it, died on June 9, 2026. His son Rob has run operations since 2015 and the combined ratio is holding, so this is not a crisis — but it removes the largest aligned owner and the culture's anchor at exactly the moment the cycle turns.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

When you encounter a cyclical business, you need a different set of tools than you use for a steady compounder. Here is a numbered framework you can apply to any cyclical — insurer, steel company, semiconductor maker, or homebuilder.

Step 1: Identify where you are in the cycle right now. Cycles have four phases: expansion (pricing rising, margins expanding), peak (margins at highs, capacity flooding back in), contraction (pricing softening, weaker players bleeding), and trough (capacity exits, pricing firms again). For P&C insurers, the leading indicators are: combined-ratio trends, whether national carriers are "broadening appetite" or pulling back, whether alternative capital (cat bonds, ILS) is near record issuance. In June 2026, all three signals point to early contraction. You are not buying at a trough.

Step 2: Normalize earnings across the full cycle — never anchor on the peak. This is the most important step and the one most investors skip. For a P&C insurer, the normalized metric is mid-cycle ROE — the return the company earns averaged across a full hard/soft cycle. For WRB, peak ROE is 20-21%; the historical mid-cycle ROE is roughly 13-15%. A good analyst builds a valuation off 13-15%, not 20%. The current 20% is real cash — it happened — but it is not the correct number to project.

Step 3: Use the right valuation multiple for the cycle phase. This is where most people compound their Step 2 error. They not only use peak earnings, they apply a high multiple to them because the business "looks great." But peak-earnings multiples are always the most dangerous multiples, because they are based on a temporary reality. For insurers, the correct metric is price-to-book vs. normalized ROE vs. cost of equity. A franchise earning 15% ROE on a 10% cost of equity deserves roughly 1.5x book. Peak-earning glory at 20% ROE might justify 2x book — temporarily. At cycle peak with the soft market beginning, you should discount to mid-cycle multiples, not pay a premium.

Step 4: For insurers specifically, watch the combined ratio and reserve releases. The P&C cycle shows up in two places: (a) the combined ratio, which tells you whether current underwriting is profitable (below 100 = good; above 100 = you are losing on underwriting and relying entirely on investment income), and (b) prior-year reserve development. In hard markets, insurers often set conservative reserves, and the excess gets released in later years as "favorable development," which flatters current earnings. When the cycle turns, those releases stop — or reverse into "adverse development," which is a nasty surprise. The reserve question is especially dangerous in long-tail casualty lines (think: bodily injury lawsuits that settle 7 years later) where social inflation and "nuclear verdicts" can make today's reserves look badly inadequate.

Step 5: Buy at the trough, not the peak. The best time to buy a cyclical is when the industry looks terrible — combined ratios are at 103, competitors are exiting, headlines say the business is broken. That is when pricing firms and the next hard market begins. The worst time is now: peak ROE, peak multiple, cycle just turned. Patient investors who understand the cycle wait for Step 1's "trough" signal before committing capital.

4. How They Make Money

WRB makes money in three stacked layers, each sitting on top of the last.

Layer 1 — Underwriting profit. A business owner pays WRB a $100,000 premium to insure its warehouse. WRB's actuaries estimate it will pay out $75,000 in average claims plus $15,000 in expenses — leaving $10,000 of underwriting profit. The combined ratio measures this: if claims + expenses = 92% of premiums collected, the combined ratio is 92. WRB's Insurance segment ran 92.2 in Q1 2026 — solidly profitable. Layer 3 — Capital compounding. When WRB earns more than it pays out in dividends and buybacks, book value per share grows. Over 59 years, William R. Berkley compounded this book value at a high-teens rate. That compounding effect is why investors pay a premium to book value for WRB at all.
