Historian of Disruption — POOL Corporation Pattern Analysis

1. DEAD ANALOGUES (The Casualties)

Yellow Pages / Dex Media (2000–2014)

Yellow Pages held near-monopoly positions in local business directories — dense, recurring relationships with SMB customers, dominant in every geography. Market share: ~80% of local advertising pre-internet. What killed them: Google/internet didn't eliminate the need for customer discovery, they eliminated the friction that gave Yellow Pages value. The information asymmetry (only they knew who the plumber was) evaporated. Decline timeline: 10–12 years from peak relevance (2000) to Chapter 11 (2013). What management said before collapse: "We are the authoritative local business resource; no one knows local business like we do." POOL parallel: POOL's information asymmetry (which product, which SKU, availability, water testing advice) is currently encoded in POOL360 and inside 6,000+ sales center staff. If a well-capitalized competitor replicates that knowledge layer digitally or through hired staff, POOL's information moat erodes. The difference: Yellow Pages' knowledge became free; POOL's knowledge is still operationally embedded.

Ingram Micro in book distribution / Borders Group adjacent (late 1990s–2008)

Borders held a distribution density moat in physical book retail — massive SKU breadth, fast replenishment, supplier relationships. When Amazon commoditized the distribution function and made SKU breadth table stakes, Borders had no differentiated value left. Revenue concentrated in the very channel being disintermediated. What killed them: not direct price competition, but the elimination of the reason customers needed a middleman. Decline: 8 years (2003–2011 bankruptcy). POOL parallel: The antitrust complaint [SEARCH per Federal Register] alleges POOL used its monopoly power to prevent manufacturers from supplying rivals since at least 2003. If the FTC order forces manufacturers to supply Heritage/Leslie's directly, POOL loses its supplier exclusivity buffer — the structural advantage that made density valuable evaporates faster.

RadioShack (2000–2015)

RadioShack dominated local electronics components distribution — the only place a hobbyist or repair technician could walk in and get an obscure capacitor. 7,000+ locations; unmatched local density. What killed them: Amazon Prime and Digi-Key made next-day delivery of components cheaper and with superior breadth. RadioShack's density became cost without value. Management response: Diversified into cell phones (not their core), tried to rebrand repeatedly, dismissed Amazon as "not serving the professional." Timeline: 15 years of managed decline. POOL parallel: The Amazon risk is documented in the dossier [SEARCH, Biologist section] — direct-to-consumer for above-ground/commodity pools already happening on TikTok/Amazon. Professional channel remains safe today because professionals need same-day, credit terms, technical support. But the Amazon threat to the long tail of commodity SKUs is real.

2. SURVIVOR ANALOGUES (The Adapters)

Fastenal (FAST) — Industrial MRO Distribution (1990s–present)

Fastenal faced identical structural threats: Amazon Business, direct manufacturer e-commerce, and big-box competitors (Home Depot/Lowe's entering industrial). Market position: ~30% gross margin, density-based moat, sticky professional customer relationships — the exact profile as POOL [SEARCH per dossier]. What they did differently: (1) Invested in on-site vending machines embedded inside customer facilities — raised switching costs to near-infinite; (2) Built a tech platform (eBuy) that integrated into customer procurement systems; (3) Continued opening locations through downturns. When they started: Systematic onsite investment began ~2008–2010, ahead of Amazon Business (2015). Investment: Hundreds of millions in vending + technology infrastructure at below-peak margins. Outcome: FAST stock grew 15x from 2009–2020; ROIC maintained above 20%. The lesson: density alone wasn't the moat — operationally embedded density was.

Sysco (SYY) — Foodservice Distribution (1970s–present)

Sysco faced existential threats from: restaurant chains building their own distribution, Amazon Fresh/restaurant supply marketplaces, and regional disruptors backed by PE. Market position: ~17% gross margin, 330+ distribution centers, ~200K SKU breadth. What they did: (1) Acquired regional competitors during downturns (consolidator in trough, not victim); (2) Built Sysco Shop e-commerce platform; (3) Invested in customized menu development that made switching costly. Timeline: Began digital investment ~2015–2017, before Amazon fully entered foodservice. Outcome: Sysco now does ~$79B revenue and has maintained 20%+ ROIC through cycles. POOL parallel: POOL is doing the Sysco playbook — Porpoise Pool & Patio +87 locations [SEARCH], commercial warehouse expansion [SEC], POOL360 platform build. The pattern matches a survivor, not a casualty.

Grainger (GWW) — Industrial Distribution (2000s–present)

Grainger faced the purest disruption test: Amazon Business directly attacked their SMB customer base with lower prices on the same SKUs. Grainger's initial response was wrong — they tried to compete on price across all SKUs. Then they restructured: (1) Accepted that commodity SKUs would migrate to Amazon; (2) Invested in Zoro (their own Amazon-like platform for SMB); (3) Doubled down on enterprise contract customers where service/credit/expertise matter. Timeline: Strategic inflection 2015–2017; painful margin compression 2014–2017; recovery 2018–present. Outcome: ROIC recovered from ~15% trough to ~40%+ by 2022. POOL parallel: POOL has not yet faced the Grainger price war because no well-capitalized e-commerce competitor has directly attacked their professional channel. Heritage Pool Supply [SEARCH per In Practise] is the first credible threat. The question is whether POOL responds like early-Grainger (deny/compete on price) or late-Grainger (segment and defend).

This is a cyclical/timing problem — and knowing that upfront changes everything about how you analyze it.

By 2026, that number had fallen to about 60,000 — a 50% collapse. Pool Corporation's earnings fell in lockstep. EPS dropped from $19-something at the 2022 peak to $10.85 in 2025.

Now here's where most investors make a mistake with cyclical companies. They see the low earnings and think the business is broken. The other 60% of POOL's revenue comes from non-discretionary maintenance: pool owners buying chlorine and chemicals and replacement parts to keep existing pools running. You can't skip maintaining your pool the way you can skip adding a new room to your house. This means the central analytical question is not "is this a good business?" — it clearly is. The question is: what is the right price to pay for a good business whose earnings are temporarily depressed? That is a timing problem, not a quality problem.
