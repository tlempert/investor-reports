Chubb Limited (CB) — How to Think About This Company

Date: 2026-04-26 | Current Price: $326.12

1. What This Company Actually Does

Chubb is a global casino that bets on disaster — and wins because it does the math better than anyone else. It collects premiums from businesses and wealthy individuals all over the world in exchange for a solemn promise: "If your factory burns down, if your shipping container sinks in the Strait of Hormuz, if a jury awards $200 million against your pharmaceutical company, we will write the check." Chubb operates in 54 countries, has $85 billion sitting in an investment portfolio earning money while it waits to pay future claims, and has spent decades developing the actuarial and underwriting judgment to charge exactly the right price for each promise it makes.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a cyclical/timing problem. That label is the most important thing you can learn before reading a single number in Chubb's financial statements, so let's unpack it carefully.

Insurance is cyclical in a way that is almost perfectly predictable once you see the mechanism. It works like this. After a bad stretch of hurricanes, wildfires, or asbestos claims, insurance companies lose a lot of money. That scares away weaker competitors. The survivors — Chubb, AIG, Zurich — look at the wreckage and raise prices because they can. Premiums surge. Combined ratios (the insurance world's efficiency measure — costs plus claims divided by premiums) fall below 90. Earnings soar. Wall Street upgrades the sector. New competitors flood in, attracted by the fat margins. Pricing softens. Combined ratios drift back toward 95, 97, 100. Earnings compress. Wall Street downgrades. Repeat.

Chubb's combined ratio was a record 86.5 in 2023 — during the hardest commercial pricing market in 20 years. By Q1 2026 it had risen to 95.7. That 9-point move is not Chubb getting worse. It is the cycle turning, exactly as it always does.

The danger — the only danger that truly matters for cyclical companies — is confusing the peak of a cycle for a permanent new reality. In 2023, an analyst who extrapolated Chubb's 86.5 combined ratio into perpetuity was making the same error as someone who projected oil prices at $140 forever in 2008. The cycle will turn. The question is where you are in it, and what the company looks like in the middle.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Here is a mental framework you can apply to every cyclical business you will ever encounter — oil companies, semiconductor manufacturers, shipping lines, steel producers, and yes, insurance companies.

Step 1: Identify the cycle driver. Every cyclical business has one or two variables that oscillate. For oil, it is the rig count and OPEC discipline. For semiconductors, it is inventory build-up in the supply chain. For insurance, it is the combined ratio and interest rates. Name the driver before you do anything else, because everything else follows from it.

Step 2: Locate yourself on the cycle map. Is this peak, trough, or mid-cycle? Chubb in 2023 was at or near peak — sub-90 combined ratio, hardest market in a generation, earnings inflated by favorable pricing. Chubb in 2026 is transitioning from peak toward mid-cycle. The market is frightened by the move from 86.5 to 95.7, treating it as structural deterioration. But 95.7 is still underwriting profit. Only above 100 do you lose money on underwriting.

Step 3: Calculate mid-cycle earnings, not peak or trough earnings. This is the hardest step and the most important. For Chubb, mid-cycle means a combined ratio of approximately 93-94 over a full pricing cycle. At that ratio, with $85 billion in float generating $7 billion annually in investment income, and with the Asian life segment producing $1.2 billion on its own, the through-cycle earning power is roughly $22-24 per share. That is the number you anchor to, not the $25.93 peak-year EPS or the $18 trough estimate your most pessimistic analyst will publish.

Step 4: Apply a multiple appropriate to the business quality, not the cycle position. Here is where most investors go wrong in both directions. They apply a high multiple at the peak (when earnings are temporarily elevated) and a low multiple at the trough (when earnings are depressed and the market is terrified). The right move is the opposite: use a consistent, quality-adjusted multiple anchored to mid-cycle earnings. For a franchise insurer with Chubb's moat, that multiple is 15-18 times.

Step 5: Never extrapolate one engine's headwind into the whole machine. This is the specific mistake the market is making with Chubb right now. The P&C underwriting cycle is softening — that is one engine. But Chubb has two others: $85 billion in float compounding at elevated interest rates, and an Asian life segment growing at double-digit rates that the market has not separately valued. When you read a bearish note on Chubb citing the deteriorating combined ratio, ask yourself: is this analyst accounting for the other two engines?

4. How They Make Money

Chubb runs three earnings engines simultaneously, and the market is currently focused almost entirely on the one that is temporarily stalling.

Engine One: Underwriting profit. Chubb collects premiums and pays out claims. In a good year, claims plus operating costs total less than the premiums collected. The gap is underwriting profit. In 2023, Chubb earned $7.3 billion in underwriting income on $53 billion in net premiums earned — an extraordinary result. In a normal year, underwriting profit is thinner, but positive. The sub-100 combined ratio is what separates real insurers from catastrophe factories.

Engine Two: The float. Here is the insight that Buffett has been teaching for 50 years. When a business buys insurance in January, Chubb collects the premium immediately. But the claims come in throughout the year — or in the case of liability policies, over the next decade. In the meantime, Chubb holds approximately $85 billion of other people's money and invests it. In 2025, this float generated $7 billion in net investment income. Think of it as a free loan of $85 billion that Chubb gets to keep as long as it writes insurance. Better still, the float has been growing for 30 years. At elevated interest rates, this engine is running hotter than it has in a decade — and it runs independently of whether premiums are rising or falling.

Engine Three: Asian life insurance. Buried inside Chubb's segment reporting is a $7.2 billion life insurance premium book, mostly in Asia, that generated $1.24 billion in income in 2025 and is growing at double-digit rates in constant currency. This business — assembled through the Cigna Asia acquisition and the 87% ownership of Huatai in China — serves the expanding Asian middle class's demand for life, health, and accident protection. Valued on Asian life insurance multiples, this segment alone might be worth $20-25 billion, roughly 16-20% of Chubb's total market cap. The market has not assigned it a separate valuation because it is hidden inside a conglomerate report.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Chubb has four distinct defenses that compound on each other.

The regulatory moat is 30 years deep. Operating as a licensed, admitted insurer in 54 countries requires decades of regulatory relationships, local capital commitments, and actuarial credibility. A startup that wanted to replicate Chubb's global footprint today could not do it in less than 30 years. No amount of venture capital can buy a 30-year track record of paying claims in a regulated industry. This is not a software moat — it is a physical, regulatory, and reputational infrastructure that cannot be copied.

The AA balance sheet is a product feature. A company buying $500 million in directors-and-officers liability coverage cannot buy it from an insurer with a B credit rating. The counterparty risk is the product. Chubb's AA financial strength rating is not just a bragging point — it is a requirement for landing Fortune 500 accounts, sovereign-adjacent programs like the U.S. government's $20 billion maritime reinsurance plan for shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, and any D&O program that a CFO will sign off on. Maintaining that rating requires capital discipline that further limits competition.

The underwriting culture is 30 years institutionalized. Evan Greenberg has run Chubb and its predecessor ACE for 30 years, repeating the same mantra in every shareholder letter: we emphasize quality of underwriting over volume of business or market share. That is not a slogan. It is why Chubb's combined ratio is 86.5 at a cycle peak while the industry median is 96-99. Cultures that produce that gap do not develop in a decade. They are built over generations of hiring, firing, training, and institutional memory.

The broker distribution moat is getting stronger, not weaker. Chubb does not sell directly to most customers. It sells through brokers — Marsh, Aon, Lockton, and thousands of independent agents. You might expect this to be a weakness (the broker owns the customer relationship), but the data tells a different story: 94% of Generation Z buyers and 89% of Millennials rely on agent or broker advice when purchasing commercial insurance, compared to only 40% of Boomers. The generation that is supposed to disintermediate everything is actually more reliant on experts for complex insurance decisions. Chubb's broker network is an asset that compounds generationally.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

There are several risks worth naming — PFAS liability exposure, China geopolitical tail risk on Huatai, California wildfire exposure — but the load-bearing risk, the one that could permanently impair the investment thesis rather than merely denting quarterly earnings, is this: Greenberg departs without a culturally-aligned successor in the middle of his AI transformation.

Greenberg is approximately 70 years old. He has no publicly announced heir. He has also just committed Chubb to eliminating 8,600 positions and automating 85% of underwriting and claims operations over the next four years. That is the right strategic move — agentic AI is producing 3-5 percentage point loss-ratio improvements for insurers that deploy it well, and Chubb needs to capture that before faster-moving startups commoditize mid-market commercial underwriting.

But transformation programs eat culture. They require decisions made under pressure, shortcuts taken in the name of speed, and a thousand daily choices about whether underwriting quality or throughput takes priority. Greenberg has spent 30 years building an organization where the answer to that choice is always quality. The person who follows him will inherit a company mid-surgery. If that successor lacks the same conviction — if they are a professional manager rather than a committed underwriter — the moat that takes 30 years to build begins to erode through a thousand small compromises, each invisible from the outside until the reserve charges arrive five years later.

This is not a reason to avoid Chubb. It is a reason to watch the succession announcement closely and to take comfort in the fact that the buy zone already prices in some multiple compression for this risk.
