UnitedHealth Group — Feynman's Business Explainer

1. What This Company Actually Does

Imagine a giant tollbooth sitting at the center of America's healthcare highway — except this tollbooth doesn't just collect tolls. It also owns some of the cars, runs some of the rest stops, fills the gas pumps, stocks the pharmacies along the road, and writes a report back to the government about how much everyone drove. That's UnitedHealth Group.

More concretely: UnitedHealth is the largest health insurance company in America, covering about 50 million people. It collects premiums from employers (who sign you up), from Medicare (for seniors), and from state Medicaid programs (for low-income Americans). When you go to the doctor, UNH pays the doctor (most of the time). When you fill a prescription, UNH's pharmacy arm — Optum Rx — negotiates the price. When a hospital files a claim, it often travels through UNH's claims-processing infrastructure (Change Healthcare).

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a Regime/Political problem dressed up as a Cyclical/Timing problem — and understanding the difference matters.

A pure "cyclical/timing" problem is a company whose earnings go up and down with a predictable cycle — like a homebuilder during interest rate cycles. 3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Here is a reusable framework for mixed-regime/cyclical problems:

Step 1: Ask which variable dominates NOW. Cyclical pressures usually resolve in 1-3 years; regime pressures usually resolve in 2-10 years. If the cyclical pressure is severe enough to break the business during the 2-10 year regime-resolution window, the regime dominates regardless. If the business can survive the cycle, the regime question determines whether normalized earnings are recoverable.

Step 2: Size the position AS IF you are wrong about the regime. Munger's rule: if regime risk is real, assume your analysis of the business is irrelevant and size the position to what you can afford to lose. A 2-3% position is fine; a 20% position is reckless.

Step 3: Look for regime-resilient value inside the company. UnitedHealth has Optum Rx (a Big Three pharmacy benefit manager), Optum Insight (a healthcare data/analytics business with 13% margins), and Optum Financial (a fintech with 26M accounts). Each of these is potentially valuable EVEN IF the core insurance regulation shifts dramatically. When you evaluate a regime-risk company, identify the pieces that survive regardless of regime outcomes.

Step 4: Demand a catalyst, not just a price. In pure cyclical problems, time heals — just buy cheap and wait. In regime problems, time can make things worse if the political environment keeps deteriorating. You want a specific event — a court ruling, a settlement, an election — that will either confirm or deny your thesis. If no catalyst exists within 2 years, lean toward "Too Hard."

Step 5: Value the business at multiple regime scenarios. Don't just model the base case. Model: (a) benign regime — apply quality multiple, (b) status quo — apply historical average multiple, (c) hostile regime — apply Graham-style low multiple with margin of safety. Your buy price should be below the WEIGHTED AVERAGE of these scenarios, not below the base case alone.

This framework applies to every regulated business: banks, utilities, insurers, defense contractors, pharma, telecoms, healthcare systems, oil in emerging markets. Master it, and you'll sidestep a decade's worth of value traps.
