Charlie Munger — Final Investment Synthesis: Admiral Group plc (ADM.L)

Date: 2026-06-21 · Price: £32.54 (3,254p) · Market cap ~£10.4B · UK motor insurer, FTSE 100

Let me start where the dossier's machine got it wrong, because it frames everything. The mechanical FCF-DCF stamps Admiral "OVERVALUED" at a fair value near £11. That number is garbage for an insurer and every serious member of this council said so independently. An insurer does not run on free cash flow — it runs on float, reserves, underwriting margin, and return on equity. You value this business on the combined ratio, on ROE against cost of equity, on book-value compounding, and on a sustainable dividend. So I throw the £11 in the bin and reason like an insurance man — which, after sixty years of owning GEICO, is the one thing I am qualified to do.

And here is the thing the council understood with unusual unanimity: this is not a quality problem. It is a timing problem. Admiral is a genuinely superb business caught at the top of its cycle, with its biggest profit contributor — prior-year reserve releases — mean-reverting in plain sight. Classify the problem correctly and the whole analysis falls into place. This is CYCLICAL / TIMING. You do not capitalize peak earnings on a cyclical. You normalize across the cycle. Peter Lynch said it in one line and he is right: "Never buy a cyclical at a low P/E." The 11x looks cheap precisely because the E is at its peak.

Warren Buffett Analysis — Admiral Group plc (ADM.L)

Insurance is my home turf. I have spent sixty years studying what separates a durable underwriting franchise from a commodity risk-taker dressed up as one. Let me apply that lens to Admiral.

Preliminary: Why the Dossier's DCF Verdict Is Noise

The mechanical DCF in this dossier spits out £11 fair value versus a £32.54 price and stamps "OVERVALUED" in red. Discard it entirely. [SOURCE: dossier forensic block, NOTE section]

For a personal-lines insurer, free cash flow as reported by yfinance — operating cash minus D&A — captures nothing of economic reality. The "float" is not debt; it is a liability the market values on underwriting quality, not on a discounted-cash-flow clock. Graham Number floor (£20.46) is similarly useless — it assumes a manufacturing balance sheet. The right valuation framework here is: ROE vs. cost of equity, price-to-book, sustainable dividend yield, and combined ratio trajectory. Everything below uses only those tools and only data from this dossier.

1. The Anti-Fragile Moat Test

The Chaos Test. Does Admiral get stronger when competitors stumble or the economy breaks?

Partially yes, and this is the most admirable thing about the business. Admiral is a direct writer with a structurally lower cost base than legacy composites. When claims inflation spiked in 2022-23 and marginal competitors bled, Admiral had the pricing analytics and data advantage to re-underwrite ahead of the market. The result: FY2025 record profit of £958m, UK Motor profit over £1bn. [SOURCE: TRANSCRIPT, UNIT_ECON — GlobeNewswire FY2025 results] That is a real cost-leadership moat expressing itself precisely when it should — during a hard market.

But the protection is not absolute. The 2025 underwriting year is, on management's own guidance, "less profitable" than 2024, and group profit is guided flat for 2026. [SOURCE: TRANSCRIPT — AMIGF earnings call highlights] This means the chaos-to-strength conversion only works in the ascending phase of the underwriting cycle. We are past the peak.

The £1B Competitor Test. Could a well-capitalized rival kill Admiral?

Not easily. Admiral's moat rests on three interlocked advantages that take years to replicate: - Proprietary pricing data from ~10 million customers across five countries [SOURCE: NARRATIVE — Yahoo Finance, Competitive Landscape] - A meritocratic low-cost culture built since 1993 under founder Henry Engelhardt and now institutionalised [SOURCE: FORENSIC INTERROGATION — management/ownership block] - Confused.com was sold (£508m disposal) [SOURCE: FORENSIC INTERROGATION — portfolio/capital], simplifying the business but removing one data-collection asset

The problem: telematics and open-data aggregators are rapidly commoditising the data advantage. [SOURCE: COMPETITOR — competitive landscape reference, "data_advantage" moat type listed alongside concerns about cost leadership] A large bank or a tech-native aggregator with £1B and patience could buy policy growth at a loss for three years and damage Admiral's customer acquisition economics. I would not say the moat is impenetrable.

Pricing Power. Can they raise prices without losing volume?

During a hard market, yes — and they proved it. UK motor customers grew 2% YoY even as premiums rose to the £460-590 band. [SOURCE: FORENSIC INTERROGATION — cycle/competition block] But the softening cycle means this test gets harder in 2026-27 as Aviva, Direct Line, and Ageas chase volume recovery.

Moat verdict: MINOR. It is real — cost leadership, brand, data — but it is not the castle-and-moat permanence I look for. It is more like a well-run factory with a good reputation. Valuable, but not inevitable.

2. The Capital Allocation Machine

ROIC. Admiral's reported ROIC is 25.1% (2025), 27.0% (2024), 19.4% (2023), 17.9% (2022). [SOURCE: FINANCIAL PHYSICS table]

For an insurer, the more informative number is ROE. The dossier notes ROE of ~58% (Simply Wall St reference). [SOURCE: NARRATIVE — "Is Admiral Group plc's (LON:ADM) ROE Of 58% Impressive?"] That is genuinely exceptional. When I see an insurer sustaining 50%+ ROE, it tells me the float is working and the underwriting discipline is real. Geico at its best ran in this zone.

However: Admiral's ROE is partly inflated by the capital-light dividend policy. They pay out 65% of post-tax profits as a normal dividend plus a special dividend on top. [SOURCE: NARRATIVE — Yahoo Finance FY2025 results, "normal dividend (65% of post-tax profits)"] This keeps book value low, which makes ROE look high. The true test is whether deployed retained capital earns its hurdle rate — and the dossier does not give us enough detail on reinvestment returns to judge cleanly.

Share count. Essentially flat: 301M shares (2025) vs. 303M (2022). [SOURCE: FORENSIC BLOCK] No meaningful buyback programme historically — but management announced a "new capital return framework including share buybacks" alongside FY2025 results. [SOURCE: TRANSCRIPT — AMIGF] This is new and worth watching, but I do not give credit for intentions.

The Cannibal score: NEUTRAL. Barely eating shares. Announced intention but no track record.

3. The Underwriting Engine — The Only Numbers That Matter

Combined Ratio. - FY2025: 80.1% [SOURCE: UNIT_ECON — Insurance Today, GlobeNewswire] - FY2024: 77.1% (implied: 3 points better than 2025) [SOURCE: TRANSCRIPT — "group combined ratio increased by 3 points compared to 2024"]

An 80% combined ratio is exceptional by any standard. Geico under Berkshire ownership averaged roughly 95-97% in a normal year. A 20-point underwriting profit margin is genuinely rare.

But there are two shadows on this number:

Shadow 1 — Reserve releases. The dossier forensics are explicit: prior-year reserve releases contributed 16.1% of premium in H1 2025, down 10.8 percentage points from the prior period. [SOURCE: FORENSIC INTERROGATION — quality of earnings block] This is the single most important data point in the entire dossier. Reserve releases are a finite, non-recurring tailwind. The years 2022-2023 were terrible for UK motor (EV repair costs, parts inflation, whiplash reform); Admiral built reserves conservatively then; those reserves are now releasing into profit. As those vintage accident-years are fully developed, the release runway shortens. Future combined ratios will look worse even if current underwriting is identical.

Shadow 2 — Ogden discount rate. The dossier notes the Ogden rate change impacted the 2025 combined ratio (3-point deterioration partly attributed to it). [SOURCE: TRANSCRIPT — highlights] A further Ogden rate cut — plausible given UK personal injury litigation trends — would require Admiral to strengthen reserves by an estimated ~£100m. [SOURCE: FORENSIC INTERROGATION — regulatory sensitivity block] That is roughly 10% of annual profit. Not catastrophic but not trivial.

Cycle position. UK motor pricing is softening after the 2023-24 hard-market spike. Aviva, Direct Line, and Ageas are competing again. Admiral's typical premium has been in the £460-590 band. [SOURCE: FORENSIC INTERROGATION — cycle/competition block] We are moving from the peak of the cycle (exceptional profitability) toward the competitive mid-cycle (normalising returns). Management guidance of flat 2026 profit is an honest acknowledgement of this. [SOURCE: TRANSCRIPT]

Reserve adequacy conclusion: The reserve position appears sound — positive development on prior years is ongoing — but the rate of positive development is clearly decelerating. This is not a reserve-deterioration story; it is a reserve-release-exhaustion story. There is a difference. The business is still solvent and well-capitalised. But earnings will look structurally lower as the tailwind fades.

4. Float Quality

This limits the investment income optionality I prize most — the ability to compound a large, long-duration float in equities. Short-tail float is more valuable than no float, but it is not the long-tail gift that life insurance or workers' comp provides.

Debt on balance sheet rose from £1.03B (2022) to £1.89B (2025). [SOURCE: FORENSIC BLOCK] This reflects growth in Admiral Money (personal lending). The lending book is a different risk profile than insurance float — it is credit risk, not underwriting risk. The doubling of Admiral Money profit [SOURCE: TRANSCRIPT] is encouraging but I would want to understand default rates in a UK rate-stress environment before getting comfortable. The dossier does not provide that.

RSA entered the 2000s with a dominant UK personal lines franchise, consistently posting combined ratios in the low-to-mid 90s. Between 2007 and 2012 it chased premium growth in motor and home, writing business at combined ratios above 100 to maintain market share, relying on investment income (which collapsed post-GFC) to cover underwriting losses. The pattern: record profits in the soft-market tailwind years, followed by a reserve strengthening cycle that wiped out three years of earnings in two reporting periods. RSA eventually sold its UK direct personal lines home and pet operations — to Admiral [Section E, Annual Report 2023] — a pointed historical irony. The signal management missed: they denied the cycle was turning, pointing to "stable claims frequency" while severity was quietly escalating. RSA's motor book went from franchise asset to disposal candidate in under a decade.

What killed it: Over-reliance on investment income to subsidise underwriting losses; no proprietary pricing edge to outrun the cycle; management optimism bias on reserve adequacy.

1B. Direct Line Group (2021–2024 value destruction)

Direct Line is the closest structural peer to Admiral — also a direct motor insurer, also UK-focused, also price-comparison-distributed. In 2021–22, Direct Line held pricing flat while claims inflation (used-car prices, labour, replacement parts) ran at 15–20% annually. Combined ratio exploded above 110. The company issued three profit warnings in 2023, suspended its dividend, and ultimately accepted a takeover approach from Ageas. Its share price fell from ~340p to below 150p — a 55% peak-to-trough destruction. This is the canonical modern example of a motor insurer failing to reprice fast enough.

What killed it: Underwriting discipline abandoned in favour of retention; failure to pass through claims inflation in premium renewals; weak telematics/data infrastructure relative to Admiral.

1C. Esure (2019–2022 growth-into-losses cycle)

Esure re-listed in 2017 promising disciplined underwriting. By 2019–21 it chased volume in a soft market. Combined ratios climbed. The company was taken private in 2022 (Pollen Street Capital buyout at distressed multiples) precisely at the moment the hard market was about to reward patient disciplined writers — i.e. Esure exited at the wrong time because it had no balance sheet resilience left.

What killed it: Marginal cost/data position relative to Admiral and Hastings; no unique pricing model; chased growth when discipline was required.

2. SURVIVOR ANALOGUES — The Adapters

2A. GEICO (1970s–2021 endurance)

GEICO is the ur-analogue for Admiral. Founded on the same insight — sell direct, eliminate broker commission, use data to price better than the market — GEICO suffered a near-death experience in 1975–76 when claims inflation from the oil shock and litigious 1970s America crushed every insurer. GEICO's combined ratio hit 116+. The company survived not because it was lucky but because it had a cost structure that was structurally below peers (operating expense ratio in the mid-teens vs. peers in the high 20s), which allowed it to absorb a hard cyclical hit and emerge with market share intact. Buffett bought in during the crisis at pennies. The lesson: GEICO at cycle troughs was always the best time to buy — and it required patience through exactly the "record profits → management guides flat → cycle turns" sequence Admiral is now living through.

What it did differently: Never chased volume to maintain growth optics; let policies lapse rather than write unprofitable risks; invested continuously in direct-response technology even when returns weren't immediate; CEO Jack Byrne accepted a temporary growth standstill.

2B. Hastings Group (2015–2019, before Admiral absorbed its market position post-Ageas)

Hastings was a technology-first insurgent that ate market share from legacy players precisely because it operated like Admiral in miniature — direct, data-driven, price-comparison-optimised. It was acquired by Ageas in 2020–21 at a full-cycle premium. The lesson for Admiral: the structural advantage of the low-cost/data model is durable, but every player in this cohort eventually faces a bid or an integration test. Admiral's size (16.5% UK market share) [Section J, GlobalData] means it is past the insurgent phase and is now the incumbent — which changes the strategic posture required.

2C. Progressive Insurance (US, 1990–present)

Progressive is Admiral's American sibling in both timeline and model. It pioneered usage-based insurance (Snapshot) in the early 2000s when telematics was expensive and adoption was low. Progressive invested in the data infrastructure years before the payoff was visible, which is why it now runs loss ratios 5–8pts below peers in its core personal auto book. The key pattern: Progressive has never optimised for a single year's combined ratio at the expense of future data collection. It gave discounts to install telematics devices, collecting data that became a competitive moat. Admiral's BlackBox/Veygo programs are the UK equivalent — early stage, low market penetration, but directionally correct.

What it did differently: Invested in telematics when returns were uncertain; used real-time claims data to reprice faster than the cycle; maintained discipline during soft markets by letting market share fall.

The named technology: ADAS-driven frequency decline, and embedded OEM insurance.

Fewer at-fault collisions means fewer claims, which in a competitive market compresses aggregate premium volume.

Admiral's response: price the residual risk more precisely. Telematics lets Admiral identify the dangerous minority within a lower-frequency population and extract premium from them. This partially offsets TAM shrinkage — but it does not fully compensate. The math: if accident frequency drops 30% across the fleet but Admiral's data edge lets it capture 5% more margin on the remaining risk, the net is still a premium pool 20-25% smaller.

Admiral Group (ADM.L) — A Great Business at the Wrong Moment

A teaching piece on peak-earnings traps, insurance cycles, and why "cheap" can be expensive

1. What Admiral Does

Imagine a very disciplined Welsh bookmaker who will only take bets when the odds are in his favour. He started in Cardiff in 1993 with a card table and a telephone, made one product — car insurance for young British drivers — and got extraordinarily good at pricing it. He is now the largest motor insurer in the UK by customer count, with 16.5% market share and nearly ten million customers across five countries.

Here is how the bookmaker's game works in insurance terms. Every year, roughly 35 million British cars need insurance by law. Drivers go to a price-comparison website (think of it as a reverse auction), and insurers bid for their business. Admiral bids using 32 years of accumulated claims data, more granular pricing models than almost anyone else on the market, and a genuinely lean cost structure that lets it offer a competitive price and still make money while rivals do not. When Admiral wins a policy, it collects the premium upfront — your £500 car insurance cheque in January — and pays claims later, sometimes years later for a serious bodily-injury case. The cash sitting between "premium collected" and "claim paid" is called the float, and it earns investment income while it waits.

That is the whole machine: collect premiums, pay claims for less than you collected, invest the float meanwhile, keep costs low enough that even a modest underwriting margin compounds beautifully. Admiral has done this for 32 years without once abandoning pricing discipline — no small feat in an industry famous for chasing volume off a cliff.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

Peter Lynch had a saying: never buy a cyclical at a low P/E.

It sounds paradoxical. Low P/E is supposed to mean "cheap." But in a cyclical business — one whose profits swing dramatically with conditions outside management's control — a low P/E at the top of the cycle is the most dangerous-looking bargain in investing. The earnings are temporarily swollen. You are dividing today's price by a number that will not repeat.

Admiral is the textbook illustration. At £32.54 per share, Admiral trades at roughly 11x reported earnings. Eleven times! For a company with a 50%+ return on equity, an 80% combined ratio, and 32 years of discipline. Sounds cheap, doesn't it?

It is not cheap. It is the low-P/E-on-peak-earnings trap, and the trap is laid perfectly here.

The classification for this analysis is CYCLICAL/TIMING. Not a quality problem. Not a management problem. Not a broken thesis. Admiral is a genuinely superb business. The problem is that 2025's record £958 million profit was manufactured partly by a tailwind — prior-year reserve releases — that is already visibly shrinking. The earnings are not durable at this level. The P/E looks low because the E is high. When the E normalizes, the P/E will look much less appealing, even at the same price.

This is the lesson. An excellent business and an excellent investment are not the same thing. Timing matters. The cycle matters. Buying the right business at the wrong point in its cycle is a mistake that takes years to recover from.

3. How to Think About It

Normalize across the cycle

An insurer's profit in any single year is only loosely related to the underlying health of the business. It is a composite of several moving parts: the premiums earned this year, the claims paid (often lagging by years), investment returns, and crucially — prior-year reserve releases.

The right way to value an insurer is to ask: what does this business earn across a full underwriting cycle? Look at Admiral's recent history: £290m net income in 2022 (trough), £340m in 2023, £660m in 2024, £740m in 2025 (peak). The honest mid-cycle number — what the business reliably earns through thick and thin — is probably around £650-700m. That is the number to capitalize. Not £740m.

On normalized earnings, Admiral trades at roughly 14-15x. That is fair value for a MINOR-moat cyclical insurer. Not cheap.

The reserve-release tell

This is the single most important signal in the entire analysis, and it is hiding in plain sight in the footnotes.

When an insurer sets aside reserves for future claims, it has to estimate what those claims will eventually cost. Insurance companies are incentionally conservative — they over-reserve. Over time, when the claims settle for less than reserved, the excess is released back through the income statement as profit. This is a reserve release.

Reserve releases are real profit. They are not fraudulent. But they are temporary and mean-reverting. Once you have released a reserve, it is gone. And the rate at which you can release depends on how conservatively you reserved in prior years.

Here is the warning light for Admiral: in the first half of 2025 alone, the benefit from prior-period releases fell from roughly 27% of premium to 16% of premium — a 10.8 percentage-point collapse in a single six-month period. That is a dramatic move. It means the tailwind that powered record profits is already fading. Management itself guided to FLAT 2026 profit and explicitly cited "the less profitable 2025 underwriting year" as the reason. When management says flat, analysts should read that as the high end of the range.

Every percentage point of combined ratio is worth roughly £50m of profit to Admiral. A 10-point normalization in reserve releases, combined with softening motor premium rates as competitors return to the market, could take £400-500m of earnings air out of the reported figure. Not permanently — the business is not broken — but for the next one to three years.

Buy insurers at the trough, not the peak — the GEICO lesson

Warren Buffett did not buy GEICO at the top. He bought it in 1976 when GEICO was functionally bankrupt — reserve deficient by hundreds of millions, its stock at $2, analysts convinced it was finished. He bought a superb, low-cost auto insurer at the absolute nadir of its existential crisis and held it forever. The return was spectacular.

The GEICO lesson is not "buy great insurers." It is "buy great insurers cheap." That means buying at the trough of the cycle, when the combined ratio is ugly, reserve releases are zero, and the dividend has just been cut. That is the moment of maximum fear and minimum price. That is when the great ones compound.

Admiral at £32.54 is the opposite of that moment. The combined ratio is 80% (historically excellent), reserve releases are enormous (if shrinking), and the dividend is at record levels. Everything looks wonderful. Which is exactly when you do not buy.

4. How They Make Money

Admiral's profit machine has four gears.

The first gear is underwriting profit — the core. Admiral writes a policy for £500, expects claims and expenses of £400 (an 80% combined ratio), and pockets the difference. An 80% combined ratio is world-class. Most insurers run 95-100%. Every percentage point is real money on a £5bn premium base, which is why every point of the combined ratio is worth about £50m.

The second gear is float income. Between collecting your premium and paying the claim, Admiral holds your money in bonds and cash. With £4bn or so of float and interest rates at current levels, this is genuinely meaningful income.

The third gear is ancillary income — the extras. Motor legal protection, breakdown cover, excess protection, multi-car discounts. These are high-margin add-ons sold to a captive customer base at the point of purchase and renewal. This is where insurers print money quietly.

The fourth gear is the low-cost model. Admiral's expense ratio is structurally below its competitors because it was born direct (no broker network) and culturally lean (10,000+ employees get free share awards rather than expensive hierarchy). Confused.com was sold; the business is now simpler and more focused than ever. Admiral Money (personal loans) doubled its profit in 2025 and is a small but growing fifth gear.

The combination of these gears means Admiral has generated a return on equity above 50% in good years. That is extraordinary for an insurer. It compounds book value fast. It pays out most of what it earns as dividends. For a long-term income investor already in the stock, it is a lovely machine to own. The question is only: at what price?

5. Why They Are Hard to Kill

Admiral has a genuine edge. It is not a toll bridge — customers can and do leave at renewal with one click on a comparison site — but it is a real, durable advantage nonetheless. Call it a minor-to-moderate moat.

The data edge is real. Thirty-two years of granular claims data across millions of policies means Admiral's pricing models are more accurate than a competitor who started ten years ago. Accurate pricing is the single most important skill in insurance. If you can price risk more precisely than your rivals, you can either undercut them (and still profit) or avoid the overpriced risks they take on (and profit while they bleed). Admiral has done both.

The combined ratio of 80% is the proof. The industry average is 95%+. That 15-point gap represents structural cost and pricing advantage, not luck. You do not accidentally run 80% for thirty years. It is discipline, data, and culture compounding.

The culture matters more than outsiders appreciate. Admiral was built by Henry Engelhardt on the idea that happy employees serve customers better than miserable ones. Every employee owns shares. The meritocracy is real. The management team Engelhardt built has outlasted him — Milena Mondini, the current CEO, runs the same playbook. Culture is hard to copy because it is not a spreadsheet; it is what people do when nobody is watching.

But — and this is important — the moat is MINOR, not a franchise. Car insurance is a commoditized, price-transparent product in a market dominated by aggregators. If a customer can get the same policy for £30 less at Aviva by pressing refresh on Confused.com, most customers press refresh. Admiral's edge buys margin, not loyalty. The stickiness is weak. That limits how much premium you should pay for the franchise — and it is the key reason the buy zone is at a conservative multiple on normalized earnings, not a lofty one.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

If you own Admiral at the wrong price and one thing goes wrong simultaneously, the stock is unpleasant for several years. That one thing is: the cycle turns harder and faster than expected, while reserve releases unwind more aggressively than the market has priced.

Here is the mechanism. The 2023-2024 UK motor insurance market was a hard market — premiums spiked 30-40% as insurers repriced post-COVID claims inflation. Admiral's reserve releases reflect those profitable underwriting years. As competitors re-enter a now-softening market (Aviva, Direct Line, Ageas are all returning to compete for volume), premiums will soften. A softer premium environment means the 2025 and 2026 underwriting years are less profitable than 2022-2024. Those future reserve releases will be smaller. The machine slows down.

The wildcard is the Ogden discount rate — a government-set rate used to calculate lump-sum payments for catastrophic bodily injury claims. A reduction in the Ogden rate increases Admiral's reserve obligations by roughly £100m in one shot. It is an unhedged regulatory option sitting on the balance sheet, and if it triggers, it accelerates the earnings step-down precisely when the soft market is already pinching.

Note what Admiral would NOT be doing wrong in this scenario. Management would not have made a mistake. The business model would not be broken. The cost edge would still be there. The data flywheel would still be spinning. This is a cycle, not a collapse. The distinction matters enormously. RSA and Direct Line — the industry cautionary tales — abandoned underwriting discipline when the market went soft and wrote unprofitable business to maintain volume. Admiral has never done that. Even in the 2022 trough, when the combined ratio looked poor, it was because inflation hit claims, not because management chased volume recklessly.

A cycle-induced earnings trough at Admiral is a buying opportunity. A discipline-abandonment is permanent impairment. They are completely different animals. Right now, the evidence points to the former — which is why the right response at the wrong price is HOLD, not SELL, for an existing holder.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Imagine you want to buy a three-bedroom house in a desirable Cardiff neighborhood. The house is excellent — solid construction, great location, well-maintained, rents reliably. A fair price for this house, based on what comparable properties earn as rentals over a full property cycle, is £280,000.

Today, the rental market is booming. The current tenant is paying 40% above the long-run average rent because the market went crazy during COVID and has not normalized yet. Based on today's rent, an estate agent shows you that this house is going for £325,000 — and points out that it is only trading at 11x the current annual rent, which looks historically cheap.

But you are a thoughtful buyer. You know the current rent is inflated. You normalize to the long-run rent. At 11-13x the sustainable rent, you would pay £280,000. At today's price of £325,000, you are paying 15-16x the sustainable rent. Not outrageously expensive — this is a good house in a good street — but definitely not a bargain, and the rental income you'll collect for the first few years will step down as the market normalizes.

That is exactly the ADM.L situation.

The buy zone — the price at which this investment earns you a genuinely good return — is £24 to £28. The lower end (£24) is the "absurdly cheap" floor: roughly 10-11x normalized earnings of £650-700m, where the sustainable base dividend of 73p represents a 6%+ yield and you are being paid handsomely to hold through the trough. The upper end (£28) is fair value: roughly 13-14x normalized earnings, a Quality Floor appropriate for a MINOR-moat cyclical business with genuine but limited pricing power. Below £28 with cash, you pull the trigger. At £24, you back up the truck.

At £32.54 today, you are paying roughly 15-16x normalized earnings — the same as paying £325,000 for a house worth £280,000 in the long run. Not a catastrophe. You are not buying garbage at a lunatic price. But you are paying a fair-to-full price for peak earnings on a cyclical, and the dividend headline (4.8%) overstates what you should bank on — strip out the special dividend and the sustainable yield is 3.5-4%, thin against UK gilts at 4.5-5%.

The conclusion follows cleanly. For a new investor with fresh cash: wait. The specific catalyst to watch is the FY2026 reserve-release rate. That is your signal. The price by then may well be in the buy zone — the analysts who own it on peak-earnings assumptions will have sold, and the stock will look "disappointing" at exactly the moment it is cheapest. That is when you buy a great insurer.

For an existing holder: the business is not broken. Selling into a cycle you can already see turning creates more risk than it eliminates — you need to be right twice (on the exit and on the re-entry). The base dividend is covered down to trough earnings. The moat is intact. Hold it, collect the income, and add more when the price falls to the zone.

The hardest part of investing in cyclicals is resisting the siren song of "record profits, cheap multiple, great management." Admiral offers all three right now. And all three are true. They are just not sufficient to make £32.54 a great price. The great price comes later, when the headlines are ugly and the instinct is to stay away. That is the whole game.

Admiral Group plc (ADM.L) · £32.54 · HOLD

1. The Napkin Pitch

Admiral is Britain's biggest car insurer. You pay them a premium every year so they cover you if you crash — and they make money by charging more than they pay out in claims, then sitting on everyone's premiums and collecting investment income while they wait. Simple as a toll booth, and they've been at it since 1993.

2. The Good News (Why We Like This Business)

The Moat — they are the cheapest factory in town. Think of insurance as a factory that converts premiums into profits. Admiral's factory runs on less fuel than everyone else's. Their "combined ratio" — the accountant's way of saying how many pence in costs they spend per £1 of premium — sits around 80p. The industry average is closer to 95p. That 15p gap is their edge: 32 years of pricing data, a lean operation out of Cardiff (not a gleaming London tower), and a culture where employees still get free shares every year. When you are the low-cost producer in a commodity industry, you win the slow boring way — and that is the only way that actually works.

The Secret Sauce — 32 years of "who crashes" data. Every time you renew your car insurance, someone at Admiral runs your details through a pricing engine built on three decades of UK driving data. They know, better than almost anyone, which 28-year-old in a certain postcode is actually a good risk. That is a real competitive advantage — the kind that takes a new entrant a generation to replicate. They have also bought Flock (a fleet telematics business) and are building out electric-vehicle data. The moat is real. It is just not a drawbridge — it is more like a really good reputation and a very big filing cabinet.

3. The Bad News (The Banana Peels)

Banana Peel #1 — Record profits were partly borrowed. Here is the part that the headline numbers hide. Car insurance is cyclical — premiums spike, then soften, rinse and repeat. In FY2025, Admiral reported a record £958 million profit. Wonderful! But a meaningful chunk of that profit came from "prior-year reserve releases" — insurance jargon for: "we set aside more money than we needed for old claims, so we get to take some back as profit." Think of it like a restaurant counting leftover ingredients from last season as this year's revenue. The release rate dropped 10.8 percentage points in a single half-year. That tailwind is fading fast, and management itself already warned that 2026 profit will be flat. This is not a scandal — it is just the cycle turning.

Banana Peel #2 — The pricing war is restarting. Aviva, Direct Line, and Ageas all got hurt in the last soft market and pulled back. They have recovered, and now they want market share back. When competitors fight on price, Admiral has to decide: hold margins and lose customers, or hold customers and lose margins. Their discipline is legendary, but discipline gets tested in soft markets.

Banana Peel #3 — The Ogden wildcard. There is an obscure UK government figure called the Ogden discount rate, which determines how much insurers must set aside for serious injury claims. If it moves the wrong way, Admiral could need to add ~£100 million to reserves overnight. Not fatal — but it is an unhedged grenade sitting in the balance sheet.

Banana Peel #4 — No founder at the wheel. Admiral was built by Henry Engelhardt, an American who ran it with unusual character from Cardiff for over 20 years. He retired in 2016, and his successor David Stevens left too. The culture he built — egalitarian, data-obsessed, employee-first — is still real. But there is no big owner sitting on 30% of the shares who would refuse to cut the dividend or sell the company at a bad moment. Professional managers with good incentives are running it now. Fine. Just not the same as a founder who built the place.

The catch is the classic cyclical trap: at £32.54, the stock looks cheap if you use the record 2025 numbers (about 11x earnings). But if you normalize those earnings — strip out the borrowed reserve releases and use what Admiral is likely to earn through a full cycle — you get somewhere around £650-700 million of true annual earning power, and suddenly the stock is not cheap at all. As Peter Lynch put it: never buy a cyclical at a low P/E. The low P/E is the trap.

The reliable, sustainable dividend — the portion Admiral will pay even in a bad year — is roughly 3.5-4%. With UK gilts yielding 4.5-5%, you are not being compensated much for the risk of owning a cyclical insurer at the peak.
