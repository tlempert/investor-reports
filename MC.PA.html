The unease is narrower and sharper: management has spent the brand's accumulated trust to fund price hikes, and Bain now reports the ultra-wealthy feel "betrayed." That is a self-inflicted wound, which is both more dangerous (it's a management choice that compounds) and more curable (management can stop) than a competitive one. No SEVERE flag means I use the normal Quality Floor (15x–18x), not the Graham 10x discount. The Historian explicitly declined the "Casualty Trajectory" — he tagged Coach as an analogue to watch, not a verdict, so the 14x ceiling cap does not trigger.

Bull-Bear Balance + Private Buyer Test

Let me put every bull case on the table, because the bears here are loud and the council leans cautious. Bezos: owner earnings flat at ~€11B across three trough years; this is a cash machine, and a 20–25% correction flips him to BUY. Buffett: genuine 170-year franchise, family aligned and buying. Lynch's point deserves a real answer, not a dismissal. He is right that the headline understates the breadth of the business. The genuine debate — stated by the dossier itself as the load-bearing question — is whether the aspirational customer's retreat is cyclical (China and macro recover, the customer returns) or structural (price hikes and logo fatigue evicted that customer permanently, the Coach script). I weight the bull case at 60% and the bear case at 40%. Private Buyer Test: no strategic acquirer could ever reassemble this portfolio — 75+ Maisons, Tiffany, Bulgari, Dior, owned ateliers and 6,280 stores. I cannot extract the synergies, I cannot wait forever indifferent to entry price, and crucially the private-market value is also impaired if the aspirational customer is structurally gone. The discrepancy is real but it does not rescue a thin margin of safety for a minority public shareholder.

1. DEAD ANALOGUES — The Casualties

Coach (2010–2014): The Price-Hike Brand Destroyer

Coach is the most precise historical analogue to LVMH's current predicament. Between 2011 and 2013, Coach aggressively raised prices and pursued volume — flooding department stores and outlets with product to hit quarterly numbers. Revenue peaked at ~$5.1B in FY2014; the stock fell roughly 65% from 2012 to 2014.

What killed them: Coach broke the scarcity covenant — the foundational promise of a luxury brand. Once a brand appears in every outlet mall, the aspirational customer (who bought it because it felt exclusive) departs. The ultra-wealthy customer, who never needed the bag for status, had never been the volume driver. Coach lost both simultaneously.

How long the decline took: ~3 years from peak pricing confidence to recognized brand-equity destruction. Management's response in 2012–2013 was to deny the shift as "temporary" and blame macroeconomics. Sound familiar? LVMH's dossier notes [SEARCH per Bain] "years of aggressive price increases have alienated customers… ultra-wealthy feeling 'betrayed.'" The language matches Coach's 2012 investor relations almost verbatim.

The management tell: Coach's CEO Lew Frankfort said in 2013 that "the aspirational customer is returning" — 12 months before the brand's worst earnings collapse.

Pierre Cardin (1970s–1990s): Death by Dilution at Scale

Pierre Cardin was once a legitimate haute couture house — a peer of Dior and Balenciaga. By the 1970s, Cardin had licensed his name to hundreds of products: jeans, cigarettes, furniture, frying pans. Revenue grew initially. By the 1990s, the Cardin name had zero luxury credibility and the house had exited serious fashion entirely.

What killed them: Over-distribution destroyed the scarcity signal. The brand became a generic descriptor, not a status marker. Every category extension reduced the margin on every other category.

Relevance to LVMH: LVMH operates 75+ Maisons — the dossier itself notes this is "the opposite of focus." The conglomerate structure creates a specific Pierre Cardin risk at the portfolio level: if LVMH needs to accelerate revenue to manage its €36.73B debt load [SEC], the temptation to license or dilute lower-tier Maisons is real. The [SEARCH per BoF] op-ed "Is it time to break up LVMH?" and the conglomerate discount narrative indicate this pressure is live, not theoretical.

Timeline to irrelevance: Pierre Cardin took roughly 20 years to fully self-destruct — slow enough that investors kept underwriting it.

Kodak (1997–2012): Monopoly Imprisoned by Its Own Cash Flow

Kodak is the archetypal "brilliant moat, slow death" story. At peak (1997), Kodak had ~80% US market share in photographic film and a ~70% global share in color film. Their engineers invented the digital camera in 1975. Kodak chose not to commercialize it because digital cannibalized their film margin. By 2012, Kodak filed for bankruptcy.

What killed them: Management optimized for the existing margin structure rather than investing ahead of the disruption curve. They saw the threat, quantified it, and buried it.

Relevance to LVMH: The Kodak analogue applies to a specific LVMH exposure: lab-grown diamonds. [SEARCH] Lab-grown diamonds are projected at $29.5B (2025) → $91.9B (2034), 13% CAGR. Tiffany and Bulgari — LVMH's Watches & Jewelry segment (€1.51B op profit, 2025 [SEC]) — are structurally exposed to this shift. Tiffany's moat rests on natural-diamond scarcity and emotional positioning. Lab-grown diamonds destroy the scarcity argument without necessarily destroying the emotional one. The question is whether LVMH will invest in defining lab-grown luxury (Tiffany blessing them) or defend natural-diamond pricing until the market decides for them. No evidence yet of a proactive pivot — this is Kodak's 1980 posture.

2. SURVIVOR ANALOGUES — The Adapters

Hermès (2009–Present): The Scarcity Doctrine

Hermès is the most instructive survivor in the exact same industry. During the 2008–2009 luxury downturn, Hermès refused volume concessions and maintained strict waitlists for Birkin bags. Revenue dipped modestly; the brand emerged with higher desirability than before because scarcity was reinforced, not compromised.

What they did differently: Hermès explicitly refused to chase aspirational volume. No licensing, no outlet exposure, no department store presence. They accepted lower short-term revenue to preserve the covenant with ultra-HNW customers.

Hermès today [SEARCH]: Trading at ~40x earnings vs LVMH's lower multiple. The market has separated them precisely because Hermès demonstrates what moat-preservation looks like structurally.

LVMH's posture vs Hermès: LVMH chose the opposite path — the dossier notes Louis Vuitton doubled some bag prices since 2019 [SEARCH per WSJ/FT]. Price hikes without scarcity reinforcement is not the Hermès strategy; it is the Coach strategy with different branding.

The pattern: Each cycle produced a full earnings recovery within 2–4 years. The structural case for LVMH as a survivor is that it HAS SURVIVED EVERY PRIOR ANALOGUE and emerged with more market share.

Teaching LVMH — A Feynman-Style Explanation

MC.PA | Price: €499.25 | Date: 2026-06-22

1. What This Company Actually Does

LVMH is the world's largest owner of luxury brand names. Imagine a single landlord who owns the most coveted addresses on Earth — not real estate, but names: Louis Vuitton, Dior, Tiffany, Bulgari, Moët & Chandon, Hennessy. People pay €1,650 for a Louis Vuitton handbag not because it holds things better than a €40 bag, but because it says something about who they are — or who they want to be. LVMH collects rent on that human desire, across 75 brands and 6,280 stores worldwide, every single day.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a Cyclical/Timing problem with a structural-demand overlay — and the two are genuinely hard to tell apart right now. That ambiguity is the entire investment problem.

Here is what I mean. LVMH's profits have declined two years in a row: net profit fell from €15.2B in 2023 down to €10.9B in 2025 — a 28% drop. The stock fell roughly 28% in Q1 2026 alone. The brand itself hasn't changed. The factories are still there. The 170-year heritage is intact. Bernard Arnault, the founder and controlling shareholder, is actually buying more shares into this decline, raising his stake to 50% of capital and 66% of votes in February 2026. So what happened?

A specific combination of events: China's luxury spending crashed after COVID reopening hype faded. A certain category of customer — call them the "aspirational luxury buyer," the person who saves up for one Louis Vuitton bag a year as a symbol of arrival — pulled back sharply. Meanwhile, LVMH had spent three years aggressively raising prices (some bags doubled since 2019), and Bain & Company now reports that even wealthy customers feel "betrayed" by the price hikes. Add in the US threatening 50% tariffs on European goods and China imposing 35% tariffs on cognac, and you have the recipe for a painful earnings squeeze.

Now here is the critical question, and it is genuinely unresolved: Is this pain temporary or permanent?

  • If cyclical: China recovers, the aspirational customer returns as the economy improves, LVMH's earnings rebound toward their 2023 peak, and the stock at €499 — down 28% from its recent high — looks like a wonderful buying opportunity at a depressed moment. This is the pattern of 2008, 2015, and 2020: every luxury cycle recovered.
  • If structural: LVMH priced out a generation of aspirational buyers permanently, logo fatigue is real, Gen Z prefers experiences or "quiet luxury" (discreet, logo-free brands), and the 2023 peak was the high-water mark. In that case, you are extrapolating peak earnings forever when the business has fundamentally changed — the Coach handbag collapse is the cautionary tale.

Why does this distinction matter so much for analysis? Because every valuation number you calculate — every DCF, every multiple — is applied to earnings. And if you do not know whether today's earnings (depressed, cyclical) or last year's earnings (peak, possibly unsustainable) are the right anchor, your valuation is built on sand. You are not really analyzing the business. You are taking a position on a macro/behavioral question: will the aspirational luxury customer come back?

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

When you encounter a cyclical business — any business whose earnings fluctuate significantly with a macro or behavioral cycle — here is the framework that keeps you from making the most common mistake (buying at the peak or panic-selling at the trough):

The Cyclical Investor's Discipline — Five Steps:

Step 1: Normalize, don't extrapolate. Never use peak earnings as your valuation base, and never use trough earnings as your base either. Instead, average earnings across a full cycle — ideally 5–7 years including at least one downturn. For LVMH, the right anchor is the "owner earnings" figure that held remarkably flat across three stress years: roughly €11B per year in 2023, 2024, and 2025. That stability is itself a signal — it means the business hasn't been structurally broken, at least not yet.

Step 2: Ask "what does recovery require?" For LVMH's thesis to work, you need China to recover and the aspirational customer to return and tariffs to not materialize. Every "and" you add to a recovery story reduces the probability it happens on your schedule. Count the "ands." In LVMH's case there are at least three, which is why a patient, disciplined investor waits for confirmation rather than paying up in advance.

Step 3: Calculate the multiple you're paying, not just the price. Cyclical stocks look expensive at the bottom (depressed earnings make the P/E ratio look high) and cheap at the top (peak earnings make the P/E look low). This is the opposite of what your instinct tells you. At €499, LVMH trades at roughly 46× its depressed 2025 earnings — which sounds absurd — but about 23× normalized €11B earnings, which sounds reasonable. Which multiple is "right" depends entirely on whether you believe earnings recover. This is why cyclical investors argue endlessly: they are arguing about which earnings figure is the correct denominator.

Step 4: Separate the structural question from the cyclical question. This is the most important and hardest step. Ask yourself: has something fundamental changed in how this business makes money, or is this simply a bad year in a predictable pattern? The warning signs of structural damage are: customer defection that does not reverse with improving macro (logo fatigue), distribution of the brand to mass channels (LVMH has resisted this), and pricing that permanently evicts a customer tier you cannot get back. LVMH has yellow flags here — the price hikes may have been too aggressive — but not yet red flags. Flat owner earnings across three tough years is the empirical defense.

Step 5: Wait for a confirming data point before buying. In a cyclical business, you rarely need to catch the exact bottom. The greatest returns come from buying early in the confirmed recovery, not in the anticipated one. For LVMH, the confirming signal would be one or two consecutive quarters of positive organic growth in Fashion & Leather Goods — the 75% of profit segment that is still declining. You give up some upside by waiting for confirmation, but you protect yourself from the scenario where the cycle does not recover as expected.

The meta-lesson: Cyclical/timing problems reward patience and punish conviction. The investor who says "this is a great business, I'll buy now" can be right about the business and still lose money because they paid peak multiples before the cycle turned. Discipline means holding cash and a watchlist, not a position.

4. How They Make Money

LVMH has five businesses under one roof, but one of them runs the show.

Fashion & Leather Goods (~75% of profits): This is Louis Vuitton and Dior. A €1,650 handbag costs perhaps €200 to make. The difference — that €1,450 gap — is the brand premium. Customers pay it for two reasons: craftsmanship (real ateliers, real leather, real artisans) and social signaling (the logo on the bag does work in the world that no other bag can do). Because LVMH controls every step from design to manufacturing to retail, they capture almost the entire margin. Operating margin on these bags is estimated around 40%.

Wines & Spirits (~6% of profits now, down from ~9%): Moët champagne and Hennessy cognac. Cognac in particular is a Chinese luxury staple — and that business has been hammered: a 35% Chinese tariff on EU brandy collapsed Wines & Spirits operating profit by 52% in two years, from €2.1B to €1.0B. A genuine cyclical/political wound.

Watches & Jewelry (~9% of profits): Tiffany, Bulgari, TAG Heuer. The fastest-growing segment right now — +7% organic in Q1 2026. Tiffany's €16B acquisition in 2021 is beginning to pay off.

Selective Retailing (~10% of profits): Sephora, primarily. Sephora is a quiet star — beauty is more resilient than handbags because the entry price is lower and the purchase is more frequent. Sephora's operating profit grew 28% over two years even as the rest of LVMH declined.

Why do customers pay so much? Because the product is not really a bag or a bottle of champagne. It is a social credential — proof of taste, success, or belonging to a certain world. The utility is psychological, not functional. That is why this moat is so durable: it is rooted in human psychology, not technology.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Think of the LVMH moat like a 170-year-old river carving a canyon. The canyon is deep precisely because it took 170 years to carve. You cannot build a competing canyon in a decade.

The brand heritage: Louis Vuitton was founded in 1854. Moët & Chandon in 1743. These are not marketing constructs — they are history. Heritage, in luxury, is a literal asset that cannot be reproduced. A new luxury brand cannot buy itself 170 years of history.

Controlled distribution: LVMH sells through its own stores — 6,280 of them. They do not sell Louis Vuitton through department stores, discount channels, or outlets. This scarcity control is the primary reason the brand retains its cachet. The moment you can buy a Louis Vuitton bag at a discount, the moat begins to erode. They have not done this.

The founder buying: Bernard Arnault, who built LVMH into a luxury empire over 40 years, currently owns 50% of the company. He raised his stake in February 2026 — buying more shares into the downturn. When the architect of the fortress is buying bricks during a storm, it is worth noting.

Scale: LVMH is so large that it has negotiating power over landlords, media companies, and suppliers that no individual luxury brand can match. It can absorb a weaker brand, renovate it, and monetize its latent heritage. That is how it acquired Tiffany, Bulgari, and Dior over the decades.

The moat is being tested, not destroyed. That is a distinction with enormous financial consequences.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

LVMH may have permanently evicted the customer it needs most.

For 40 years, the genius of Louis Vuitton was that it had two customer groups: ultra-wealthy people who bought freely, and aspirational middle-class people who saved up for one special purchase. The second group — accountants and teachers in Shanghai buying a €500 wallet as a life milestone — was never the biggest spender per customer, but it was the engine of volume growth, the cultural signal-amplifier that kept the brand visible and desirable to the ultra-wealthy. It made LVMH both a luxury brand and a mass aspiration.

Between 2019 and 2023, LVMH aggressively raised prices — some bags doubled. This made sense at the time: it pushed the brand further upmarket and generated record margins. But Bain now reports that aspirational buyers have largely stopped buying, and critically, some ultra-wealthy customers feel the brand has become "accessible" (a dirty word in luxury) and are switching to quieter, logo-free brands like Hermès, Loro Piana, and Brunello Cucinelli. LVMH is caught: if it cuts prices, it validates that the prices were wrong and trains customers to wait. If it holds prices, it may never recover the aspirational volume.

If this retreat is permanent — if LVMH has been permanently repositioned as a mass-premium brand with luxury pretensions rather than a true luxury brand — then normalized earnings are not €11B. They are something lower, and every valuation anchor in this analysis needs to be revised downward. That is the bear case, and it is non-trivial.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Here is the house-buying analogy.

Imagine you are considering buying a house in the best neighborhood in Paris — Île Saint-Louis. This house has been in the same family for 170 years. The building is magnificent. The location is irreplaceable. The family has deep community ties and would never sell the surrounding buildings to a developer.

The current asking price is €2.5 million. A careful appraisal — based on what similar (though not as good) homes nearby actually rent for — suggests fair value is around €2 million. The house has been poorly maintained for the last two years (a family argument, some deferred renovation, fewer tenants than usual), and your calculation of what it could rent for right now, today, puts it even lower — perhaps €1.7 million.

Is it a good house? Absolutely. Is €2.5 million a good price? Probably not, because you are paying a premium over fair value for a house that is currently producing less income than normal, in hopes that it returns to full income soon. If it does return, you might be okay. If the neighborhood changes — if the Seine floods three years in a row, or if the fashionable Parisians move elsewhere — you have overpaid for a house that no longer commands its historical premium.

The Munger framework puts fair value for LVMH at around €395 per share, based on normalized owner earnings of approximately €11B (the figure that held flat through three tough years), applied at a quality multiple of 15–18×. At €499.25, the stock is roughly 27% above that ceiling. You are being asked to pay tomorrow's price today, before the recovery is confirmed.

The discipline is simple: WAIT. Not because LVMH is a bad business — it is arguably one of the finest on Earth. But because Munger's whole game is refusing to pay for certainty he does not yet have. The price to own LVMH at a real margin of safety is €395 or below. That price may arrive on a China scare, a tariff headline, or a succession announcement. When it does, the founder will buy — and so should you.
