Date: 2026-04-17 | Price: $68.50 | Market Cap: ~$342B | Shares: 4,995M

It is not a business I would want to own at any price I had to calculate myself. The question in front of me is narrower — is it a business the market is now pricing so badly that even I, an old man who has spent sixty years trying not to step in things, can make money owning it? I will answer that question, but first I will do what the skill demands: I will let the Moat Tribunal convict or acquit.

Preliminary Framing: What Kind of Problem Is This?

Before I apply my usual framework, I must acknowledge what the dossier states plainly: this is a Regime/Political + Binary/Event-Driven hybrid problem — not a clean business-quality question. My framework was built for companies where competitive economics determine the outcome. Intel's outcome is partly determined by Washington. That alone narrows my margin of safety requirement.

1. THE "ANTI-FRAGILE" MOAT (Durability)

The Chaos Test: Does Intel Get Stronger When Competitors Fail?

No. The evidence points the opposite direction.

When competitors struggle, Intel does not gain — it cedes. AMD grew revenue 34% while Intel contracted 0.5% [CALC]. Nvidia built a $500B+ AI compute empire while Intel's Gaudi captured under 5% share [MEDIA]. When TSMC faces geopolitical risk, the world does not run to Intel 18A — it panics to build TSMC Arizona. Intel is not the beneficiary of competitor stress; it is the incumbent watching its walls erode.

The one partial exception: U.S. national-security logic does make Intel more necessary when TSMC concentration risk is discussed in Congress. The $10B government stake [MEDIA NPR Aug 2025] is precisely that dynamic becoming formal equity. So Intel gains quasi-utility resilience through geopolitics — but that is not anti-fragility, that is a government backstop. I do not confuse the two.

The $1B Competitor Test

Could a smart rival with $1B kill Intel? No. But TSMC did not need $1B — they needed 40 years and tens of billions, and they already won the foundry game. The question is now inverted: can Intel, with $105.4B in PPE [SEC], claw back relevance in foundry? That requires a competitor to lose — and no competitor is losing.

The Products business (x86 CPUs) has a moat that costs far more than $1B to replicate. The x86 ISA, the software ecosystem, 200+ notebook designs on Panther Lake [SEC], the BIOS/firmware stack — these are genuine switching costs. But this moat is being eroded at the edges (ARM Grace, AWS Graviton, Google Axion, Microsoft Cobalt) [MEDIA], not at the core. The PC and enterprise server base is sticky; the cloud is defecting.

Verdict on moat: The Products moat is real but shrinking. The Foundry has no moat yet — absence of a multi-billion external backlog when TSMC N2 is sold out is the clearest possible signal [MEDIA per dossier on contracted backlog].

Pricing Power: The Gross Margin Test

This is my most reliable moat indicator. I have always said a wonderful business has pricing power you can see in the margins.

  • Intel gross margin: 34.8% [CALC]
  • Texas Instruments: 57.0% [CALC]
  • Nvidia: 71% [CALC]

A 34.8% gross margin in semiconductors is not a monopoly premium — it is a competitive business with real cost pressure. TXN makes analog chips with 40-year design lives and 57% margins. Nvidia sells picks-and-shovels for the AI gold rush at 71%. Intel is selling CPUs at margins that suggest real competition, not pricing power.

The CCG segment at 28.9% operating margin [SEC] is healthier than consolidated, but not franchise-level for a technology monopoly. I compare this to See's Candies: a business with true pricing power earns returns on incremental capital well above its cost of capital. Intel's ROIC is 1.4% [CALC]. That is not pricing power — that is a business earning its cost of capital at best.

2. STRATEGIC EVOLUTION

Feature Absorption (The Borg Check): Absorbing AI or Being Replaced?

Intel is being replaced in AI, not absorbing it.

The evidence is unambiguous: - Gaudi 3/4, Crescent Island: <5% data center AI accelerator share [MEDIA per FinancialContent 2026] - Falcon Shores cancelled [MEDIA per Tom's Hardware] - Nvidia GTC 2026 CPU roadmap excludes Intel — Jensen is building ARM-based Grace CPU for NVL systems [MEDIA] - Hyperscalers (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Meta MTIA) building custom AI chips on TSMC/Broadcom [MEDIA]

Intel's answer — the AI PC with on-device NPU inference — is a defensible niche (Panther Lake, 200+ notebook designs [SEC]), but it is not where the trillion-dollar AI capex is flowing. Training compute and data center inference are lost markets. Edge AI PC is the remaining moat. It matters, but it does not compensate.

Commoditize the Complement

Intel has historically used low-cost platform components to drive CPU demand. The current equivalent would be: drive AI PC software adoption to lock in Panther Lake x86 demand. CEO Tan is attempting this (vPro 2026 overhaul, Core Ultra Series 3 NPU positioning [MEDIA]). It is a reasonable strategy. But I note that the complement (AI software) is being commoditized by open-source and cloud APIs — not by Intel. Intel does not own the software layer. Microsoft, Google, and Meta own it. Intel is the hardware hoping the software platform drives demand — a weaker position than owning the complement yourself.

3. THE CAPITAL ALLOCATION MACHINE

The Cannibal Test

The inverse of a cannibal: +22.7% share count increase over 4 years [SEC]. From 4,070M shares (2021) to 4,995M shares (2025) [SEC]. This is not capital return — this is capital consumption. Management has been issuing equity to fund a foundry build-out and strategic partnerships.

I understand the rationale: NVIDIA took 4.3% ($14.7B value) [MEDIA/SEC], SoftBank took 1.74% ($5.96B value) [MEDIA/SEC], the U.S. Government took ~10% ($10B) [MEDIA NPR Aug 2025]. These are strategic, not dilutive in the traditional sense. But they are dilutive in the economic sense — existing shareholders own less of the franchise for every share issued.

A business I want to own is buying back shares, not issuing them. Intel is doing the opposite.

ROIC Mastery Test

ROIC: 1.4% in 2025; -5.7% in 2024 [CALC]. Peer median: 16.1% [CALC].

I have one test for whether a business is a true franchise: does it earn returns well above its cost of capital? Intel's cost of equity is roughly 10–12% for a semiconductor company with this balance sheet profile. ROIC of 1.4% means Intel is destroying economic value — shareholders are better off in an index fund.

The correct response to this data is not to dismiss it because of foundry investment. Even adjusting for foundry losses: the Products segment earns $12.74B op income on a massive capital base [SEC]. The PPE alone is $105.4B [SEC]. Products on allocated capital likely earns sub-20% returns — fine for an industrial, not for a technology franchise.

The ROIC tells the story. This is not See's Candies. This is more like a steel mill with proprietary metallurgy.

4. THE YELLOW PAGES TEST (Moat Decay Pattern)

Historical Monopoly Parallels

Intel in 2026 rhymes with several historical monopolies I have studied:

Kodak: Owned the most important consumer photography platform in the world. The moat was real — chemistry, brand, distribution, professional lock-in. But the platform (film) was being replaced by a superior architecture (digital), and Kodak's own digital R&D did not save it because the economics of film were too good to abandon voluntarily. Intel owns x86, the most important PC/server ISA in the world. The platform is being challenged by ARM in mobile (long done), cloud (in progress), and now AI inference (accelerating). Intel's own AI R&D has not saved it.

IBM in 1990: A dominant mainframe/minicomputer franchise watching client-server eat its TAM. IBM survived by pivoting to services and software. Intel has no equivalent services business.

The key difference from Yellow Pages: Intel's underlying technology (x86, leading-edge silicon) is not obsolete — it is contested. Yellow Pages was replaced by something unambiguously superior (Google). x86 is being replaced by something cost-advantaged (ARM) in specific workloads, not everywhere. This makes Intel a decay story, not an extinction story.

Is the x86 Moat Built on Something Permanent?

The x86 moat rests on: 1. Software compatibility — 40+ years of compiled Windows/Linux code. This is permanent in the near term (10 years), weakening in the medium term (ARM translation layers improve). 2. OEM ecosystem — 200+ Panther Lake designs [SEC]. Sticky but not immovable. 3. Enterprise IT inertia — Fortune 500 data centers run x86 Windows Server. Extremely sticky. 4. AMD cross-license — Intel and AMD share x86 cross-licenses; neither can be eliminated without disrupting the other. A protection and a constraint.

What the moat does NOT rest on: - AI training (lost to Nvidia) - Mobile (lost to ARM/Apple Silicon in 2020) - Custom data center inference (losing to hyperscaler ASICs) - Foundry economics (TSMC N2 yields vs. Intel 18A: 145 good dies/wafer vs. 69) [MEDIA per SemiAnalysis-style report]

The x86 moat is durable for the PC/enterprise cycle; it is not durable for AI-era compute.

The Cigar Butt Calculation: At What Price Does One Last Puff Make Sense?

The Products stub is worth approximately $30/share on a sum-of-parts basis: - Products op income: $12.74B [SEC] - At 15x (fair multiple for a contested franchise with real competitive pressure): ~$191B - Less net debt ($9.8B) and corporate drag, attributable to 4,995M shares: ~$36/share

At $68.50 current price [CALC], the market is pricing in roughly $32/share for the Foundry call option ($68.50 - $36 = ~$32). That is a large bet on an unproven technology platform with no external backlog and a $10B/year loss rate.

A true cigar butt investor would want the Products franchise alone at 10–12x (distressed industrial multiple): ~$25-30/share. Below $30 (a 56% discount to today's price), there is one last puff of value in the Products franchise even if Foundry goes to zero.

At $68.50, I am being asked to pay for both a stressed franchise AND an unproven foundry. That is not my style.

5. THE VERDICT: Wonderful / Fair / Decaying?

DECAYING — but with a Protective Exception

Intel is a decaying franchise by the metrics I use to identify wonderful businesses: - ROIC 1.4% vs. 16.1% peer median [CALC] — failing the fundamental franchise test - Gross margin 34.8% vs. TXN 57%, NVDA 71% [CALC] — no pricing power premium - Share count +22.7% in 4 years [SEC] — anti-cannibal - FCF negative for 3 consecutive years [CALC] — not generating returns - AI market capture <5% [MEDIA] — losing the most important technology transition of the decade

The Protective Exception — Why I Don't Sell Short:

The U.S. Government holding 10% equity [MEDIA NPR Aug 2025] changes the calculus in one specific way: this business cannot be allowed to fail on current political logic. The national-security framing, the CHIPS Act infrastructure, the geopolitical value of a U.S.-domiciled leading-edge foundry — these create a political floor that I would not have seen in any prior semiconductor cycle.

This is not a moat. A government backstop is a subsidy, not a competitive advantage. But it does mean the bankruptcy scenario — which would clear even the Products franchise value at $30/share — is unlikely in the near term. It also means the business faces quasi-utility constraints: capital allocation will be influenced by policy, not purely by ROIC.

NVIDIA's 4.3% stake is not a validator of long-term franchise value — it is a supply chain hedge. Jensen Huang is a brilliant capital allocator who would take a stake in a struggling supplier to ensure foundry optionality and x86 access for NVL platforms. That is a rational bet at $5B for a company with $342B market cap. It does not tell me Intel is a wonderful business.

The Regime/Political Framing Forces Discipline on Sizing:

I have always said: wonderful company at a fair price beats fair company at a wonderful price. Intel is a fair-to-poor company at an expensive price (relative to franchise value). The political backstop prevents catastrophic loss but does not create wealth compounding. My margin of safety rule requires the business to be able to earn excess returns on capital without government assistance. Intel cannot.

Final Assessment:

  • PASS at $68.50 — current price embeds $32/share of foundry option value with no external backlog to support it
  • REVISIT at $25–30/share if Foundry losses persist through 2026 without 14A external commitment — at that price, the Products franchise offers one last puff
  • UPGRADE TO HOLD if 14A secures a hyperscaler anchor customer ($5B+ committed volume) by H1 2027 — that would validate the foundry call option and justify a blended valuation

The question "wonderful, fair, or decaying?" has a compound answer: The Products business is a Fair company at an expensive-for-its-quality price. The Foundry is a speculative venture with a government backstop and binary outcomes. I am not buying a lottery ticket dressed as a franchise.

Certainty compounds. Uncertainty speculates. PASS.

INTEL (INTC) — A Feynman Explanation

For the reader who has never studied this company before.

1. What This Company Actually Does

Intel designs and manufactures the "brains" inside computers — the central processing unit, or CPU, the little silicon square that does the actual thinking inside your laptop and inside most of the servers that run the internet. Think of Intel as a bakery that for forty years owned almost every oven in town: it both invented the recipe (the chip design) AND baked the bread (ran the factories, called "fabs"). Most chip companies today only do one or the other — Nvidia designs but doesn't bake; TSMC bakes but doesn't design. Intel still tries to do both, which is either its greatest strength or the anvil tied to its ankle, depending on who you ask.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a Regime/political problem fused with a Binary/event-driven problem. That is a mouthful, so let me unpack why this matters more than the usual "is the business good?" question.

A regime/political problem means the company's fate is no longer determined primarily by customers and competitors — it is determined, or at least heavily shaped, by a government. The U.S. government literally owns about 10% of Intel. It put $10 billion in, not because it liked the return on investment, but because Washington decided — on a bipartisan basis — that it cannot allow the only American company capable of making leading-edge chips at scale to go bankrupt. When a government becomes your single largest shareholder, your business stops being a pure economic engine and becomes a hybrid of a business and a public utility. That changes both the downside (you probably can't go to zero — Washington won't let you) and the upside (Washington will also pressure you to build factories in politically useful places rather than the cheapest ones).

A binary/event-driven problem means the whole story rides on a single future event that will say "yes" or "no" — with very little middle ground. For Intel, that event is: will a major outside customer — think Apple, Qualcomm, or Nvidia — commit to having Intel manufacture their chips on Intel's next-generation "14A" factory process by early 2027? If yes, Intel's foundry business becomes a real rival to Taiwan's TSMC and the stock re-rates upward dramatically. If no, Intel's $100 billion in factory equipment becomes, bluntly, the world's most expensive captive workshop — strategically useful to America, financially a black hole.

Why does naming the problem type matter? Because if you analyze Intel the way you'd analyze Coca-Cola or Costco — steady cash flows, predictable growth, clean DCF — you will get a confidently wrong answer. A standard discounted cash flow model on Intel's trailing twelve-month free cash flow says the stock is worth negative twenty dollars. That's not because DCF is broken; it's because DCF assumes the future looks like the past, and Intel is in the middle of a bet-the-company transformation subsidized by the U.S. Treasury. Standard tools break down. You need a different lens.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

When you face a hybrid regime/political + binary problem, use this five-step framework. You will use it again for any company where government policy and a single looming decision jointly dominate the outcome:

  1. Separate the protected floor from the upside option. The political backstop prevents catastrophe but does not create franchise economics. Figure out what the company is worth without the optionality. For Intel, that's the Products segment (the CPUs that go in laptops and data centers), which earns about $12.7 billion in operating income on its own and is worth roughly $30 per share. That is your floor. The foundry — the factory-for-hire business — is the option. Price them separately, never blend them.

  2. Size the position as if the binary goes against you. The single most important discipline for event-driven bets: do not risk more than you can afford to lose if the event resolves "no." Probability estimates of 30% sound reasonable but feel very different when you've written a check. If Intel's foundry fails to win a marquee customer, the stock probably drops to $35–45, and you will not enjoy the ride.

  3. Demand a bigger margin of safety than usual. In a clean analytical problem, paying fair value is fine. In a regime/binary problem, paying fair value is a mistake — you are getting no compensation for the regime risk and the binary uncertainty. Either buy well below the protected floor (so the option is free) or wait for the event to resolve and buy on confirmation (even at a higher price, because you now have a fact instead of a probability).

  4. Watch the regime variable independently from the business variable. A political shift can destroy the thesis even if the business is executing. For Intel, a repealed CHIPS Act or a dissolved government equity stake matters more than a quarterly earnings miss. Keep a separate mental ledger for regime signals.

  5. Define the tripwires upfront. Before you invest, write down: what would make me buy more? What would make me sell? For Intel, a named hyperscaler committing to 14A is a buy-more tripwire. A third foundry executive leaving in twelve months is a sell tripwire. Commit to these before you're emotionally entangled.

Applied to Intel, the framework delivers a clear verdict: the current price of $68.50 is roughly at the council's expected fair value of $58.50 — slightly above. You are paying an option premium for a coin flip that hasn't landed yet. The discipline is to wait.

4. How They Make Money

Intel makes money in two very different ways, and understanding the split is the single most important financial fact about the company.

The Products business ($49 billion in revenue, $12.7 billion in operating profit) is the cash cow. It sells CPUs — the main computing chip — to two kinds of customers. First, laptop and desktop makers like Dell, HP, and Lenovo, who stick an Intel chip inside every PC they ship. Second, data center operators — the companies running cloud computing — who buy Xeon chips by the truckload to put in servers. Customers pay because Intel's x86 instruction set is the "language" that Windows, Linux, and decades of enterprise software were written to speak. Switching away from x86 is like a city deciding to change which side of the road it drives on — technically possible, wildly disruptive. This is a real, profitable, franchise-quality business.

The Foundry business ($17.8 billion in revenue, but a $10.3 billion operating loss) is the opposite story. This is Intel renting out its factories to make other people's chip designs. Almost all that revenue today is Intel making chips for itself — only about $222 million per quarter comes from genuine outside customers. Imagine a bakery with the world's most expensive ovens running at 10% capacity while across town TSMC is running theirs hot and selling every loaf before it's baked. That's the picture. Intel is burning ten billion dollars a year keeping those ovens warm, betting that tomorrow's customers will show up.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Intel has three moats, none of which is as strong as it used to be, but all of which are still real.

First, scale and software lock-in around x86. Every Windows laptop, every business application, every database written for the last forty years assumes x86. Replacing it across the economy would take a decade. This is why Intel's Products segment still throws off real profits even as AMD and ARM chip away at share.

Second, and newest, regulatory capture. When the U.S. government owns 10% of you and has declared you a national security asset, you have something no competitor can replicate: a politically enforced "too important to fail" status. This is more like being a regulated utility than a normal business — less upside, but a very solid floor.

Third, manufacturing know-how and intellectual property. Intel is one of only three companies on Earth — with TSMC and Samsung — that can manufacture chips at the cutting edge. Its 18A process uses genuinely novel techniques (backside power delivery, gate-all-around transistors). Copying that takes a decade and tens of billions of dollars, which is why nobody else is trying.

The honest caveat: all three moats are narrowing. ARM-based chips from Amazon, Google, and Microsoft are eating into x86's server dominance. Political winds can shift. And TSMC's competing process is denser and cheaper per chip. Intel is hard to kill — but that's different from saying it's winning.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The single biggest risk is this: Intel spends another two to three years and another $20 to $30 billion trying to make the foundry business work, and still fails to sign a major outside customer. Everything else — the AI chip weakness, the dilution, the CHIPS Act politics — is secondary to this. If a big-name customer like Apple or Qualcomm or Broadcom does not commit to having Intel manufacture their chips on the 14A process by the first half of 2027, then the entire $100-billion-plus factory complex becomes what investors call "stranded capital" — assets that exist but cannot earn a competitive return. At that point Intel becomes a smaller, Products-only business weighed down by the debt and dilution it took on trying to become something else. The stock would probably drop to the mid-$30s, and the government stake, while preventing bankruptcy, would not prevent years of dead money.

Imagine you're buying a house. The house is a solid four-bedroom on a good street — worth about $300,000 on its own. Attached to it is an unfinished extension the previous owner was building into either a guest suite or a home business. If he finishes it and it works, the house is worth $500,000.
