Steve Jobs — Product Soul Verdict on Greggs (GRG.L)

They don't know what they have. Most operators would kill for what Greggs has stumbled into: a brand that Gen Z — the generation that ironically detests everything corporate — voted their #1 favourite UK brand [SEARCH per Pion]. That is not a marketing achievement. That is soul. And soul is the only thing you cannot buy, copy, or commodity-compete away.

Let me run them through the framework.

1. THE "NO" TEST — Is the product line simple?

PASS. Emphatically.

Look at the 4-quadrant grid I drew at Apple in 1997: consumer/pro, desktop/portable. Four machines. Kill everything else. Now look at Greggs:

  • Hero SKU: Sausage roll. One product. Iconic. Ubiquitous.
  • Adjacent core: Steak bake, vegan sausage roll, bacon roll, sandwiches, sweet treats.
  • Format: One format — the grab-and-go bakery counter — rolled out across 2,739 shops [SEC]. Not 50 store concepts. Not a dozen fast-casual pivots. The same widget, replicated with discipline.
  • No R&D bloat: "R&D: Effectively zero (operational innovation via recipe/shop format)" [SEC]. Good. They're not faking innovation theatre. The product is the product.
  • Channels: Walk-in (~95%), Iceland branded frozen (~2%), franchise (~3%). Extension through partners, not through internal kingdom-building.

This is the anti-Sculley company. They have not tried to be 50 mediocre things. They sell hot pastry, cheaply, beautifully executed. When you can describe a company in six words, the product has focus. Greggs passes the No test.

The only smell of drift: 120 net openings/yr targeting >3,000 UK shops [SEC]. That is unit rollout, not product drift. Rollout is a different discipline — and I'll flag a concern about late-cycle rollout below.

2. THE LOVE METRIC — Do people love it, or are they trapped?

LOVE. And it's measurable.

Consumer retail doesn't have NRR or enterprise churn. But the equivalents here are extraordinary:

  • PION100 UK report: Greggs = Gen Z's #1 favourite UK brand [SEARCH per Pion]. Not top 10. Not food category. #1 UK brand, full stop, from the generation that will be earning for the next 50 years.
  • YouGov Brand Index Dec 2025: UK's #1 food-to-go brand [SEARCH per Greggs prelim].
  • 350k TikTok followers on the M&S-Greggs joint account [SEARCH per Startups.co.uk] — Gen Z is making the content for them, which is the only marketing that matters anymore.
  • Rewards App scanned in 26.7% of company-managed transactions, 1.7M downloads in 2025 [MEDIA]. One in four transactions is a voluntarily identified customer. That is the retention layer, quantified.
  • "Popping to Greggs" — colloquial UK usage [MEDIA]. The brand is a verb. Apple went decades without becoming a verb. Google did. Xerox did. Greggs did.
  • Market share +0.5pp in FY25 in a declining FTG market [MEDIA per Circana] — customers actively chose them while the sector shrank.

This is love, not lock-in. They have no contracts. No switching costs. No captive network effects. Every sale is an opt-in vote. And volumes held — early 2026 LFL +1.6% despite ~4% pricing = implied volume -2.4% [MEDIA]. Yes, that shows elasticity is appearing. But a 2.4% volume dip after a 4% price increase means the love is real enough to absorb price. That is the definition of pricing power from brand strength, not from lock-in.

Compare: Pret had to close 30 shops and cut 33% of staff in the prior restructuring cycle [MEDIA], and is now trialling meal deals to defend against Greggs. Pret is the premium brand reacting. Greggs is the one being chased.

Love: confirmed. Not sugared water. Sugared pastry, consciously chosen.

3. THE INTEGRATION — Do they own the whole widget?

Landlord. Not tenant.

This is the part analysts miss because Greggs doesn't look like a tech company. But the vertical stack is complete:

  • Recipe / IP: Proprietary. The sausage roll is theirs. The vegan sausage roll is theirs.
  • Manufacturing: Owned bakeries. New Derby site coming H2 2026 [MEDIA] adds ~20% capacity headroom. They make the food.
  • Distribution: Own logistics into own shops.
  • Retail: 2,739 owned/franchised shops with unified brand [SEC].
  • Digital/data layer: Rewards App with 1.7M downloads, 26.7% transaction coverage [MEDIA]. CEO citation: "price increase in May did not impact customer behaviour, as evidenced by app data" [MEDIA]. That is the Apple move — use first-party data to make confident pricing decisions that tenants cannot make.
  • Channel extension: Iceland partnership extends the brand into home freezers without ceding control of brand presentation.
  • Balance sheet signal of ownership: Goodwill: £0 [SEC]. They built this. They didn't buy a mess of things and paper over it. Organic, owned, integrated.

This is the whole widget. Compare to a DoorDash-dependent cloud kitchen: no brand, no real estate, no product soul — just a tenant on someone else's platform paying the landlord tax. Greggs is the landlord.

The one integration weakness: delivery margin is structurally lower and Greggs is now "top four in delivery" via Uber Eats/Just Eat [SEARCH per prelim]. Every order on someone else's platform is a tenancy. Acceptable at current scale; watch the mix.

4. THE NEXT BIG THING — Are they inventing or optimizing?

Optimizing, honestly. And that's fine at this price.

Be direct. Greggs did not invent food-to-go. They perfected the UK-specific, price-point-specific version. They are BlackBerry in reverse — they're keeping a physical product franchise relevant while digital-native competitors (cloud kitchens, app-first brands) try to disrupt from below.

What they are inventing: - The cultural-irony brand positioning with Gen Z — genuinely novel. "Uncool is the new cool" executed on TikTok is an actual product-marketing innovation. - Data-driven price elasticity decisions via Rewards App — not glamorous, but operationally new for UK food retail. - Iceland frozen channel — redefining where a "Greggs" can be consumed.

What they are not inventing: - No international breakout (pilots only, immaterial). - No format disruption (bakery-counter shop is unchanged for decades). - No digital-only product line.

Category creation verdict: they created the "cheap British hot-pastry occasion" category in its modern form, and they still own it. The market is growing — UK food-to-go projected at £24.9bn in 2026 [MEDIA]. They're compounding inside a category they define, even if they're not opening a new one.

This is not iPhone. This is iPod Classic in year 5 — dominant, loved, generating cash, facing a smartphone-era question. The GLP-1/health trend is the smartphone threat here.

The dossier flags this as an open question — I flag it harder.

1. THE ANTI-FRAGILE MOAT (Durability)

The Chaos Test — Does Greggs get stronger when the economy breaks?

On paper, yes. Greggs is the cheap-hot-food-to-go operator in a country where real wages are squeezed, mortgage resets bite, and tax drag is pulling middle-class Britain toward the value end of the lunch market. In a proper recession, a £2 sausage roll is the upgrade path for someone formerly buying Pret.

But look at what FY25 actually shows. Revenue +6.8% to £2.15B [SEC]. Underlying op profit down 4.0%. ROIC from a lovely 20–24% range in 2022-2024 all the way down to 14.2% [CALC]. The "anti-fragile" thesis is that hardship drives customers to them. What actually happened is that hardship drove customers away from spending at all ("subdued consumers" per the CEO) while the government handed Greggs a ~£30–50M/yr labour-cost bill in the form of NLW + employer NI.

That's not anti-fragility. That's an essential business discovering that its wage base moves with minimum-wage politics, not with its own pricing power. A truly anti-fragile business — think GEICO in 2008, or See's in 1974 — would have expanding margins in a downturn. Greggs has compressing ones.

The $1B Competitor Test

Give me a clean billion dollars and tell me to kill Greggs. Can I? Honestly — no, not in under a decade. Here is why:

  • 2,739 owned shops [SEC], plus owned bakeries, plus a new Derby manufacturing site coming online 2026. You cannot replicate that physical estate in a rational timeframe at any price.
  • Brand-as-verb: "Popping to Greggs." That kind of colloquial embedding is what made See's "the only candy worth giving." You cannot buy that with a billion dollars of marketing spend. Pret has spent twenty years and hasn't earned it.
  • Gen Z #1 UK favourite [SEARCH per PION100]. Counter-intuitive. The generation supposedly most obsessed with health picked the sausage-roll company. That is a real moat property — ironic indulgence is harder to attack than earnest cheapness.
  • 8.2% share of the FTG market, which itself is growing to £24.9B by 2026 [MEDIA]. They're a fat fish in a growing pond.

So the moat exists. The question is whether it is widening or narrowing.

Pricing Power — the ultimate proof

Here is where I get uncomfortable. Management tell us they raised prices ~4% into 2026 with LFL holding at +1.6% [MEDIA per Motley Fool]. That sounds like pricing power.

But do the arithmetic Charlie would do: +4% price, +1.6% LFL means roughly -2.4% volume. [CALC] That is the opposite of See's Candies. When I raise prices at See's, I sell the same number of boxes. When Greggs raises prices, about 2.4% of the customers shuffle off to the Tesco meal deal.

Peel Hunt nailed the concern: further price hikes "may erode Greggs' reputation as a value leader" [MEDIA per Tutor2u]. That sentence, right there, is the ceiling on this moat. A true pricing moat has no ceiling. This one does — the moment Tesco's £3.50 meal deal becomes 50p cheaper than the Greggs equivalent, the habit cracks.

Verdict on Pillar 1: REAL MOAT, BUT VOLUME-ELASTIC. Not a See's. Not a Coca-Cola. Closer to a Dairy Queen in the mid-1990s — dominant in its niche, demonstrably preferred, but the customer will trade down if you push too hard.

Greggs plc (GRG.L) — A Feynman Lesson

Price: £16.43 | Market Cap: ~£1.68B | Date: 2026-04-17

1. What This Company Actually Does

Imagine every British high street has a bakery where, for the price of a coffee at Starbucks, you can grab a hot sausage roll, a steak bake, or a cheese-and-onion pasty that was made fresh that morning. That bakery is on 2,739 corners across the UK, and it has a name that Brits use as a verb — "I'm just popping to Greggs." It's a chain of working-class hot-pastry shops, baking most of its own food in its own factories, selling it fast and cheap at lunchtime. Think McDonald's, but for British bakery rather than American burgers.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a Cyclical/Timing problem dressed in Clean-Analytical clothes. Underneath, the business is in excellent shape — it's gaining market share in a shrinking market, the books are some of the cleanest in the entire UK stock market, and the brand is beloved by Gen Z (it's ranked the UK's #1 favorite brand by 20-somethings).

But two cycles are compressing earnings right now and making the stock look ugly:

  1. The UK consumer cycle — Britons are tired, mortgage-resettled, and tax-pinched. Discretionary lunch spending is cautious.
  2. The employment-cost-ratchet cycle — The UK government raised the minimum wage by ~10% and employer payroll taxes in April 2025. Greggs has 32,000 staff. Every minimum-wage hike is a direct punch to its margin.

So 2025 profit fell 9% even though revenue rose 7%. The company is healthy. The earnings print is trough. That's the whole puzzle.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Here's the reusable mental model. When you see a business that is structurally fine but whose current-year numbers are being crushed by a cycle, you do not value it using the crushed numbers. That's the #1 amateur mistake. Here's the discipline:

  1. Identify the cycle. What specifically is temporarily bad? (For Greggs: labour costs + consumer weakness.) Is it the weather, the calendar, a regulatory step-up, or a one-time shock? Name it precisely.

  2. Separate "cycle" from "structure." Cyclical = reverts within 1–5 years. Structural = permanent shift. Test: has this happened before, and did the company recover? Greggs has absorbed wage hikes through price + productivity in 8 of the last 10 years. That's strong evidence this is a cycle, not a death spiral.

  3. Normalize earnings. Look at a multi-year average (2022–2025 for Greggs: ROIC was 22% in the peak, 14% in the trough). The honest mid-cycle estimate sits around 17–18%. Value the business on that number, not trough or peak.

  4. Buy at trough multiples, not trough earnings. The danger is paying a trough P/E (say, 14x) on trough earnings (£120M). The right move is paying a trough P/E on normalized earnings (£175M). If you do that, when the cycle turns, you get paid twice — earnings go up AND the multiple expands.

  5. Demand a margin of safety for the "is it really just a cycle?" question. Because sometimes what looks cyclical is actually structural — tobacco wasn't cyclical, it was terminal. Stress-test: what if you're wrong? Size the position so being wrong doesn't kill you.

This is Buffett's whole playbook for buying See's Candies, American Express after the salad-oil scandal, and Wells Fargo in 1990. You buy good businesses when temporary things make them look bad.

4. How They Make Money

Dead simple. A customer walks into a shop, hands over £2.50 for a sausage roll or £4 for a meal deal, and walks out. Multiply that by 2,739 shops and roughly 250 transactions per shop per day and you get £2.15 billion of revenue a year.

Of that £2.15B: - ~61% is gross margin (they bake it cheaper than they sell it — the classic food-retail spread), - ~9% survives to operating profit (wages, rent, and packaging eat most of the gross margin), - ~£75M becomes free cash flow after they reinvest in new shops and a new Derby factory coming online in 2026.

There's no subscription, no contract, no B2B sales force. It's the purest form of commerce: make a thing, sell a thing, repeat. About 3% comes from franchised shops (in train stations and petrol forecourts) and 2% from frozen Greggs-branded products sold in Iceland supermarkets — but 95% is just walk-in foot traffic.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Three reinforcing reasons, in plain English:

(a) The brand is the category. If you're a British person who wants a cheap hot lunch, "Greggs" is the word that appears in your head. It's not a brand — it's a reflex, like "Google" for search or "Hoover" for vacuums. When a brand becomes a verb, competitors don't compete on product; they compete on habit, which is brutally hard to dislodge. Pret A Manger — the premium competitor — is currently copying Greggs by trialling meal deals. That's the tell.

(b) They own their factories. Most food chains buy their food from suppliers. Greggs bakes most of its own. This means every time raw material costs spike, Greggs has a structural cost advantage over competitors who pay a middleman markup. The new Derby factory adds ~20% capacity headroom — they're building ahead of demand, which is what confident operators do.

(c) The Gen Z tailwind. Here's the strange, wonderful thing: Greggs is ranked the #1 favourite UK brand among Gen Z, beating Apple and Nike. Greggs became ironically cool on TikTok. When Greggs collaborated with Marks & Spencer, they got 350,000 TikTok followers. Brand cool is a 20-year asset — hard to measure, but when you've got it, you're genuinely hard to kill.

None of these is a fortress the size of Coca-Cola's or Microsoft's. But stacked together, they make this a legitimate moat — just a weathered one, not an impregnable one.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

Ozempic. Or more precisely, GLP-1 weight-loss drugs going mainstream.

Greggs sells calorie-dense pastry. If 10–20% of the UK population ends up on appetite-suppressant drugs over the next decade — and that's roughly what industry panels are projecting — then the category itself shrinks, slowly and permanently, the way cigarettes shrunk after the 1964 Surgeon General report. The CEO says sales weakness is "subdued consumers, not fat jabs." The short sellers betting against the stock say it's fat jabs. We genuinely don't know yet, and the honest datapoint from early 2026 is a little scary: Greggs raised prices 4% and volume dropped 2.4% — the first real sign that price elasticity is showing up. If that compounds at 1–2% per year for 15 years, the business looks very different in 2040.

This is why you don't put 20% of your portfolio here. It's a real 10-year tail risk, not a fantasy.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Imagine a house on a nice street. The roof is in perfect condition. The foundations are solid. The location is desirable to young people. But right now the city has imposed a one-year construction tax on the block — so every homeowner on the street is paying £5,000 extra in taxes this year only. The rational market price for that house should reflect a temporary headwind. But what often happens instead is that the whole street trades at a permanent 30% discount, as if the construction tax were forever.

That's Greggs today.

The house (the business) is worth, on a proper mid-cycle basis, roughly £18–23 per share. A private buyer — a private equity firm doing the math on 12x EBITDA the way they would for any UK food-retail franchise — would probably pay closer to £33. The current price is £16.43. You're buying the house while the temporary tax is being visibly paid.

Why is it on sale? Three reasons: - Fear — the stock is on the UK's most-shorted list, alongside other "UK-consumer-in-decline" names. Thematic shorts routinely grab the wrong name within a basket. - Boredom — an 86-year-old pastry chain is the opposite of the AI narrative. Institutional money chases narrative, not low-narrative compounders. - Guilt by association — GLP-1 is real, so the market is applying tobacco-terminal-decline multiples to a brand that's still growing revenue and gaining share.

The Munger verdict is BUY, with discipline. Not a table-pound; a measured buy. Start with a starter position here, add aggressively if it drops to £13–14, and hold a stop-loss: if volume declines get worse for two quarters in a row, or if the Gen Z loyalty signal cracks, the thesis changes and you exit.

The lesson to take away: When a good business looks bad because of a visible, countable, historically-survived cycle — and the market has priced the cycle as if it were forever — that's where disciplined long-term investors get paid. Not always. But often enough that the pattern is worth learning by name.
