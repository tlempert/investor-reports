The test is whether getting bigger lets you lower unit costs, which drives more volume, which makes you bigger still. A true flywheel is self-reinforcing and accelerating. A doom loop is the opposite — scale breeds bureaucracy, bureaucracy slows product, product slips, customers leave.

Teaching Note: Veeva Systems (VEEV)

Feynman-style investor education | 2026-06-24

1. What This Company Actually Does

Veeva Systems builds the software that pharmaceutical companies use to run clinical trials, manage drug approvals, and track which doctors prescribe which medicines. Think of it as the operating system for the business of making and selling drugs — the system that keeps Pfizer's files in order when the FDA comes to inspect, and that helps a sales rep at Bayer know which cardiologist to call on Monday morning. Without software like Veeva's, a clinical trial involving 50,000 patients across 30 countries would be a compliance nightmare. With it, you get a single, auditable, regulator-approved record of everything.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a Clean Analytical problem with a Narrative/momentum overlay sitting on top.

Let me explain both halves, because understanding the difference is worth more than the stock tip itself.

The clean analytical core. Veeva's economics are about as legible as software gets. Revenue is 83% recurring subscriptions — customers sign multi-year contracts and renew because they have no real choice (more on that in a moment). Gross margins are 75%. Free cash flow margins are 44%. The company carries zero debt and holds $6.4 billion in cash. Growth is running at 16% per year. There is no commodity cycle, no regulatory binary, no geopolitical wildcard dominating the story. You can build a spreadsheet, run a discounted cash flow, check your assumptions against history, and get a number you can defend. That is what "clean analytical" means: the tools work.

The narrative overlay. Then why is the stock down roughly 30% in 2026? Not because anything in the business broke. The market applied a sweeping fear — "artificial intelligence will commoditize vertical software" — to every company that sells software to a specific industry. The fear arrived as a story, spread as a sentiment, and repriced a good business as if it were a bad one. This is the narrative/momentum overlay: sentiment is setting the price while fundamentals set the value. The gap between the two is what creates the opportunity.

Why does the problem type matter? Because the mistake you make in a narrative-overlay situation is different from the mistake you make in a clean analytical one. In a clean analytical problem, you might build your spreadsheet wrong. In a narrative problem, you can build the spreadsheet perfectly and still lose money if you buy right before the narrative gets worse. The right response is: trust the fundamentals, but be honest about how long the narrative can run. Here, the evidence suggests the narrative is running out of factual fuel — and we will get to why in Sections 4 through 7.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

When a business is fundamentally clean but priced by fear, there is a five-step framework that keeps you from making either of the two classic errors: (a) capitulating to the narrative and selling a good business cheap, or (b) dismissing the narrative entirely and walking into a real risk you did not model.

Step 1: Separate the narrative from the fundamentals. Write down the bear story in its strongest form. Then go look at the actual numbers. Is the business getting worse, or is the stock getting worse? These are not the same thing. For Veeva: revenue growing 16%, free cash flow growing faster, margins expanding. The business is fine.

Step 2: Find the one fact that would make the narrative true. Every fear story has a load-bearing assumption. "AI commoditizes vertical SaaS" requires the AI to be able to do the regulated, audited, validated work that Veeva's software does, without years of re-certification. If that fact does not materialize, the narrative collapses on its own weight. Identify that fact before the market does.

Step 3: Check whether the company is absorbing or being disrupted. There is a huge difference between a company that is helplessly watching a new technology eat its lunch and a company that is baking the new technology into its own product. Ask: is management shipping anything, or just talking? For Veeva, the answer is shipping — Falcon (an AI product built on Veeva's own proprietary regulatory data), plus AI Agents embedded across the Vault platform.

Step 4: Anchor on the right earnings base. Narrative situations often coincide with an accounting ambiguity that amplifies the fear. Here, the ambiguity is real: Veeva's GAAP earnings ($5.44/share) look one way; its free cash flow ($10.20/share) looks another. The entire difference is stock-based compensation — a real cost, but one that is already captured in the free cash flow figure through dilution. If you anchor on the wrong number, you will estimate the wrong price. Anchoring on free cash flow makes $160 a clear buy; anchoring on GAAP earnings makes it a borderline wait. Choose deliberately.

Step 5: Size the position for the scenario where the narrative is right. Even if you are 80% confident the fear is misapplied, hold 20% confidence that you are wrong. What happens if the bear case plays out? In Veeva's case: the company has $6.4 billion in cash, no debt, and a 44% FCF margin — revenue would have to fall by something like 60% before the company burns money. That is a company with genuine resilience. When the downside is bounded, you can size the position with confidence.

4. How They Make Money

Veeva has two businesses inside one company, and both make money the same way: annual subscription contracts.

The R&D and Quality side is where you pay Veeva to manage your clinical trial data, your regulatory submissions, your quality control documentation, and your manufacturing records. A global pharmaceutical company running 20 simultaneous clinical trials needs every document to be in a system the FDA can audit on 24 hours' notice. Veeva's Vault platform is that system. Customers pay $5 million to $50 million a year for this privilege, depending on their size and how many modules they use.

The Commercial side is where you pay Veeva to manage your sales force — which doctors are you targeting, what did your reps say in their last visit, what data do you need to know about prescribing trends in Cleveland. This is the CRM (customer relationship management) business, and it is the one being migrated off a legacy platform onto Veeva's own Vault infrastructure by the end of 2029.

The reason customers pay so much is simple: the software is not optional, and the cost of switching is enormous (see Section 5). Veeva is not selling a nice-to-have productivity tool. It is selling a compliance requirement. And compliance requirements, in a regulated industry, are about as price-inelastic as anything in the economy.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Here is the moat in one sentence: Veeva's software is not just installed — it is validated.

That word — validated — does all the work. When a pharmaceutical company deploys Veeva, it runs a formal qualification process to prove to the FDA and the EMA that the system operates correctly and that its records are trustworthy. This validation takes months and costs millions of dollars. It generates thousands of pages of documentation. Once a company has done it, ripping out Veeva and switching to a competitor means doing the entire exercise again, from scratch, while all ongoing clinical trials continue to run. No compliance officer voluntarily puts her career on the line for that.

This is what investors mean by "switching costs" — not the inconvenience of learning new software, but the organizational and regulatory trauma of replacing a system that a federal regulator has effectively blessed. It is more like switching the electrical grid in a hospital than switching your email client.

On top of that, Veeva has quietly built what is arguably the world's most complete database of healthcare professionals — 12 million physicians, hospitals, and clinics with regulatory-grade contact and prescribing data. That data set took a decade to build and cannot be replicated cheaply or quickly by a competitor. The Bloomberg terminal of life sciences, if you will: if you need the data and you need it to be compliance-ready, there is one real source.

These two moats — validated lock-in and proprietary data — mean that even a well-funded competitor (and Salesforce, in partnership with IQVIA, is a well-funded competitor) faces years of work just to get to the starting line.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The biggest single risk is the 2029 commercial CRM cliff, and it is worth understanding precisely.

Veeva's legacy CRM — the software its pharmaceutical sales reps use to manage their doctor relationships — was originally built on top of Salesforce's technology platform. That arrangement worked fine for a decade. But Veeva and Salesforce are now competitors, and the legacy CRM's end-of-life is December 31, 2029. Every pharmaceutical company using the old CRM must, by that date, choose one of two replacements: Vault CRM (Veeva's own rebuilt product on its proprietary platform) or Salesforce Life Sciences Cloud (now called Agentforce Life Sciences, backed by IQVIA's data). This is a two-horse race for a market worth roughly $2.5 billion annually, with a hard deadline and a known finish line. If Veeva wins most of this migration, the commercial business compounds for another decade. If Salesforce makes a serious run at the top-20 pharma accounts, a meaningful chunk of Veeva's revenue base is contested. As of March 2026, Veeva is ahead — 125 or more Vault CRM customers already live, targeting 200 by fiscal year end — but the race is not over. This is the one number worth tracking every quarter between now and 2029.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Here is why the stock trades at $160 when the evidence suggests it is worth $250 or more — explained as a house-buying situation.

Imagine a house in a good neighborhood — brick construction, zero mortgage, renting for strong cash flow, and the landlord has a $6 million savings account in the basement. Two buyers show up to appraise it. The first buyer looks at the reported rental income on the tax return, notices that the landlord gives $50,000 a year in discounts to a family member (call this the stock-compensation equivalent), and prices the house based on the after-discount net income. He offers $1.0 million. The second buyer ignores the family discount, counts the actual cash deposited in the bank every month, and notices that the savings account in the basement is worth $6 million all by itself. He offers $1.6 million.

Both buyers are looking at the same house. The gap is entirely about which earnings number you trust. Veeva reports GAAP net income of $909 million per year — real, auditable profit. But it also pays $473 million a year in stock-based compensation, which is a real cost to shareholders (it dilutes them) but is not a cash expense. This creates a gap: GAAP says Veeva earns $5.44 per share; free cash flow says $10.20 per share. The DCF built on free cash flow gives a fair value around $250–258. The multiple built on GAAP earnings, at a conservative 18–25 times, gives something closer to $98–136. The market is pricing the stock at $160, roughly halfway between the two — and about 30% below even the conservative cash-based fair value.

The additional weight pushing the price down is the narrative de-rate described in Section 2: the AI fear. A stock that would ordinarily trade at 25–30 times free cash flow for its quality level is being held at 16 times because the crowd is applying a general fear to a specific business that the fear does not actually fit. Veeva owns proprietary regulatory data that general-purpose AI models cannot replicate, and it is embedding AI into its own products rather than being replaced by it. The fear is real — the application is wrong.

The honest case for buying at $160 is not that you are getting a perfect business at a perfect price. The genuine haircut is the $473 million in annual stock compensation, which is high relative to peers (about 2.4 times the median for comparable software companies) and which represents real dilution to shareholders. The honest case is that a debt-free, founder-led monopoly growing its cash earnings at 20% per year — while sitting on $6.4 billion in cash and protected by regulatory moats that would take a competitor a decade to replicate — is on sale because the crowd is afraid of a headline and confused by an accounting presentation. That is the kind of gap that great investors wait years to find.
