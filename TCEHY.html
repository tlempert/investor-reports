THE MOAT TRIBUNAL

Before I pass judgment, I put the business itself on trial. Charlie's first question is never "what's the price?" — it's "what's the machine?" If the machine is broken, the price is an illusion. So I count the moat flags.

Buffett flagged this MINOR. Biologist MINOR. Historian MINOR. Sherlock MINOR. Cook MINOR. Futurist MINOR. Lynch MINOR. Psychologist NONE. Anthropologist NONE domestically, MODERATE internationally. Bezos MODERATE (the ByteDance 90/10 mini-game crack). Jobs MODERATE (same). Burry MODERATE, but his flag is really a forensic worry about MTM margin inflation and a slow-drip investee portfolio, not an assertion the moat itself is dissolving. Zero SEVERE flags across twelve experts. That's a clean tribunal.

What is the machine, concretely? WeChat is a communication substrate for 1.3 billion people — the Biologist is right to call it a keystone species, and the Anthropologist is right to call it civilization infrastructure. It is TCP/IP with a payments rail bolted on. 80 million merchants integrated. Nine million mini-programs. WeChat Pay moves roughly $3 trillion of annual volume at a take rate (0.1–0.6%) deliberately set below Visa's 1.5–2.5% — which is the Biologist's single best observation: Tencent is running a mutualistic rent strategy, not a parasitic one. It keeps the take low to deepen participation, and captures the aggregate network value through ads, games, and the ability to monetize adjacent layers later. That is exactly how you build a moat that compounds instead of rotting.

Sitting on top of the substrate is a gaming franchise machine — Honor of Kings, League of Legends (via Riot), PUBG Mobile, Valorant — that grew international revenue +35% YoY in Q3 2025. The franchise machine is not the moat; it is a high-returning business riding on top of the moat, as Buffett properly distinguishes. And beneath all of it sits the $105B investee portfolio: Spotify, Epic, Meituan, Pinduoduo, Sea, JD. That's 17.9% of market cap in a nutrient reserve that Tencent has repeatedly demonstrated it will distribute when it matures (JD shares 2021, Meituan shares 2023).

The 1260H "Chinese Military Company" designation hit in January 2025 and cost 7.8% on the day. It has had zero operational consequence since. Riot operates. Epic is untouched. ROIC has traced 21.0% → 18.8% → 13.6% → 16.7%. That's not a dying franchise; that's a franchise that got beaten over the head with regulatory batons in 2021–2023 and is getting back up. Gross margin at 55.5% is the highest since 2016 — and margins don't expand in businesses that are losing their moat.

Moat Tribunal Result: Strong. Not pristine, not SEVERE-flag-free forever, but strong. Apply the normal Quality Floor.

THE BULL-BEAR BALANCE

Now the hard part. I weight Burry carefully because a lone SELL in an eleven-to-one council is either noise to be ignored or the only expert smart enough to see what the others are missing. Charlie's rule: take the dissenter seriously or he earns a free option on your humiliation.

Burry's five fractures deserve one-by-one examination. First: MTM-inflated margins. He's right that the 2024 net margin of 29.4% vs 2023's 18.9% partly reflects investee portfolio re-rating flowing through P&L. But he then does the correct thing himself and says real operating margins are 22–25%. Fine. Apply 24% margin to FY2025 revenue run-rate of ~$113B USD and you get ~$27B of real operating income. At 18.4x trailing P/E that is still a 15% earnings yield on normalized operations plus the investee portfolio on the side. The accounting quibble is real; the thesis impact is minor.

Second: buybacks funding Prosus's exit. I partly concede this. Yes, Tencent is absorbing Prosus supply. But here Charlie would invert Burry's framing: a forced seller dumping 0.2% of shares per month into a buyback executed at 18x earnings is not a bug — it is the best possible scenario for long-term holders. Management is retiring shares at a discount to intrinsic value from a seller who must sell for reasons unrelated to value. That's an arbitrage, not a pass-through. If Prosus weren't selling, Tencent would be paying higher prices for the same repurchases. The Psychologist has this right: aggressive, accelerating buybacks ($29B → $44B → $102B HKD over three years) during maximum external uncertainty is the clearest insider-confidence signal available, and Burry's framing hides that fact.

Third: the $358B debt wall plus AI capex in 2026. This is Burry's sharpest point and I give it real weight. He is right that FCF will compress in 2026 by management's own admission, and a leveraged balance sheet meeting a deliberately suppressed cash flow year is how companies get into trouble. But the dossier's partial answer is that the $358B gross debt nets against $105B of liquid investees plus $23.8B of annual FCF generation — the coverage isn't pristine, but Tencent is not refinancing its entire debt stack in 2026. Burry himself doesn't produce a maturity schedule showing a refi cliff; he flags it as a risk. I price it as a risk: position sizing should reflect that 2026 will have weaker headline numbers than 2025 or 2027.

Fourth: $105B portfolio is slow-drip, not a cash reserve. Correct. Burry's 40–60 cents on the dollar under forced liquidation is a fair stress case. But I don't underwrite the investee portfolio at forced-sale value — I underwrite it at what Tencent has historically realized through orderly distributions, and that number has been close to book. The portfolio is shrinking by design (the Buffett "commoditize the complement" pattern) and returning value to shareholders. This is a feature of the strategy, not a crack in it.

Fifth: ad ramp will decelerate. Also correct, eventually. Facebook's video-ad ramp from 2015–2017 did decelerate from 40–60% to 20–25%. But the Futurist's point is load-bearing here: WeChat ARPU is roughly $38/user/year versus Meta at $200+. Even if growth rates decelerate sharply, the absolute revenue increment from closing even a quarter of that gap is $30B+ per year. Burry is pricing deceleration; he is not pricing the denominator.

So Burry gets credit for forensic discipline and I revise my enthusiasm downward for 2026 specifically. But his thesis does not overturn the tribunal.

Peter Lynch's counter-weight matters here. Lynch's single best observation is that Tencent trades at a 10.8-turn P/E discount to peer median (18.4x vs 29.2x) and the marginal seller is a Western fund manager whose compliance department said "CMC list, get out." That is institutional constraint, not fundamental analysis. When stock is sold for compliance reasons rather than business-value reasons, the mispricing is the opportunity. This is a fear premium, not a risk premium. Fear premiums evaporate when the fear doesn't materialize. Risk premiums stay. I find this argument decisive at the margin.

The Private Buyer Test. What would a rational strategic acquirer pay for Tencent in a world where it could be acquired? The answer is a lot more than $586B. WeChat alone, at Meta's EV/user of roughly $400, would value the messaging franchise at $500B+. Add the gaming franchise at 15x $8B of segment FCF = $120B. Add $105B of investees at book. That's $725B+ before any synergy or control premium, and I'm being stingy on WeChat because I've used a Western peer at substantial political discount to the underlying utility value. A Private Buyer Test floor says we are buying at 80 cents on the dollar versus the strategic-acquirer price.

Weighting: I weight the bull case at 72% and the bear case at 28%. The bear case carries real weight because Burry's 2026 FCF compression worry is quantifiable and because the 1260H tail, while low-probability, is genuinely binary and irreversible on the US asset stake. The bull case dominates because the moat is clean, the price is cheap on any honest calculation, capital allocation is among the best I have seen outside Omaha, and the dominant driver of the discount is compliance-driven selling that does not reflect business reality.

Tencent: A Feynman-Style Explanation

Ticker: TCEHY / 0700.HK Price at Analysis: $64.87 (ADR) Council Verdict: BUY — 11/12 experts, 5–8% position, prefer 700.HK over ADR

1. What This Company Actually Does

Imagine if the U.S. Post Office, Venmo, Facebook Messenger, the App Store, and a massive video game studio were all fused into a single app that every American was required to use for daily life — to talk to their boss, pay their landlord, order lunch, and file their taxes. That app is WeChat, and Tencent built it. Tencent's second job is running the world's largest gaming empire — the company behind League of Legends, PUBG Mobile, and dozens of other titles played by hundreds of millions of people globally. Together, these two machines generate nearly $100 billion in annual revenue, and the business is accelerating.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a Regime/Political problem — with a crucial twist.

Here is the first thing you must recognize: some investment decisions are fundamentally analytical, and some are fundamentally political. In an analytical case, you read the balance sheet, estimate the moat, run a DCF, and make a call. In a political case, the analytical work is necessary but not sufficient — because a government with enough will can override any fundamental no matter how strong. When you are dealing with a political case, you are not really asking "is this business good?" You are asking "will this business be allowed to stay good?"

Tencent is a political case. It operates inside China, which means the Chinese Communist Party has the ability to regulate, restrict, fine, or fundamentally reshape what Tencent is allowed to do. We saw this play out between 2021 and 2023: gaming licenses were frozen, Tencent was publicly labeled a purveyor of "spiritual opium," a gaming regulator announced spending limits that wiped $46 billion from the stock in a single day. These were not market moves. They were edicts from a government.

But here is the twist that makes Tencent a more nuanced case than a simple "China risk, stay away" conclusion: Beijing has publicly rehabilitated Tencent. In February 2025, Xi Jinping personally invited Pony Ma — Tencent's founder — to a technology summit alongside the founders of DeepSeek, Xiaomi, and other national champions. The regulator who announced the devastating gaming restrictions in 2023 was fired within weeks. Gaming license approvals resumed at normal pace. This is not a rumor or a hope; it is a documented public signal from the top of the Chinese state that Tencent is now positioned as part of the solution — China's AI champion — rather than a target for restraint.

The second political layer is American. In January 2025, the U.S. Department of Defense added Tencent to its "Chinese Military Company" list under Section 1260H. The stock fell 7.8% on the day. As of this analysis, that designation has had zero operational consequence — Riot Games still operates, Epic Games is untouched — but it creates a latent tail: if Congress advances legislation with actual asset-freeze or investment-ban provisions, ADR holders could face forced liquidation.

So you have a regime/political problem with dual-regime architecture: Beijing's risk has been materially de-risked, while Washington's risk remains latent but real. That asymmetry — and how you size your position around it — is the analytical heart of this decision.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

The Dual-Regime Framework for Political Investments

Every time you encounter a regime/political investment case, run this four-step framework before touching a single financial ratio. Apply it to Tencent today, apply it to Samsung tomorrow, apply it to any company where a government's preferences determine the business trajectory.

Step 1: Identify who the relevant regime actors are, and what they want.

Political risk is not monolithic. "China risk" is not one risk — it is at minimum two distinct risks (Beijing + Washington), and often more (provincial, sector regulator, foreign jurisdiction). For each actor, ask: what do they want from this company? Are they extracting rent, making an example, building national champions, or managing capital flows? The answers are different, and they lead to different probability estimates. Beijing wanted to signal control over tech billionaires in 2021; by 2025 it wants AI dominance and needs Tencent's infrastructure to get there. That is not the same political regime as 2021 even though it is the same government.

Step 2: Classify the regime risk as "crackdown," "rehabilitation," or "latent."

Crackdown means the political actor is actively hostile and the fundamental is under direct threat. Rehabilitation means the actor was hostile but has signaled reversal. Latent means no current operational impact but a plausible escalation path exists. Tencent's Beijing risk is rehabilitated. Tencent's Washington risk is latent. These require completely different responses in your analysis.

Step 3: Size the position as if you could be wrong about the regime — not as if you are right.

This is the discipline most investors skip. If the business is wonderful but a regime tail exists, you do not get to size the position as if the tail is impossible. You size it to survive the tail being wrong. For Tencent: if Beijing reverses course, the stock probably falls 30–50%. If Washington escalates 1260H to asset freezes, ADR holders face similar or worse. A 5–8% position means you lose 1.5–4% of your portfolio in the worst case — painful but survivable. A 20% position means you lose 6–10% in the same scenario. The moat does not change; only the sizing discipline changes.

Step 4: Prefer the legal structure that minimizes regime tail.

When two listing vehicles exist — the ADR and the primary HK listing — they carry different regime exposures. The Hong Kong-listed shares (700.HK) are insulated from the specific U.S. ADR-freeze vector. Buying the HK shares instead of the ADR does not eliminate political risk, but it eliminates one specific escalation path. Always ask: which instrument has the smallest exposure to the dominant tail? Buy that one.

The reusable output of this framework: fundamentals earn you the right to analyze the business. Regime analysis earns you the right to size the position. Do not conflate the two steps.

4. How They Make Money

Tencent's revenue comes from three engines, stacked on top of the WeChat infrastructure.

Engine 1: Games (~48% of revenue). Tencent either owns or has major stakes in virtually every large gaming studio on earth — Riot Games (League of Legends, Valorant), Supercell (Clash of Clans), Epic Games (Fortnite), and dozens of Chinese titles. Players pay for in-game items, characters, and access. The brilliance is that Tencent integrates these games into WeChat, removing every friction point: you log in with WeChat, pay with WeChat Pay, and invite friends through WeChat chat. The platform is the distribution channel.

Engine 2: Advertising (~18% of revenue). WeChat's 1.3 billion users generate an enormous amount of behavioral data — who you talk to, what businesses you use, what you search for. Tencent sells access to that attention. Critically, WeChat monetizes far below its potential: users generate about $38 in ad revenue per year, versus Meta's $200+ per user. That gap is not a weakness — it is a multi-year monetization runway. Video Accounts ads, Mini Program ads, and WeChat Search ads are all growing 50–60% year over year as Tencent gradually closes that gap.

Engine 3: FinTech and Cloud (~31% of revenue). WeChat Pay processes roughly $3 trillion in annual payments. Tencent deliberately charges merchants a take rate of 0.1–0.6% — far below Visa's 1.5–2.5% — because their strategy is to make the payments rail free enough that everyone uses it, and then monetize the usage data and the relationships it creates. This is the mutualism strategy: keep the fee low, deepen the network, capture value at the adjacent layers.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

WeChat is not a social app. That is the single most important thing to understand about Tencent's moat. It is communication infrastructure. Eighty million merchants have their entire customer relationship management built inside WeChat. Nine million Mini Programs — entire businesses — exist only inside WeChat. Chinese citizens use WeChat to pay utilities, renew licenses, file insurance claims, and schedule hospital appointments. You cannot switch away from WeChat the way you might switch from Instagram to TikTok, because WeChat is not entertainment — it is the substrate through which daily commerce and government services operate.

This creates a network effect of the most durable kind: not "my friends are here," which can migrate, but "my bank, landlord, employer, and government are here," which cannot migrate without rebuilding the entire social and commercial infrastructure of 1.3 billion people's daily lives. No competitor has come close. ByteDance's Douyin has 750 million daily users for entertainment, but merchants do not run their businesses on Douyin. The moat is not the social graph. It is the commercial and civic infrastructure.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The single risk that matters most is not ByteDance, not AI competition, not debt levels. It is Washington escalating the 1260H Chinese Military Company designation from a label into operational law — specifically, legislation requiring U.S. institutions to divest positions in designated entities or freezing cross-border transactions. That scenario, which the Historian estimates at 10–15% probability over three years, would crater the TCEHY ADR by 30–50% in a matter of days regardless of whether Tencent's business had changed at all. The business does not have to get worse for this risk to materialize; Congress simply has to act. Everything else on the risk list — ByteDance's mini-game economics, AI capex compressing 2026 free cash flow, Prosus selling its stake — is meaningful but manageable. The geopolitical tail is the only scenario where being right about the business does not protect you.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Here is the house-buying analogy. Imagine a magnificent house — the best-built house on the most desirable block in the city, with structural features no contractor could replicate today. The house generates substantial rental income that has been growing every year for two decades, and the landlord who owns it is one of the most disciplined capital allocators you have ever met. The house is worth, by any honest appraisal, somewhere between $63 and $82 per share equivalent.

The asking price is $64.87.

Why is the house not priced at $82? Because the realtor has a reputation problem. A neighbor's house on the same block — built differently, with a more confrontational owner who picked fights with city hall — had its permits revoked three years ago and the owner disappeared for a while. Western buyers heard "Chinese block" and stopped visiting open houses entirely. The remaining sellers are large institutional funds whose compliance departments said: "The city has flagged this block. We cannot hold it. Sell." And so they sold — not because the house's fundamentals changed, but because their rulebooks changed.

That is what a fear premium looks like. It is not based on what the house is worth. It is based on what the institutional buyers are allowed to own. When compliance constraints, not fundamentals, drive selling, the price can stay below value for a long time — until the fear either materializes (in which case, your position-sizing saved you) or evaporates (in which case, you bought a great house at a fair price and the market eventually noticed).

The stock is not screaming cheap. At $64.87, you are paying fair value for a wonderful business, not a distressed price for an uncertain one. The upside comes from three sources: ongoing buybacks retiring shares at below-intrinsic-value prices, a $105 billion investment portfolio in listed companies that the headline P/E ignores entirely, and an advertising monetization ramp from $38 to something approaching $100 per user over the next several years. The gain scenario requires only that the compounding machine keeps running. The loss scenario requires that Congress escalates a designation that has been operationally inert for fifteen months. Those are not symmetric odds.

Buy the house. Own it in Hong Kong, not New York. Size the position for the tail you cannot eliminate.
