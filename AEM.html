Agnico Eagle is in the business of digging gold out of the ground and selling it. 2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a cyclical/timing problem — perhaps the purest example in public markets.

Here is the core tension: Agnico Eagle's revenue is almost entirely a function of gold price, not of how hard they work or how cleverly they operate. In a given year, they mine roughly 3.4 million ounces. That number does not swing wildly. What swings wildly is the price they receive per ounce.

Gold is trading near $3,100/oz today — an all-time high. At that price, Agnico earns a margin of roughly $1,600-1,800/oz after all-in costs of around $1,300-1,500/oz. Those are extraordinary margins. Their net margin is 37.5%. Free cash flow is $4.26 billion per year. Every metric looks spectacular.

Now rewind to 2018-2019, when gold traded near $1,250-1,300/oz. At that price, Agnico's margin per ounce was slim — $100 to $200 per ounce on a good day. Free cash flow was a fraction of today's number.

This is the defining feature of commodity cyclicals: the business hasn't fundamentally changed, but the income statement looks completely different depending on when you look at it. The company didn't get four times better between 2018 and 2026. Gold got more expensive.

The question every investor must answer before buying AEM is not "Is this a well-run mining company?" (it clearly is). The question is: Has gold permanently repriced to $3,000+, or is this a peak that will revert to a $1,800-2,000 mid-cycle mean?

That question is genuinely unresolved. Gold's recent rise has been driven by central bank buying, de-dollarization fears, geopolitical uncertainty, and inflation hedging — some of which may be structural. But every prior gold spike has eventually mean-reverted. The 1980 spike to $850 was followed by a 20-year bear market. The 2011 spike to $1,900 was followed by a 45% crash to $1,050. History does not guarantee a repeat, but it insists on being heard.

The ambiguity is real. This is not a case where the answer is obvious. It is a case where the price you pay must reflect the possibility that today's gold price is temporary.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

The framework for cyclical commodity investing was codified by Benjamin Graham and refined by every value investor since. It has one central rule: normalize earnings across the cycle before you set a price.

Here is why that rule exists. Imagine a steel company. In a boom year, steel prices are high, margins are fat, earnings per share hit $10. The stock trades at $100 — which looks like 10x earnings, a seemingly cheap multiple. In a bust year, steel prices crash, margins disappear, earnings per share fall to $1. The stock trades at $30 — which looks like 30x earnings, a seemingly expensive multiple. A naive investor buys at $100 because "it's only 10x earnings" and sells at $30 because "it's too expensive at 30x." They bought peak earnings and sold trough earnings. They did exactly backwards.

The correct approach is to ask: What does this company earn at mid-cycle commodity prices? Then pay a fair multiple of that normalized number.

For Agnico Eagle, the math works like this. At $3,100/oz gold (today), earnings power is roughly $8.85/share. At $2,000/oz gold (a plausible mid-cycle level), earnings power drops to roughly $3.50-4.50/share — because costs are relatively fixed but revenue per ounce falls by $1,100. At $1,500/oz gold (a bear case that has occurred twice in the last 15 years), the company still survives and generates modest cash, but per-share earnings shrink to $1-2.

A sensible mid-cycle earnings estimate is somewhere around $4-5/share. Paying 25-30x that normalized number gives you a fair value in the range of $100-150/share. That is the ceiling on what a disciplined value investor should pay.

The trap is that at peak earnings, the P/E ratio looks cheap. AEM at $217 trades at 24.5x peak earnings. Investors see "only 24x" and think it is reasonable. But 24x peak earnings is actually the most expensive possible moment to buy — because those earnings are the highest they will ever be, and the multiple is calculated on an inflated denominator.

Munger's version of this rule: Never pay peak multiples for peak earnings. The combination is doubly lethal. You pay a high price, and then earnings fall, and then the market also applies a lower multiple to those lower earnings. The stock can easily fall 50-70% without the company doing anything wrong.

The only time to confidently buy a cyclical commodity producer is when earnings look terrible, the P/E ratio looks frighteningly high (because earnings are depressed, not because the price is inflated), and everyone is convinced the commodity will never recover. That is when you buy. Not today.
