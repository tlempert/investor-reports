Dino Polska — A Business Teacher's Guide

Feynman clarity. Buffett patience. Munger inversion. ~1,200 words.

1. What Dino Does

Picture a small town in rural Poland — population 8,000, one main street, no Walmart, no Whole Foods. The nearest big city is forty minutes away. Most residents are buying their groceries at a local corner shop where the sausages are limp, the prices are high, and the selection is whatever the owner felt like ordering that week.

Then Dino shows up and builds a clean, well-lit, 400-square-meter supermarket on the edge of town. Fresh meat counter. Polish-cut cold cuts. Good bread. Competitive prices. Open seven days. The town's families switch almost immediately, because for the first time they have a real grocery store without the forty-minute drive.

That is Dino Polska's entire business model, repeated roughly 62 times per quarter across small-town and rural Poland. They now operate about 2,700 of these stores, all under the same brand, all roughly the same format. They are the Walmart of Polish villages — except they got there first, and in this analogy the village roads are still mostly unpaved and the 4,000+ remaining sites are sitting there waiting.

Dino sells what people eat every day: meat, dairy, vegetables, bread, and basic household goods. There is no luxury angle. The customer is not choosing between Dino and a Michelin star; they are choosing between Dino and driving further for the same thing.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

Before you can evaluate any business, you have to correctly classify the problem. Get this wrong and every number you look at will lead you to the wrong conclusion.

Dino is not a broken company. It is not a value trap. It is a growth-capex compounder going through a cyclical margin squeeze. These two things are happening simultaneously, and the market is treating them as one catastrophic signal. Separating them is the whole game.

The first thread: Dino is in the middle of its aggressive store rollout. Building and stocking a new store costs money today, but the store earns money for decades. When a company invests heavily in stores it intends to own and operate, the accounting looks like cash destruction. The cash statement turns ugly not because the business is failing, but because the business is building. You have to distinguish growth capex (building future earnings) from maintenance capex (keeping existing earnings alive).

The second thread: Polish minimum wages roughly doubled over five years. That hits Dino hard because most of their 55,000+ employees are in-store workers. Margins fell from 5.7% to 4.5% over that window. The critical question — the entire investment debate in one sentence — is whether this margin compression is cyclical (wages shot up sharply, the business adapted, now the 2026 wage hike is just ~3% and margins will recover) or structural (Dino has permanently lost pricing power to Biedronka and Lidl, and margins will keep eroding regardless of the wage cycle). The council's majority view is cyclical; Michael Burry's minority view is that the evidence is not yet conclusive. Both sides are worth holding in your mind.

3. How to Think About It

Owner earnings vs. reported free cash flow. This is the single most important lens. Dino's reported FCF is PLN 0.80B. Its owner earnings — roughly operating cash flow minus only the capex needed to maintain existing stores, not build new ones — are PLN 2.25B. That PLN 1.45B difference is not waste or fraud; it is the annual investment in new stores. If those new stores earn even halfway decent returns (and their track record shows 18–22% ROIC on invested capital), the PLN 1.45B is worth far more than PLN 1.45B over time.

The simple rule: if a company has a consistent track record of deploying capital at returns above its cost of capital, do not penalize the FCF line for the deployment. Evaluate the returns on the deployment separately.

Normalizing margins across the wage cycle. When a one-time or temporary cost spike hits a grocery chain, the right move is to ask: what would margins look like in a normal year? Polish minimum wage was growing ~20% annually at its peak. In 2026 it grows ~3%. That shock is nearly over. If Dino's margins were 5.5–5.7% before the shock and are 4.5–4.7% now, the question is whether those 100–120 basis points come back as the wage pressure decelerates, or whether the price war has permanently taken them. Revenue growing +14.8% and like-for-like sales growing +4.4% during a price war is not the fingerprint of a company losing. It looks more like a company absorbing a cost cycle while still taking share.

4. How They Make Money

Dino's economics run on three interlocking engines.

First: negative working capital. Customers pay cash at the register today. Suppliers wait 74 days to get paid. Dino collects money, holds it, earns on it, and then pays suppliers. At PLN 25B in annual cost of goods sold, 74 days of float is roughly PLN 5B sitting in Dino's account at any moment — money they didn't borrow and don't pay interest on. This is why Walmart and Costco have historically needed almost no external capital to grow: the suppliers are involuntarily lending it to them. Dino has the same structural advantage.

Second: vertical integration. Dino owns Agro-Rydzyna, a meat processing operation. They cut, package, and distribute their own meat products. This removes a middleman's margin and gives them fresher, cheaper product on the shelf. Their owned distribution network means the trucks run on their schedule with their cost structure. Every link in the chain they control is a link they don't pay a third party for.

Third: format discipline. Every store is roughly the same size, the same layout, the same assortment. This sounds boring. It is the source of their advantage. Boring formats scale. Boring formats make training predictable, inventory management tractable, and supplier negotiations simple. The person who owns the one interesting artisanal format in Warsaw is not building 62 stores per quarter.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Three overlapping fortifications.

Geography first. In a rural town of 8,000, there is probably room for one or maybe two grocery stores of Dino's format. Once Dino has the site, the economics of building a competitor are bad — too small a market to split. This is a local monopoly, repeated 2,700 times. Biedronka and Lidl prefer higher-density locations. They are not racing to build in every village Dino has already claimed.

Vertical integration as a cost floor. Because Dino processes its own meat and runs its own trucks, it has a structural cost advantage that competitors cannot easily replicate in the short term. A competitor would need years and significant capital to build an equivalent supply chain. In the meantime, Dino keeps the cost edge.

The founder at 51%. Tomasz Biernacki owns slightly more than half the company and has paid himself no dividend, issued zero stock-based compensation to management, and kept the share count flat for four consecutive years. His wealth goes up only if the company gets more valuable. He is not extracting; he is compounding. This is extremely rare in public markets, and it removes an enormous source of capital misallocation risk.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The real danger is not that a competitor builds more stores. The real danger is a margin erosion that turns structural rather than cyclical.

Here is the scenario. Biedronka (Portugal's Jeronimo Martins, operating 3,700+ stores in Poland) and Lidl decide to compete more aggressively in small towns on price. To hold market share, Dino keeps its prices down, absorbing the margin hit. Wages keep rising moderately. The combination means margins never recover to 5.5% — they settle permanently at 4% or lower. Meanwhile, the DPO squeeze reversal Burry identified: if Dino's suppliers get fed up with 74-day payment terms and push back to 57 days, that drains roughly PLN 1.2–1.8B from the cash position with no revenue change at all. At that point, the owner-earnings thesis collapses — not because the stores stopped working, but because the financial architecture that funded the rollout gets disrupted.

The key monitoring question is simple: do EBITDA margins stabilize at or above 6.7% as we move through 2026? One or two quarters of data will tell the story.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Think about buying a house. You are looking at a house in a neighborhood where comparable houses have sold for PLN 37–40 when they were in normal condition. This particular house is on sale at PLN 29 because the previous owners had a rough few years: their income was squeezed by a temporary spike in costs, they needed to spend heavily on adding extensions, and the accounting made it look like they were losing money even though the house itself is sound and the extensions are nearly done.

The "absurdly cheap" floor — the price at which you would buy it even if it never grew again — is PLN 16. That is the Graham floor: what is the business worth with zero growth, just existing assets? PLN 16. The current price of PLN 29 is comfortably above that floor, meaning you are not in distressed territory.

The fair-value ceiling — what the house is worth once you normalize for the temporary cost disruption and credit the new extensions at a reasonable return — works out to roughly PLN 37–40. That is calculated as normalized earnings of about PLN 1.7–2.2 per share multiplied by 17–18x, which is the appropriate multiple for a good-but-not-exceptional business with a solid moat and a clean balance sheet.

At PLN 29, you are buying at about 75 cents on the dollar relative to that ceiling. Not a screaming bargain — not "50 cents on the dollar" back-up-the-truck territory — but a real discount for a real business. The margin of safety is genuine. The risk is that the ceiling proves optimistic because margins do not recover, in which case fair value drops to PLN 22–25 and you have overpaid slightly at PLN 29.

This is what value investing looks like in practice: not a certainty, but a bet where the odds are in your favor, the downside is manageable, the structure is proven, the owner is aligned, and the price already embeds a meaningful dose of pessimism. The council's verdict is BUY with moderate conviction — meaning: own it, size it sensibly, and watch the margins with discipline over the next two quarters.
