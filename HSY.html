Bear Case #2: "GLP-1s will kill chocolate consumption"

Counter: This is the bear narrative with the best macro story but the weakest actual data.

Even in the bear scenario, GLP-1 uptake at scale (15-20% of US adults in 10 years) with a 67% reduction in sweets consumption would represent roughly a 10-13% structural reduction in the addressable market — spread over a decade. The GLP-1 bear case assumes maximum penetration + maximum behavioral change + zero brand resilience. None of those three assumptions survive contact with actual consumption data.

1. CULTURAL ROLE: Ritual, Not Utility

Hershey occupies the rarest slot in consumer culture: ritual indulgence. This is not utility (like buying ibuprofen) and not pure status signaling (like buying a Rolex). It is the third category — emotionally anchored, calendar-driven, identity-adjacent.

Three observations confirm ritual status:

The calendar moat. Reese's Peanut Butter Cups and Hershey's Kisses are not merely associated with Halloween, Valentine's Day, and Easter — they are those holidays for tens of millions of households. You do not decide in October whether to buy Halloween candy; you decide which bag to grab at CVS. The decision at shelf is habit execution, not purchase deliberation. This is a culturally embedded behavioral loop with 100+ years of reinforcement.

Brand-as-noun. "Hershey bar" is the generic American term for a milk chocolate bar. "Reese's" has genericized the peanut-butter-cup category to the degree that consumers say "get me some Reese's" the way they say "get me a Kleenex." This is the linguistic embedding that network effects produce in software — here it exists in snack culture. Once a brand achieves noun status, it requires active mismanagement to dislodge. Oreos achieved it. Jell-O achieved it. Kit Kat's "have a break" verb use in UK culture is the closest international analogue.

Emotional pricing power. Chocolate is comfort food with documented cultural valence around stress, celebration, and grief. Hershey benefits from this association in a way that neither a chip brand nor a cereal brand does. The emotional category means that consumers anchor to the brand, not the price, for the specific occasions where it matters most.

Rating: Cultural ritual. The core role is strong.

2. TRIBAL DYNAMICS: Happy Buyers, Not Captives

The Captive vs. Devotee test yields a clear answer: Hershey consumers are devotees, not prisoners. The checkout lane impulse buyer who grabs a Reese's cup is not trapped — they could grab a Snickers. They don't, habitually, because the brand has earned the default position. That is community behavior, not monopoly behavior.

Tribal loyalty maps:

  • Reese's tribe: Intensely loyal, skews across all demographics, generates social media content organically (Reese's-themed recipes, Halloween costumes, "Reese's for breakfast" TikTok micro-genre). This tribe is growing.
  • Hershey's Kisses tribe: Older, gifting-oriented, nostalgia-heavy. The foil-wrapped Kiss is a grandmother's candy bowl artifact. This tribe is not growing.
  • Kit Kat tribe: Casual, convenience-oriented. Licensed from Nestle — HSY does not own the global brand equity. If Nestle ever reclaims the US license, this tribe evaporates overnight.
  • Salty Snacks tribe (SkinnyPop, Dot's): Younger, health-adjacent, trend-sensitive. Not yet loyal to HSY as parent — loyal to the sub-brand. This matters: if HSY fumbles the brand architecture, these buyers don't follow the corporate logo.

Gatekeepers and cultural reinforcers: Social media candy-culture (TikTok, Instagram) overwhelmingly features Reese's — the product photographs well, the flavor combination is universally legible, and the cup format generates portion-control messaging (which accidentally aligns with permissible-indulgence zeitgeist). The NPR skimpflation episode — featuring the Reese's creator's grandson campaigning against Hershey for reducing PB content — is a meaningful gatekeeper moment. It does not threaten the brand today, but it seeds doubt about authenticity and corporate stewardship. In a cultural environment where consumers increasingly distrust large food conglomerates, this narrative is ammunition for "craft" and "clean" competitors to exploit.

3. GENERATIONAL ANALYSIS: Split Verdict

This is the most important section for the long-term thesis, and the data is genuinely contradictory.

The positive evidence: - 70% of Gen Z and 63% of Millennials likely to return to restaurants for Hershey-branded desserts [SEARCH per Center for Generational Kinetics, commissioned by HSY Foodservice]. Branded dessert association is strong. - Reese's, not Hershey's, is the primary engagement vehicle for younger cohorts. Reese's has social media cultural saturation that Hershey's lacks. - The salty snack acquisitions (SkinnyPop, LesserEvil, Sour Strips) address Gen Z snacking preferences — portion flexibility, organic/clean positioning, novelty formats.

The negative evidence: - IDealogic Brand Lab study (undated, but relevant): Hershey brand "very relevant to older audiences but has yet to establish relevance with younger demographics." The Hershey's chocolate bar is a Boomer/Gen X icon more than a Gen Z one. Cadbury resonates more in UK. Lindt resonates more with premium-seeking Millennials. The Hershey bar itself may be a cultural relic even while Reese's is a cultural institution. - The 70% Gen Z dessert stat is from 2018 and is foodservice-specific (restaurants) — this is not the same as "Gen Z buys Hershey products at retail." There is a meaningful gap between brand-in-context (dessert tray at restaurant) and brand-as-default-choice (checkout lane decision). The Gen Z data point flatters the brand more than it should given the source and framing. - GLP-1 drugs are not just a demand headwind — they are a cultural reframing. The narrative that is taking hold among health-conscious Gen Z and Millennial consumers is that snacking is a behavior to engineer out of your routine. GLP-1 adoption as a cultural signal (not just pharmacology) shifts how young adults talk about candy. Even non-users may be influenced by the social norming of "I don't snack anymore." The Lindt data point (GLP-1 users buying more chocolate) is encouraging but measures current behavior, not cultural trajectory. - The My Kids Test: A 12-year-old in 2026 is Gen Alpha. Their candy touchstones are likely to be driven by YouTube/TikTok creator culture — Stanley cups, Lunchly, novelty candy from viral videos. None of these are Hershey's. The brand is not present in the cultural formation moments of the youngest consumer cohort. Reese's has some presence via meme culture; the Hershey bar does not.

Generational verdict: Reese's is crossing into Gen Z successfully. Hershey's (the core chocolate bar brand) is not. The portfolio's survival depends on Reese's continuing to carry the franchise and on salty snack acquisitions successfully establishing new cultural footholds with younger buyers.

4. ORGANIZATIONAL CULTURE: Preservation Dominant, Adaptation Emerging

The Hershey Trust problem. A company where ~80% of voting control rests with a charitable trust that has a legal mandate to preserve the Hershey community (the town, the school, the legacy) is structurally conservation-oriented, not innovation-oriented. The Trust rejected Mondelez's bid not on financial grounds alone — it was a cultural act of preservation. This is a double-edged cultural fact: it gives the company extraordinary stability and long-term orientation, but it also means the board and management operate under constraints that no other publicly traded consumer brand faces.

Kirk Tanner: Cultural mismatch risk. Bringing in a Wendy's QSR operator to run a brand-heritage confection company is, anthropologically, a signal of board pragmatism over cultural stewardship. Tanner was hired to fix operations and margins — both legitimate. But the cultural risk is that an outsider CEO optimizes for the metrics that matter to investors (ROIC, margin recovery) while eroding the intangible cultural capital that makes the brand valuable in the first place. The skimpflation campaign against Reese's is the canary — any further reformulation or cost-reduction that consumers can taste is a cultural event, not just a cost event.

Innovation cadence: The M&A playbook (Dot's, SkinnyPop, LesserEvil, Sour Strips) represents defensive cultural absorption — buying brands that Gen Z has already chosen rather than growing them organically. This is smart but carries integration risk: sub-brand consumers who are loyal to LesserEvil's organic positioning may resist if Hershey's parent brand bleeds into the experience. Authenticity is the currency of Gen Z food culture; Hershey's is not perceived as authentic.

Glassdoor/job posting signals: Not extracted in dossier. However, the public record of a 130-year-old Pennsylvania company with Trust control and a new outsider CEO suggests cultural tension between legacy operators and the new regime. Worth watching in transcript language.

5. CROSS-CULTURAL COMPARISON: The Brand That Doesn't Travel

The international problem is a cultural problem, not a logistics problem. International segment: $941.6M revenue, $3.3M segment income (0.4% margin). This is not a distribution failure — it is a cultural failure. The Hershey's bar is deeply, specifically American. Its sweetness level, its texture (the slight waxiness that is a function of the manufacturing process), its cultural associations (Milton Hershey, Pennsylvania, wartime ration bars) — none of these translate abroad.

Cross-cultural analogues: - Cadbury vs. Hershey: Cadbury (Mondelez-owned) travels internationally; Hershey does not. The reason is cultural embeddedness: Cadbury is tied to British/Commonwealth identity, which travels with diaspora. Hershey is tied to American identity, which does not generate the same global diaspora loyalty. - Kit Kat (US): The one globally-resonant brand in HSY's portfolio in the US is Kit Kat — which is licensed FROM Nestle, the company that actually understands cross-cultural brand management. If Nestle ever reclaims the US license, Hershey loses its only globally-tested brand. - Japanese Kit Kat comparison: Nestle's Kit Kat Japan generates $500M+ annually with hundreds of regional flavors and cultural co-creation. Hershey's Kit Kat in the US is a straight chocolate-wafer bar with minimal innovation. This is a case study in the cultural gap between Hershey's preservation-oriented culture and the innovation culture that drives global brand relevance.

Could a culturally-native competitor displace Hershey in the US? In the mass-market segment, no — the distribution moat and shelf-space dominance are too entrenched. In the "better-for-you" and premium segments, this is already happening. Tony's Chocolonely (Dutch, fair-trade-native), Compartes (DTC, Gen Z aesthetic), and other challenger brands are winning the premium cultural conversation. Hershey's response — acquiring LesserEvil and Lily's — is the right defensive move, but the parent brand provides no cultural lift to these acquisitions. The M&A is entirely additive, not synergistic.

1. INVENTORY VELOCITY — The Freshness Test

DIO trend (TTM basis): | Period | DIO | |--------|-----| | 2023 | 79 days | | 2024 | 78 days | | TTM 2025 | 67 days |

Signal: Improving — but for the wrong reason.

The 11-day reduction in DIO (2024 → TTM) looks like operational efficiency, but the denominator is distorted. Revenue grew +4.4% while inventory grew +14.4% in absolute dollars ($1.25B → $1.43B). What's actually happening: the inventory dollar value is rising due to cocoa cost inflation embedded in raw materials, while physical volume turnover appears faster because revenue recognition at elevated prices inflates the denominator.

This is the classic trap: unit velocity is not improving — cost-per-unit is rising. A factory floor supervisor who reports "we're moving chocolate faster" while his raw material cost is up 40% is not winning; he's hiding the problem behind a ratio.

Verdict on inventory: YELLOW FLAG. Nominal DIO improvement masks cost-driven bloat. Watch whether DIO normalizes further as cocoa prices cool, or whether physical inventory build continues. The correct comparison is unit volume in/out, not dollar DIO.

2. SUPPLIER POWER — The Squeeze

Structural vulnerability: high.

HSY's cocoa supply chain is a textbook single-source risk:

  • 70% of global cocoa supply: West Africa (Ghana + Ivory Coast) [SEC/dossier]
  • Climate risk: Cocoa swoon shoot virus threatens 15% of Ivory Coast supply [dossier moat threats]
  • Political/regulatory risk: EUDR compliance costs, Living Income Differential (LID) disputes with Ghana/Ivory Coast [dossier]
  • Tariff exposure: US tariffs on West African cocoa (since exempted for Ghana Nov 2025 [dossier]), but structural fragility persists
  • Swiss trading company: Sophisticated procurement arm, but the $423M M2M derivative loss in TTM 2025 proves the hedge is imperfect, not absent

The Apple comparison: Apple built out of single-source by diversifying to Vietnam, India, Mexico over 15 years — an active strategic operation. Hershey has not materially diversified cocoa geography in a decade. The Swiss trading company is a sophisticated middleman, not a structural fix. It optimizes within a concentrated supply base; it cannot conjure cocoa from non-existent alternative sources.

DPO deterioration: SERIOUS SIGNAL.

DPO dropped from 72 days (2024) to 59 days (TTM 2025) — a 13-day compression. There are two interpretations:

  1. HSY's cash position tightened and suppliers demanded faster payment — negotiating leverage reversed
  2. HSY chose to pay faster to maintain relationships with stressed cocoa suppliers during supply crunch

Either interpretation is bad for an operations auditor. Apple at 90+ DPO negotiates FROM strength. Hershey at 59 days and declining is operating from a position of supply dependency. When you need your supplier's goodwill to maintain supply, the squeeze is on YOU, not them.

McLane concentration (27% of revenue [SEC]): This is a structural distribution dependency. McLane is Berkshire-owned and stable, but 27% revenue concentration through a single distributor creates operational fragility. Any disruption — pricing negotiation failure, distribution reorientation — hits revenue immediately with no substitute channel.

The Hershey Company: A Feynman-Style Investment Lesson

1. What This Company Actually Does

Hershey is America's candy company. They make the chocolate bars, Reese's peanut butter cups, and Kit Kats you grab at the checkout line — and increasingly the Dot's Pretzels and SkinnyPop popcorn you eat watching a movie. Think of them as the company that owns the emotional real estate of American sugar rituals: the orange-wrapped Reese's on Halloween, the foil-covered Kisses in Valentine's Day heart-boxes. They sell $11.7 billion worth of that feeling every year.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a cyclical/timing problem — with a narrative overlay that makes it trickier than a clean commodity cycle.

Here is what that means. Hershey does not have a broken business. Their brands are intact, retailers still give them premium shelf space, and Americans still buy their chocolate at roughly the same rate. But their earnings look terrible right now, and the stock price has compressed to reflect that. Why? Because cocoa — the raw ingredient in chocolate — has gone through a historic price super-cycle. The price of cocoa roughly doubled (and at points tripled) from 2022 to 2024, driven by West African crop failures and a structural supply disruption. That is not Hershey's fault, and it is not permanent. But it crushes margins in the near term because you cannot instantly pass through a 2x input cost to consumers without volume damage.

So the "problem" an investor faces is purely one of timing: are we near the bottom of the cost cycle, where normalized earnings will recover, or is there something structurally broken underneath?

The narrative overlay complicates this. Two stories haunt Hershey's valuation right now. First, GLP-1 weight-loss drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy) — the market fears a future where millions of Americans simply lose their appetite for candy. The data is ambiguous at best; candy volumes have not shown a measurable GLP-1 dent. But ambiguous data is enough to suppress a multiple when investors are already nervous. Second, private-label chocolate is gaining shelf space as consumers trade down during inflation. These are real questions, but they are narrative overlays on top of a fundamentally cyclical earnings compression story.

The practical consequence: Hershey's current earnings per share (~$5.30 TTM) are meaningfully below mid-cycle normalized earnings (~$8.36). If you anchor your valuation to TTM earnings, the stock looks expensive at 36x. If you anchor to mid-cycle earnings, you are paying 23x for one of America's most durable consumer franchises. The entire investment question is: which earnings number is real?

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Cyclical investing requires a different mental operating system than growth investing. Here is a reusable framework.

Rule 1: Normalize across the cycle, never extrapolate trough or peak. Trough earnings make cyclicals look expensive (high P/E). Peak earnings make them look cheap (low P/E). Both are illusions. The right question is: what does this business earn in a normal year, in the middle of a normal commodity cycle? For Hershey, that mid-cycle number is roughly $8.36 EPS. That is your anchor.

Rule 2: Distinguish cycle compression from structural impairment. Cocoa prices will mean-revert — they always do; no commodity super-cycle lasts forever and supply responds to price signals. That is cycle compression. If, however, GLP-1 drugs permanently reduce candy consumption by 20%, or if private label permanently captures Hershey's shelf space, that is structural impairment — and it permanently lowers the mid-cycle earnings estimate. These require different responses. Cycle compression = patience and possibly buying. Structural impairment = reassess the entire thesis.

Rule 3: Buy near cycle bottoms, not cycle peaks. The best entry points for cyclicals are when the news is worst, margins are squeezed, and the market is most pessimistic. Hershey's cocoa costs may be near peak pain now (cocoa prices have started to fall from highs). That creates a potential entry window — if you believe the franchise is structurally intact.

Rule 4: The multiple itself is cycle-dependent. Cyclicals compress to low multiples at earnings peaks (the market knows it won't last) and expand to high multiples at earnings troughs (the market prices in recovery). This means a 23x multiple on mid-cycle earnings today might look like a 15x multiple two years from now when earnings recover. Multiple expansion and earnings recovery can compound powerfully.

Rule 5: Watch management buyback behavior. Disciplined management buys back stock at cycle troughs (when shares are cheap) and conserves cash at peaks. If Hershey's management bought heavily at $192 while cocoa was crushing earnings and the stock was down 40% from highs — that is a signal of conviction. Buybacks at peak prices are a red flag that management has lost the plot.

4. How They Make Money

Hershey earns money in the most straightforward way imaginable: they buy cocoa, sugar, and peanuts; combine them in factories they have spent 130 years optimizing; and sell the result through every grocery store, convenience store, and Walmart in America. About 80% of their revenue is domestic. The salty snacks segment (Dot's Pretzels, SkinnyPop) adds diversification and some insulation from cocoa volatility.

The financial engine works because of pricing power. Hershey has historically passed through 4-5% annual price increases without meaningful volume loss. Consumers do not do the math on whether Reese's went from $1.49 to $1.59. They buy it anyway because it is a ritual, not a considered purchase. At $192.63 per share, you are paying roughly 23x mid-cycle earnings of $8.36 — a fair but not cheap price for a franchise of this quality.

Reese's is the single best-selling candy brand in the United States by dollar volume. "Hershey's" is not just a brand name — it is the generic American word for milk chocolate, the way "Kleenex" is the word for tissues. That linguistic ownership took 130 years to build and cannot be replicated.
