Decision: WAIT.

Because the floor I just computed is built on normalized earnings that do not yet exist — they require the Amazon analogy to play out through a credit cycle that has not been tested. Munger's discipline: the margin of safety exists to make the forecast unnecessary. Here, at $1,660, the entire gap between price and value is the forecast. That is not margin of safety; that is paying for the thesis. I want the market to either (a) hand me the price with proof, or (b) hand me a cheaper price that gives me safety without needing the proof. Steve Jobs — Product Soul Analysis: MercadoLibre (MELI)

1. THE "NO" TEST — Is This Focus or Sprawl?

Here is the test I always applied at Apple: a company that says yes to everything is a company that believes in nothing.

MELI's portfolio looks sprawling on the surface — marketplace, payments, credit, logistics, advertising, crypto, classifieds. But look at what they killed. Mercado Shops, discontinued and folded into "Mi Página" (Dec 2025) [SEC]. That is the tell. Only companies with real editorial conviction kill products that have users. It takes more courage to say no to your own engineers than to say no to a competitor.

The super-app is not sprawl — it is integration. Every element connects to the others. Payments enable the marketplace. The marketplace generates the repayment data. The repayment data enables better credit scoring. Better credit enables more merchants to scale. More merchants need logistics. Logistics generates more data. Data powers ads. Ads are the margin. This is not a portfolio of bets. This is a machine.

The coherence test: could you remove any layer and leave the others intact? No. Pull Mercado Pago out and the credit flywheel dies. Pull logistics out and the merchant value proposition halves. Pull ads out and you lose the margin engine that funds the reinvestment. That is integration — the pieces need each other. That is coherent.

Verdict on the "No" Test: PASS. Focused ecosystem, not a conglomerate.

2. THE LOVE METRIC — Loved or Trapped?

I always said the best products create customers who feel they chose you, not customers who feel they are stuck with you. Switching costs built on genuine delight are durable. Switching costs built on pain are brittle.

MELI's cross-sell flywheel rose from 26% to 36% of merchants adopting multiple services [SEARCH]. That is not a coerced metric. You do not force a small business in São Paulo to use your credit product — they choose it because their alternative is a Brazilian bank with absurd fees and a 6-week approval process. The adoption is volitional. That is love-adjacent.

The "Mercado Libre" and "Mercado Pago" names have become cultural defaults across LatAm — closer to a verb than a brand [SEARCH, ANTHROPOLOGIST extract]. When a category becomes a verb, you have won the product war. Amazon. Google. Uber. MercadoPago.

The weakness: there is no formal NRR, no cohort retention study in this dossier [SEC, Section G empty]. I cannot quantify the depth of the love. I know the cross-sell rate is rising. I know unique buyers in Brazil grew 32% — the fastest in five years — even as competition intensified [SEARCH]. That is not the metric of a company losing consumer loyalty. But I want to see the churn data. I want to see what percentage of lapsed merchants return. Without it, I cannot fully distinguish "they love us" from "we are the least bad option."

The honest call: it is closer to love than to trap, but the evidence is circumstantial.

Verdict on Love Metric: LIKELY LOVED — directional confidence ~70%, formal evidence incomplete.

3. THE INTEGRATION — Landlord or Tenant?

This is the question I obsessed over at Apple: are you on someone else's platform, subject to their terms and their whims — or do you own the stack?

When I brought the Mac in-house, when I insisted on our own silicon, when I said no to Flash — that was because every dependency is a vulnerability. A landlord can raise rent. A platform can change the rules. A supplier can find a better customer.

MELI owns: - The marketplace rails [SEC] - The payment rails — Mercado Pago #1 MAU in Argentina, Chile, and Mexico; #2 in Brazil [SEC] - The logistics infrastructure — fulfillment covers more than half of shipments, dedicated aircraft, MELI Places [SEC] - The credit underwriting (proprietary on-platform repayment data) [SEC] - The advertising inventory [SEC]

They are the landlord. Their LatAm merchant and consumer are the tenants. The Citi $466M credit line for SME lending [SEARCH] is not tenancy — that is MELI using external capital to extend their own credit product to their own ecosystem. They borrowed from Citi; they did not become Citi's tenant.

The one genuine tenancy risk: Brazil's PIX instant payments infrastructure [SEARCH]. PIX is a government-built rail that MELI uses but does not own. If Brazil routes consumers to PIX-native wallets that bypass Mercado Pago, there is a margin compression vector in the payments layer. This is the exception to an otherwise full-stack ownership position.

Verdict on Integration: LANDLORD — with a single tenant dependency on government-owned PIX rails in Brazil.

4. THE NEXT BIG THING — Inventing the Future or Optimizing the Past?

The difference between great companies and timid ones: the great ones do not find a market, they create one. They bring into existence something that could not exist before them.

MELI is creating LatAm digital commerce. Let that sit. LatAm has 650 million people [SEC]. E-commerce penetration is well below the US, UK, and China. Fintech penetration lower still. The unbanked in Brazil, Mexico, Argentina — they had no path to consumer credit, no digital payment identity, no way to build a credit history. Mercado Crédito gives them one. Mercado Pago gives them a digital account. This is not feature optimization. This is infrastructure construction in places where the infrastructure did not exist.

The Bezos analogy holds here: AWS did not find the cloud computing market — AWS created commercial expectations for it. MELI is creating the expectation that a small merchant in Monterrey can reach 80 million Mexican consumers, accept digital payments, access working capital, and ship next-day. That expectation did not exist before them.

The $3.4B investment in Argentina and $4.6B in Mexico [SEARCH] — the market reads this as margin destruction. I read it as land-grab. When you own the logistics and payment infrastructure of a country's digital economy, you become very hard to displace. That is what is being built.

The reinvestment-suppressed margins are not a bug. They are the proof that management understands what they are building. The company that "harvests" LatAm e-commerce in 2026 loses LatAm e-commerce in 2030 to whoever kept investing.

Mercado Ads growing 73% YoY [SEARCH] — this is the beginning of the high-margin future. Advertising follows attention. Attention follows commerce. MELI is the place LatAm commerce happens. The advertising flywheel is early; it will be enormous.

The risk that is real: Mercado Crédito. The credit book is growing so fast that provisions for doubtful accounts nearly equal operating income [SEARCH]. This is not a product insight failure — it is an operational risk. MELI may have great data on their borrowers relative to a bank, but no LatAm credit cycle has yet tested this book at scale. If Argentina macro deteriorates, if Brazil slows, the credit provisions spike, and the narrative flips from "tech platform" to "EM lender." The company has not invented away macroeconomic risk. That is the genuine uncertainty.

Verdict on Market Creation: INVENTING THE FUTURE — with a real credit-cycle tail risk that could pause but not end the story.

SOUL VERDICT

The soul of a great product company is visible in its decisions under pressure. What did MELI do when Amazon showed up with capital, Shopee showed up with low prices, TikTok showed up with social distribution? They built more logistics. They deepened the fintech flywheel. They grew the credit book. They did not flinch, they did not discount the brand, they did not pivot.

Management's stated posture — "The right response is not to harvest — it is to invest" — that is not a CFO line. That is a builder's line. That is someone who believes in what they are building more than they believe in the current quarter's consensus.

The product is coherent. The integration is real. The market being created is generational in scope. The willingness to kill products (Mercado Shops) and keep investing through criticism is the behavior of a company that knows what it is.

The one shadow on the soul: the credit book. Lending is not a product of love — it is a product of need. Mercado Crédito works because the alternatives are worse, not because borrowers prefer it aesthetically. That is a different relationship than the marketplace or the payments product. It is strategically essential, but it is utilitarian, not beloved. If the credit cycle turns, the utilitarian relationship will reveal itself clearly.

SOUL VERDICT: INSANELY GREAT — with a caveat. The core product soul — the marketplace + payments + logistics super-app — is "Insanely Great." The credit book is powerful infrastructure, not a beloved product. The soul of the company earns its rating. The risk is financial, not architectural.

MercadoLibre (MELI): A Feynman Teaching Note

For: An intelligent adult who has never studied MELI before Date: 2026-06-24 | Price at analysis: ~$1,660

1. What This Company Actually Does

Imagine you're a small business owner in Mexico City. You need a place to sell your goods, a way to accept digital payments from people who don't have credit cards, a loan to buy more inventory, a delivery truck to ship your products, and an app where you can run ads to reach customers. MercadoLibre is all of that — in one ecosystem, across 18 countries, for 650 million Latin Americans. It is the Amazon of Latin America, the PayPal of Latin America, the FedEx of Latin America, and the business bank of Latin America, all running inside a single app called "Mercado Libre" — a name so woven into daily life that people use it as a verb, the way Americans say "Google it."

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a growth compounder in the middle of an identity crisis — and understanding that distinction is the whole ballgame.

Here is the situation: MELI's revenue grew 49% in the most recent quarter. That is not a typo. Almost half again as much revenue as a year ago. And yet the stock fell 12.7% the day after that earnings report was released, and sits 32–40% below its all-time highs. How is that possible?

The answer is that the market is undergoing a narrative re-rating. For most of its history, the market valued MELI as a high-growth tech platform — the kind of business you value on a revenue or user multiple, the way you'd value Amazon or Shopify. But over the last two years, MELI has been building a massive lending business called Mercado Crédito. It now carries a $12.5 billion loan book — meaning it has lent $12.5 billion to merchants and consumers across Latin America. The provisions (money set aside for loans that might go bad) have grown so fast that they now roughly equal the company's entire operating income.

The market looked at that and said: "Wait. This is no longer just a tech platform. This is also a bank. And we don't know how to value a tech platform that is also a bank. So we're going to punish it until we figure it out."

This is the identity-crisis overlay: the company's actual business quality has not deteriorated, but the market's mental model of the company has shattered. When the mental model breaks, the stock can fall sharply even as the underlying business grows. The market is also punishing a deliberate choice: management has explicitly said it is investing rather than harvesting profits. S&M spending is up. Logistics infrastructure is being built. Credit provisions are rising because the loan book is growing fast, not because loans are going bad at alarming rates.

This matters to you as a student of investing because you will see this pattern again and again. Whenever a high-quality business makes a deliberate choice to suppress near-term profits in order to build long-term competitive position, the market often reads the income statement decline as weakness and panics. The stock goes on sale. The question — always — is whether the investment is building real long-term value, or whether it is a rationalization for a deteriorating business. That is the forensic task.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

Here is a reusable four-part framework for analyzing any growth compounder that looks optically expensive or whose earnings are confusing.

Step 1: Separate Deliberate Reinvestment from Real Deterioration. When a company's earnings fall, ask: is management choosing to invest, or is the business actually weaker? The signals of deliberate reinvestment are: revenue still growing fast (MELI +49%), management is explicitly saying "we are investing, not harvesting," and the investments have a clear strategic rationale (logistics infrastructure, credit scoring data, merchant acquisition). Real deterioration looks different: gross margins collapse, customers churn, revenue slows, management turns evasive. MELI shows the reinvestment pattern, not the deterioration pattern. The historical analogue is Amazon from roughly 1998 to 2010 — twelve years of "why is this company not profitable?" that rewarded patient holders with enormous returns.

Step 2: Normalize Earnings. When a company is deliberately suppressing profits, valuing it on today's earnings is like buying a farmhouse that the previous owner stripped for parts, and valuing it as stripped — rather than valuing what it will produce once properly equipped. MELI's net margin is 6.9% today. As recently as 2024 the company was running at 9.2%. The question is: what would the margin be if MELI stopped building and started harvesting? Apply that normalized margin to today's $29 billion revenue base, and you get a far more accurate picture of earnings power than the reported number. The Silicon Council normalized earnings to roughly $2.6–2.9 billion versus the reported $2.0 billion — a meaningful difference that changes the apparent valuation.

Step 3: Recognize That Fintech "Free Cash Flow" Is Often a Mirage. This is one of the most important accounting lessons in investing. MELI's pipeline initially reported "free cash flow" of $11.8 billion — more than six times its reported net income of $1.9 billion. That looks extraordinary. But the gap is almost entirely explained by the way a lending business works: when you grow a loan book rapidly, cash comes in the door as customer deposits and borrowings, which shows up as operating cash inflows. That cash is not free — it is owed to depositors and lenders; it is the raw material that funds the loan portfolio. Taking it out of the business would collapse the lending operation. The honest owner-earnings figure is far closer to net income. This lesson applies everywhere: any business that has a large financial-services component (insurance reserves, bank deposits, loan books) will show reported cash flows that look nothing like true owner earnings. Always ask: "Is this cash free to take out and give to shareholders, or does it have to stay in the business to keep the engine running?"

Step 4: Distinguish Paying for a Forecast from Buying with a Margin of Safety. The hardest discipline in investing. When you buy a stock, you are always paying for some picture of the future. The question is whether you are paying for a specific rosy future (which must come true for you to be right), or whether you are paying a price so obviously cheap that you'd be fine even in a mediocre future. A margin of safety means the latter. At $1,660, MELI is trading at roughly 44 times its current reported earnings. For that to be "cheap," you must believe the normalized earnings are materially higher AND that the credit book performs well. You are not paying for the known; you are paying for the forecast. Margin of safety investing says: demand either a price that's cheap enough on today's facts alone, or wait for enough proof of the reinvestment thesis that the forecast becomes a near-certainty. Never confuse a great business with a great price — they are different things.

4. How They Make Money

MELI has five interlocking revenue engines:

Marketplace fees. More than 90% of goods sold on the platform come from third-party sellers — MELI takes a percentage of each transaction (the "take rate"). This is pure platform economics: MELI doesn't own the inventory, it just provides the marketplace and pockets a fee every time a transaction happens.

Mercado Pago — payments. When a buyer pays a seller, the money usually flows through Mercado Pago, MELI's payment system. MELI charges a small fee on that payment volume. Crucially, Mercado Pago works off-platform too — anyone with a Mercado Pago account can accept or send payments, buy on installment, hold a digital balance, or get a debit card. This turns Mercado Pago into a standalone bank-like utility for tens of millions of people who have never had a traditional bank account.

Mercado Crédito — lending. MELI now lends money to both merchants (business loans) and consumers (personal credit). This is the fast-growing, controversial engine. MELI has a potential edge: it can see exactly how much a merchant sells, how quickly they restock, and whether buyers complain — data no bank can replicate. That data theoretically lets MELI price credit risk better than a traditional lender. The loan book has grown to $12.5 billion, and provisions for potential losses now run roughly equal to the company's operating income — making this the single most consequential line item on the income statement.

Mercado Ads — advertising. Amazon proved that a marketplace is secretly an advertising business. Sellers pay to appear higher in search results, and that advertising revenue is extraordinarily high-margin because the infrastructure already exists. MELI's Ads business grew 73% year-over-year in the most recent quarter — it is the margin lever that can re-expand blended profitability as it scales.

Mercado Envios — logistics. MELI owns and operates a logistics network: fulfillment centers, its own aircraft, last-mile delivery. More than half of all packages are now shipped through MELI's own network. This gives MELI speed and reliability advantages over rivals who depend on third-party carriers, and falling unit costs as volume grows.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Amazon tried. Shopee (backed by Sea Limited, one of the most aggressive digital competitors on earth) tried. Both have spent enormous capital competing in Latin America. MELI kept gaining share. Brazil unique buyers grew 32% year-over-year — the fastest pace in five years. Why can't deep-pocketed rivals dislodge them?

The answer is the ecosystem flywheel. Each part of MELI's business makes every other part stronger.

More buyers attract more sellers. More sellers drive more competition, which lowers prices and improves selection. Lower prices and better selection attract more buyers. The growing transaction volume feeds Mercado Pago, which accumulates payment data on millions of people. That payment data makes credit scoring better — MELI knows who pays their bills, who doesn't, and it knows this from on-platform behavior, not from a credit bureau. Better credit scoring lets MELI lend more profitably. Profitable lending funds more logistics infrastructure. Better logistics means faster delivery, which attracts more buyers. Around and around.

A new entrant cannot buy this flywheel. They can build a marketplace, but they have no payment data. They can build a payment system, but they have no credit data. They can hire delivery drivers, but they have no volume to fill the trucks efficiently. MELI spent twenty years accumulating all of it simultaneously. The cross-sell flywheel is measurable: the share of merchants using MELI for both commerce and financial services has grown from 26% to 36% in recent years.

This is why "Mercado Libre" has become a verb in Latin America — the way "Google" became a verb in search. Cultural verbs are extraordinarily hard to displace.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

Of all the risks — Amazon competition, currency devaluation, regulation, management transitions — one stands above the rest: the $12.5 billion loan book has never been through a bad Latin American credit cycle.

Here is the problem stated plainly. MELI has been lending aggressively for only a few years, during a period that was not uniformly disastrous for Latin American borrowers. The loan book has grown fast enough that provisions (money set aside for loans that go bad) now roughly equal operating income. That means if charge-offs — the rate at which borrowers simply stop paying — were to materially worsen, they could wipe out the operating profit of the entire company.

The bull argument is elegant: MELI has proprietary behavioral data on its borrowers (purchase history, repayment on the platform, business volumes) that no traditional lender has. This might mean MELI can predict defaults far better than a bank, keeping charge-off rates structurally lower. The bear argument is simpler and more frightening: no one has ever run this specific credit book through a genuine Latin American recession, peso devaluation, or unemployment spike. The data-flywheel story sounds compelling — but it is an untested hypothesis about a $12.5 billion bet.

Compounding this: roughly 42% of MELI's direct contribution margin (the profits before corporate overhead) comes from Argentina alone — a country with a long history of economic crises and currency collapses. MELI is doubling down there, investing $3.4 billion in Argentina in 2026. If the current Argentinian economic reform holds, that is enormously valuable. If it reverses, the pain is concentrated and severe.

This is the risk that keeps the most careful analyst on the Silicon Council — the forensic accountant — from buying. He is not wrong to raise it.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Imagine a beautiful house in the best neighborhood in the city. The owner is mid-renovation — scaffolding covers the facade, the new kitchen is half-installed, and the contractors are still working. The house will be magnificent when it's done. But right now it looks like a construction site, and the income it would generate as a rental is currently low because you can't fully use it.

A naive buyer looks at the rental income today, applies a simple multiple, and says the house is overpriced. A sophisticated buyer looks at the house's bones — the neighborhood, the lot, the structural quality, what comparable finished houses rent for — normalizes the rental income for the completed renovation, and forms a view of fair value. Then they ask: what am I actually being asked to pay?

MercadoLibre is that house. The renovation is the reinvestment cycle — the logistics infrastructure, the credit book, the merchant acquisition spending that is depressing today's earnings. The bones are extraordinary: a dominant ecosystem with 15+ years of moat-building, 44% gross margins, 27 consecutive quarters of 30%+ revenue growth, and a customer base of hundreds of millions of people across an underpenetrated continent.

The problem is the price tag. At $1,660 per share, you are paying roughly 44 times today's depressed earnings. The sophisticated valuation says: if the renovation succeeds and net margins recover toward 9–10%, normalized earnings are around $2.7 billion. A fair multiple for that quality of business on that growth rate is perhaps 18–26 times. That implies a fair value range of roughly $1,900 to $2,760. At $1,660, you are technically trading below the bottom of that range.

But here is the trap: that entire fair-value range is built on normalized earnings that do not yet exist. They require the renovation to succeed AND the $12.5 billion loan book to survive whatever cycle comes next. The price is not obviously cheap on the facts that exist today; it is only cheap if you believe the forecast. That is called "paying for the thesis," and it is the opposite of a margin of safety.

The disciplined response is not to panic and walk away from a beautiful house. It is to wait for one of two things: either proof that the renovation is proceeding on schedule — provisions stabilizing relative to income, margins beginning to re-expand, the ROIC slide arresting — at which point you might buy near the current price with real evidence behind you; or a cheaper price tag, something around $1,400, that makes the house a bargain even if the renovation takes longer than expected.

At $1,660, MELI is a wonderful business at a price that demands your thesis be right. The honest investor's answer is: wonderful businesses deserve respect, and respect means waiting until the bet is clear.
