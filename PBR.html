Petrobras (PBR) — A Feynman Explanation

1. What This Company Actually Does

Imagine Brazil owns a magical underground lake of oil — one of the richest oil deposits on the planet, buried 7 kilometers beneath the ocean floor off the Brazilian coast. Petrobras is the company Brazil created to pump that oil up, refine it into gasoline and diesel, and sell it to every driver, trucker, and farmer in the country. Think of it as the national water utility, but instead of water it pumps oil, and instead of a city it supplies an entire continent-sized country. The government owns the controlling stake, which means every major decision — how much to charge for gas, how much profit to pay out to investors, whether to build a new refinery — is ultimately a political decision, not just a business one.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a regime/political problem — the dominant type in all of investment analysis and the one most investors handle badly.

Here is why that label matters. Most of the time, when you analyze a company, you are asking: "Is this a good business? Is it cheap?" Those are the right questions for a clean analytical problem — a stable retailer, a software firm, a consumer brand. But with Petrobras, those questions are necessary but not sufficient. The oil in the ground is genuinely world-class. Petrobras can pump a barrel of oil for roughly $30–35 in production cost, while that same barrel sells on world markets for $70+. That is an extraordinary advantage — comparable to Saudi Aramco. The financial numbers look compelling: the stock trades at about 7 times last year's earnings, the geological resource is worth far more than the market price, and production is growing.

And yet every serious analyst who has studied Petrobras eventually arrives at the same wall: none of that matters if the controlling shareholder decides to extract the value before it reaches you.

Here is the concrete evidence. In May 2023, Petrobras abandoned its market-based fuel pricing policy and began selling gasoline below what it would cost to import it. That decision cost minority shareholders directly — it compressed margins overnight. In May 2024, the CEO who pushed back on this policy was fired. In March 2026, the government passed a decree mandating a diesel fuel subsidy. In April 2026, Petrobras was directed to buy back a refinery it had already sold. In every case, the controlling shareholder — the Brazilian federal government — used its voting majority to redirect value away from minority equity holders and toward voters and political goals.

This is the definition of a regime/political problem: fundamentals are real, but the pathway from fundamentals to your account balance runs through a political decision-maker whose interests do not reliably align with yours. You are not just analyzing an oil company. You are analyzing an oil company AND a government's intentions simultaneously — and the government gets the final vote.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

When you recognize that a company is regime/political, you need a different mental toolkit. Here is the framework in five steps:

Step 1: Separate the physical asset from the equity claim. The oil field is real and durable — its value does not depend on politics. But what you are buying is not the oil field; you are buying a minority equity claim on whatever profits the controlling shareholder chooses to let flow through to you. These are two completely different things. Always price them separately. Ask: "What is this asset worth as a private deal?" and then ask: "What discount should I apply because I cannot control how that value is distributed?"

Step 2: Treat the political variable as the load-bearing variable — not the oil price. With a normal oil company, the swing variable is Brent crude. With Petrobras, even that is conditional — because the government can mandate below-market domestic prices, making Brent less relevant than Brasilia. Every model you build becomes "if the regime holds" or "if the regime shifts." When the key variable in every scenario is the same political event, you are not doing financial analysis — you are doing political forecasting.

Step 3: Size your position as if you could be wrong about the regime. If the political variable resolves in your favor, the stock might double. If it resolves against you, it could fall 40–50% as the multiple compresses from 7x toward 4x (the Pemex/Gazprom range). This asymmetry — modest upside if right, severe downside if wrong — means that even a 60% probability in your favor may not justify a full position. The margin of safety required for a regime/political problem is larger than for a clean analytical problem.

Step 4: Watch the direction of travel, not the current snapshot. A single political intervention might be an anomaly. Three interventions in the same direction in 36 months are a trend. The sequence at Petrobras — IPP abandoned, CEO fired, subsidy mandated, refinery re-acquired — is not random noise. It is a direction. Trend analysis is as important as valuation here.

Step 5: Identify what observable event would resolve the uncertainty. For regime/political problems, do not "watch and wait" passively. Define in advance the observable trigger that would move you from uncertain to willing to act. For Petrobras, that trigger is the October 2026 Brazilian election — and, after that, whether a new government appoints a market-oriented CEO and reverses the pricing policy within 90 days. Without that trigger definition, you will sit in a fog indefinitely, periodically tempted by the cheap valuation.

This framework applies far beyond Petrobras. It applies to every state-controlled energy company (Saudi Aramco before its IPO, Gazprom before 2022, YPF after Argentina's renationalization in 2012), every business whose pricing is regulated by a government motivated by votes rather than returns, and every company in a country where rule of law over minority shareholders is uncertain.

4. How They Make Money

Petrobras makes money in two ways that are deeply connected. First, it pumps oil and gas out of the ground — this is called the upstream business, and it is extraordinarily profitable because the pre-salt fields off the Brazilian coast produce oil so cheaply. Second, it refines that crude oil into gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel in its network of refineries, then sells those fuels to Brazilian consumers — this is called the downstream or refining business. The whole integrated machine runs on scale: Brazil needs about 3 million barrels of oil equivalent per day, and Petrobras supplies the lion's share of it. There are no real competitors at scale inside Brazil's borders, so the company is effectively the national energy infrastructure.

The catch is that the downstream business (selling refined fuel to Brazilians) is where the government intervenes. When world oil prices rise, a Brazilian president worried about inflation can instruct Petrobras to hold domestic fuel prices below world prices — making the downstream business lose money in a way that is invisible to voters but very visible to shareholders.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Three things make Petrobras close to indestructible as a physical entity. First, the geology: the pre-salt oil fields (with names like Búzios, Tupi, and Mero) are among the most productive offshore fields on Earth. These reservoirs took 120 million years to form under a layer of salt 2 kilometers thick — no competitor can replicate them. Second, the legal structure: Brazil's constitution designates oil production in sovereign waters as a federal government activity, and Petrobras has preferred rights as the state-controlled operator. A foreign competitor cannot simply show up and start drilling. Third, the scale: Petrobras controls about 75% of Brazilian oil production, runs most of the country's refineries, and owns the pipelines and storage that connect them. Displacing this infrastructure would take decades and hundreds of billions of dollars.

The important caveat is that this moat protects the physical business — not necessarily the equity investor. As the Biologist on the analysis council put it: Petrobras is the apex predator in Brazilian waters, but equity holders are food, not passengers.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The single biggest risk is not the oil price, not the competition, and not even the debt load. It is this: the Brazilian government, which holds voting control, uses that control to run Petrobras as a social policy instrument rather than a profit-maximizing business — indefinitely. This has happened before. From 2003 to 2014, Petrobras was compelled to sell fuel below cost for years to suppress inflation. The result was a $20 billion write-down, a debt crisis, and a share price collapse of more than 80% from peak. The Lava Jato corruption scandal was partly a symptom of this same dynamic: when commercial discipline is subordinated to political goals, all manner of value destruction follows. The current evidence — diesel subsidies, a refinery being bought back rather than sold, and a CEO fired for defending shareholder returns — suggests the same pattern is early-stage but re-emerging. If Lula wins the October 2026 election and the trend continues, the stock could easily trade down to the range where the government's own fiscal requirements would eventually force a correction: well below $15, perhaps below $10.

Imagine a beautiful house in a neighborhood that might get rezoned from residential to industrial. But because of the rezoning uncertainty, it is listed for $220,000. Cheap, right?

Here is the problem. If the rezoning goes through, the house is worth $110,000. If it does not, it is worth $400,000. You do not know which outcome is coming, and the city council is voting in five months. Is $220,000 a bargain?

That is exactly Petrobras at $21.90. The discount is already there; it is the regime risk discount. The question is whether you believe you can call the election better than the market can.
