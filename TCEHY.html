The Business As I See It

Let me invert. What would make Tencent a terrible investment? Three things: the Chinese government dismantles WeChat's bundled utility model, stock-based compensation devours free cash flow so equity holders get nothing, or the debt pile ($406B, opaque, no maturity schedule) detonates during a liquidity crunch.

Burry raises the SBC/FCF ratio at 92%, and that number demands scrutiny. If true as a run-rate, it means nearly every dollar of free cash flow is redirected to employees rather than owners. But the owner earnings figure tells a different story — $34.71B in owner earnings against a market cap that implies roughly 18x. The buyback program is real and aggressive: $73B repurchased in 2025, 5.6% of shares retired over the measurement period, with SBC/Revenue at only 3.4% versus 13.7% for peers. Burry's ratio appears to capture a timing mismatch or accounting quirk rather than structural dilution. When I see SBC running at 3.4% of revenue while peers run at 13.7%, the capital discipline story holds.

The debt concerns me more than the SBC. $406B in debt with no published maturity schedule is the kind of thing that looks fine until it doesn't. Goodwill at $154B and growing adds another layer of balance sheet opacity. But the $105B investee portfolio provides a meaningful offset — it's a margin of safety embedded in the asset base that the market largely ignores when pricing the operating business.

Bull-Bear Balance

The bull case is straightforward and powerful. WeChat is not a social network — it is the operating system of Chinese commercial life. 1.38 billion monthly active users, 949 million Mini Programs, 80 million merchants. The behavioral lock-in is not the "I like this app" kind; it is the "my entire economic life runs through this" kind. The Anthropologist and Psychologist both independently identified this as utility-layer stickiness rather than social-layer stickiness, which is far more durable.

Video Accounts at 800M DAU with ad loads 40-50% below Douyin represents $15-20B in monetization headroom — revenue that requires no new user acquisition, no new product, just turning a dial. Mini-games at $18B annualized is genuinely found money. Gaming grew 22%. Marketing services grew 19% against a 14% industry rate. The business doubled net income from $16.87B to $32.93B in two years while the stock traded at a persistent discount to peers.

At 18.1x earnings versus 28.9x for peers — a 37% discount — the market is pricing Tencent as though it has inferior economics. It does not. FCF margins at 25.3% beat peers by 4.7 percentage points. ROIC at 15-16% is in line. Revenue growth at 13.9% is comparable. The discount is entirely a jurisdiction discount.

The bear case is singular but serious. Every risk in the dossier traces back to one root: the Chinese Communist Party has unconstrained authority over Tencent's operations. The 2021 regulatory crackdown demonstrated this is not theoretical. The 1260H list risk (10-15% probability over three years) could trigger mechanical institutional selling of 30-50%. Antitrust fines of CNY 10B+ are expected. GPU supply chains could be geopolitically severed. The VIE structure itself is a legal fiction that Beijing tolerates rather than endorses.

But here is the key inversion: if the CCP wanted to destroy Tencent, it would be destroying the infrastructure of its own digital economy. The Biologist's "keystone species" framing is apt — Tencent is too systemically important for Beijing to dismantle. Regulate, tax, constrain, yes. Destroy, no. The 2021 crackdown proved this: gaming approvals resumed, fines were absorbed, and the business emerged with higher margins and stronger earnings.

If AI capex doubles and margin compresses as guided, ROIC could fall to 13–14% range in 2026–27 before re-accelerating on AI monetization. The critical question is whether the AI distribution bet pays off before the stockpile constraint becomes binding.

Operational Synthesis

The machine is elegant: zero inventory risk, $121B supplier float at 134-day DPO, disciplined SBC at one-quarter of peer rate, expanding gross margins to 16-year highs. These are hallmarks of a well-run platform at scale.

The operational vulnerabilities concentrate in one vector: GPU supply chain is geopolitically severed. This is unlike any constraint Apple has faced — Cook could always call TSMC. The "distribution-first" AI strategy (deploy through WeChat's 1.3B users rather than build frontier models) is operationally rational precisely because it minimizes compute intensity. It is the correct strategic response to a severed supply chain. But it only works if Huawei Ascend chips close the performance gap, or if U.S. export policy reverses — neither is guaranteed.

The 2026 capex doubling is not waste. It is a necessary bet on infrastructure before the stockpile window closes. Execution quality on AI integration into WeChat/gaming distribution is the determinative variable for whether ROIC recovers or continues declining.

Understanding Tencent (TCEHY): A Business Teaching

1. What This Company Actually Does

Tencent is China's digital nervous system. At its center is WeChat — a single app that 1.38 billion people use to text their families, pay their rent, order food, run their businesses, and play games. Think of it as if Facebook, Venmo, the App Store, and Amazon were fused into one app that an entire country couldn't live without. On top of that, Tencent owns or backs more of the world's most popular video games than any other company on earth.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

Problem type: Regime/Political

Here is the uncomfortable truth that no spreadsheet can capture: Tencent is a phenomenal business operating inside a political system that can rewrite the rules of any industry overnight — and has.

In 2021, the Chinese government looked at Tencent's gaming division and decided children were playing too many video games. Within months, minors were restricted to three hours of gaming per week. The company didn't lose a product. It didn't face a competitor. A government official signed a paper, and hundreds of millions of dollars in revenue evaporated. The stock lost more than $170 billion in market value across the broader Chinese tech crackdown — not because the business broke, but because the rules changed.

This is the defining characteristic of a regime/political investment: the fundamentals of the business — its moat, its growth, its cash generation — are not in question. What is in question is whether those fundamentals are allowed to persist. In China's system, the Communist Party (CCP) has ultimate authority over every private enterprise. This is not a bug or an anomaly. It is a constitutional feature of how the economy works. Xi Jinping's government has been explicit that private capital serves national goals, not the other way around.

What makes this genuinely hard is that the risk is not constant or predictable. Most of the time, Tencent operates exactly like a Western tech giant — growing revenues, buying back shares, investing in other companies. The crackdown years (2021-2022) were an exception, not the norm. But "exception" is cold comfort when a single policy shift can erase years of returns.

There is a second layer: US political risk. Tencent sits on the US Section 1260H list of companies with alleged ties to the Chinese military. This doesn't ban the stock today, but it creates a plausible scenario where US institutions are forced to sell their ADR shares — not because Tencent did anything wrong, but because Washington and Beijing are in a cold technology war and Tencent is caught in the middle.

This is why regime/political classification changes everything about how you should think about this investment.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

The Regime-Risk Framework: Four Questions Before You Buy

Investing in a world-class business under authoritarian jurisdiction requires a different mental toolkit than analyzing, say, Coca-Cola. Here is the framework to apply:

Step 1: Separate the business quality from the political wrapper. First, pretend the CCP doesn't exist. Evaluate the business on pure fundamentals. Is the moat real? Is the cash generation durable? Is management honest? Only after you've done this cleanly can you price the political risk properly. Conflating them produces bad decisions in both directions — either ignoring real risk because the business is great, or avoiding a great business because the headlines are scary.

Step 2: Assess alignment between the company and the regime's goals. Not all Chinese companies face equal political risk. The CCP cares most about social stability, national security, and not being embarrassed internationally. Tencent's WeChat is now so deeply embedded in daily Chinese life — payments, commerce, communication — that shutting it down would cause more social disruption than tolerating it. This is a genuine (though not absolute) protection. Ask: does this company serve state goals, or threaten them?

Step 3: Demand a margin of safety large enough to absorb a policy shock. In a clean analytical investment, you might want a 20% discount to fair value. In a regime/political investment, you want enough cushion that even a severe crackdown — one that wipes 40-50% of earnings temporarily — doesn't destroy your long-term return. The discount needs to compensate for the possibility that the rules change, not just the probability that they do.

Step 4: Size the position to match the uncertainty, not your conviction about the business. You can be 90% confident Tencent is a great business and still only put 3% of a portfolio in it, because the remaining 10% scenario (regulatory dismemberment, forced divestiture, ADR delisting) is catastrophic and unhedgeable. Position sizing is the primary risk management tool here — not stop-losses, not hedges.

Step 5: Watch the regime signals, not the earnings reports. In a regime/political investment, the most important quarterly update is not revenue — it's the tone of Party statements about the tech sector. When the regulatory environment is loosening (as it appeared to be in 2023-2024), that's more bullish than a earnings beat. When a new crackdown is signaled, that matters more than FCF growth. You are partly an analyst and partly a political intelligence reader.

4. How They Make Money

Tencent has three main revenue engines:

Gaming generates the largest profits. Tencent either owns outright or holds major stakes in the studios behind League of Legends, Honor of Kings, PUBG Mobile, and roughly 40% of Fortnite's creator (Epic Games). When a teenager anywhere in the world buys a skin in one of those games, part of that money flows to Tencent. Gaming grew 22% last year.

Advertising is the second engine. Because WeChat knows what 1.38 billion people read, who they talk to, what they buy, and where they go, it can sell advertisers extraordinarily targeted access to Chinese consumers. Advertising grew 19%.

Payments and Cloud is the third pillar. WeChat Pay processes an enormous share of China's digital transactions — the equivalent of Visa and PayPal combined, embedded inside an app people already use a hundred times a day. The cloud business serves Chinese enterprises the way AWS serves American ones.

Total revenue: $110 billion USD, growing at 13% annually.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

WeChat is what economists call a network with switching costs — but that phrase doesn't capture how deep the lock-in goes. It's not just that your friends are on WeChat. It's that your landlord only accepts WeChat Pay. Your doctor's appointment is booked through a WeChat mini-program. Your company's internal communications run on WeChat Work. The 949 million mini-programs (essentially apps-inside-the-app) and 80 million merchants mean WeChat has become the operating layer of Chinese commercial and social life. The gaming portfolio adds a second moat: Tencent's ability to identify, fund, and distribute game studios globally is unmatched. They don't just own hits — they own the pipeline that produces future hits.
