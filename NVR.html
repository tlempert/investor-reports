What growth is the market pricing in at $7,000.92?

NVR, Inc. — A Feynman Explanation for the Intelligent Non-Expert

1. What This Company Actually Does

NVR builds houses — roughly 21,000 of them per year under brands like Ryan Homes and NVHomes. But here is the twist that makes them unusual: unlike every other homebuilder in America, NVR does not own the land it builds on. Instead, it pays small deposits to lock in the right to buy finished lots in the future, then only exercises those options when it has an actual buyer waiting. Think of it like an airline that never owns its gates — it just leases them as needed, flight by flight, and never gets stuck paying for empty runways. NVR also runs a captive mortgage company called NVRM, so when you buy the house, they can also give you the loan.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

This is a cyclical/timing problem — and that classification is the most important thing to understand before looking at a single number.

A cyclical business is one where the earnings swing dramatically with macroeconomic conditions, even when the underlying business is perfectly healthy. Housing is one of the most cyclical industries that exists. When mortgage rates are low, people buy houses, builders prosper, and earnings look fabulous. When rates spike — as they did in 2022-2023, going from 3% to 7%+ in 18 months — the same excellent business looks distressed, because buyers evaporate.

NVR is not a "melting ice cube" — a business whose economics are permanently deteriorating. It is a structurally excellent company going through a down-cycle in its underlying market. That distinction matters enormously. If you confuse a cyclical downturn for permanent impairment, you either panic-sell a great business at the bottom, or refuse to buy it at exactly the moment when it is cheapest. Both are expensive mistakes.

Here is where NVR sits today: cancellation rates have risen from 13% to 17%. Backlog is down 17% year-over-year. Average selling price growth (+2.2%) is running below inflation, meaning real pricing power is negative. The mortgage banking arm is actually doing better — refinancing and rate-buydown activity picks up when primary sales slow, which is a lovely counter-cyclical hedge — but the core homebuilding machine is running at reduced RPM. Earnings have compressed from $1.73B at the 2022 peak to $1.34B today.

None of this is surprising or alarming if you understand the cycle. What matters is: is this a temporary compression, or a structural one? The evidence points heavily toward temporary. Demographic demand for housing is not going away — millennials and Gen Z are entering their peak buying years, the country is structurally underbuilt by 3-4 million homes, and the only thing keeping buyers on the sidelines is affordability stress from elevated rates. When rates normalize, the pent-up demand will release. The question for an investor is not "is this a good business" — it clearly is — but "at what price does waiting become worth it?"

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

For cyclicals, the standard valuation playbook gets you killed. Here is the reusable framework:

1. Never anchor on peak earnings. At the top of a housing cycle, NVR earned $1.73B. A naive analyst multiplies that by a P/E multiple and decides the stock is cheap. But peak earnings are an illusion — they will not persist. Always normalize across the full cycle.

2. Use a mid-cycle earnings anchor. For NVR, the three-year average (2023-2025) is $1.53B in net income, or roughly $550 per share on the current share count. That is your analytical anchor. At $7,001 per share, you are paying about 12.7x mid-cycle earnings. That tells you much more than the TTM number.

3. Buy at trough multiples, not peak. The best returns in cyclicals come from buying when everyone is disgusted. In housing, that means buying when cancellation rates are spiking, headlines are terrible, and earnings are depressed. At that moment, the P/E ratio looks terrifying (earnings are low), but the true price relative to normal earnings is cheap. NVR in 2022 — when rates first spiked and the stock fell — was far cheaper on a mid-cycle basis than it is today.

4. Distinguish capex-cycle compression from structural decline. Sometimes a cyclical company looks bad because the industry is temporarily oversupplied or rates are high. That is a capex-cycle problem — it corrects. Sometimes the business model itself is deteriorating (think Kodak or Sears). The analyst's job is to tell these apart. For NVR, the evidence is overwhelmingly in the "temporary compression" camp: the land-option model still works, no major imitators have succeeded, and 2008 proved the model survives even a catastrophic housing crash.

5. Watch for the recovery signal. In housing, the leading indicators are: cancellation rate stabilizing, backlog resuming year-over-year growth, and mortgage rate normalization. You do not need to call the exact bottom. You need to buy at a price where you are being paid enough margin of safety that even if the cycle takes longer than expected, you are not harmed.

6. Cycle-indifferent businesses are rare — and NVR is not one. Businesses like GEICO, Coca-Cola, or Apple sell the same amounts regardless of economic conditions. NVR is not in that category. That does not make it a bad investment — some of the greatest returns in market history have come from great cyclicals bought at the trough. But it does mean you need cycle-awareness baked into your entry price.

4. How They Make Money

NVR makes money three ways, all interlocking.

The core machine is homebuilding. They build about 21,000 homes per year, averaging $460,000 per home, generating roughly $10 billion in revenue. At a 13% net margin (down from 16% at the peak), that is $1.34 billion in net income.

The structural advantage is the land-option model. Instead of buying land — which requires billions in upfront capital and creates enormous writedown risk in downturns — NVR signs Land Purchase Agreements (LPAs). They pay a small, forfeitable deposit (never more than 10% of the land value) to lock in the right to buy finished lots when they are ready to build. If the market turns bad, they walk away and lose the deposit. Their maximum lot-deposit exposure is roughly $900 million. Compare that to D.R. Horton carrying $16+ billion of owned land, Lennar carrying $24+ billion. In a housing crash, NVR's risk is capped; its peers face existential balance-sheet impairment. This is not a clever accounting trick — it is a fundamentally different business model that took decades to build into the developer relationships NVR has.

The third leg is NVRM, their captive mortgage company. When you buy a Ryan Home, NVR's own mortgage team finances the loan. NVRM closed $6 billion in mortgage principal in 2025 and earns origination fees and gain-on-sale income. Counter-cyclically, when homebuilding slows, refinancing and rate-buydown activity picks up. NVRM was up 12% in Q1 2025 while the homebuilding segment was down 24%.

Why do customers pay? Shelter is non-optional. You cannot rent a family of four into a two-bedroom apartment forever.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

Start with the numbers. NVR earns a 47% Return on Invested Capital — three to five times the 10-15% its peers achieve. That gap is not luck; it is the direct consequence of not owning land. When you carry zero land on your balance sheet, every dollar of capital you deploy earns outsized returns.

The land-option model has been operating for 30 years. In those three decades, every major competitor has looked at NVR's economics and tried to replicate the model. None have succeeded at scale. The reason is structural: to make LPAs work, you need decades of trust with independent land developers who will accept your option deposits instead of selling outright to the highest cash bidder. NVR has those relationships. A new entrant does not.

The 2008 stress test is the most compelling data point. When the worst housing crash since the Great Depression hit, Beazer Homes, Hovnanian, and WCI all teetered on bankruptcy. They collectively wrote down billions in land value. NVR earned $100 million. Earning anything during 2008 as a homebuilder was extraordinary. That survivorship was not random — it was the structural consequence of having no land to write down.

Capital allocation is the final layer. NVR has no dividend, no acquisitions, no R&D spending, no goodwill on the balance sheet — none. Every dollar of earnings goes back to shareholders via buybacks. In four years, the company returned $6.5 billion to shareholders on $6.3 billion of cumulative earnings — a 103% payout ratio. Share count has gone from 8 million in 2001 to 2.79 million today. Each remaining share owns a larger piece of an already-excellent business. Warren Buffett's Berkshire holds shares. Francois Rochon's Giverny Capital has it at 3.43% of their portfolio — a conviction position for a high-quality long-only manager.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

Pick any of half a dozen concerns and they are real but manageable — brand complaints, tariffs, slower rate normalization. But there is one risk that is genuinely binary and currently unpriced.

The DC federal workforce contraction. NVR's Mid-Atlantic region — Washington DC, Northern Virginia, Maryland — accounts for roughly 30-40% of their total revenue. It is also their highest-returning region, because that market is wealthy, dense, and underbuilt. The federal government is the largest employer in the DC metro area. If federal workforce reductions in 2025-2026 are deep and sustained, the modeling suggests up to 9.6% housing demand destruction in NVR's single most important market — at exactly the moment when the housing cycle is already soft.

NVR has had 30 years to geographically diversify. They have chosen not to. That is a deliberate strategic choice, and in normal times it is defensible because the Mid-Atlantic relationship is a genuine moat. But it means the company is structurally exposed to a macro event — federal workforce contraction — that no amount of operational excellence can offset. If DC demand drops sharply, NVR's earnings in their most profitable market compress simultaneously with the national cycle downturn. That is the scenario that turns a temporary earnings trough into a painful extended one.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Here is a useful analogy. Imagine a neighborhood where houses have historically sold for $500,000 on average. In a hot market they fetch $600,000; in a downturn they drop to $400,000. The neighborhood itself is excellent — good schools, safe streets, appreciating over time. Someone offers you a house today at $490,000. Is that a deal?

The honest answer is: it is fair value. Not a bargain, not a rip-off. You are paying roughly what the neighborhood is worth on average. If nothing bad happens, you will earn an okay return. But if the market turns down — a recession, a macro shock, a neighborhood-specific event — you could find yourself sitting on a house worth $350,000. You bought at average, not at the moment of maximum safety.

That is NVR at $7,001.

At $7,001, you are paying roughly 12.7x mid-cycle earnings of $550 per share. The council's mid-cycle fair value target is around $8,250 (15x mid-cycle earnings), which means there is real upside over a 2-3 year horizon if the cycle turns and rates normalize. But there is also a 38% potential drawdown if the DC federal workforce shock hits simultaneously with an extended rate-trough scenario and earnings fall to near-zero for a year.

The margin of safety at $7,001 is thin. The math says you want to be buying this stock below $6,200 — roughly 11x mid-cycle earnings — where you have meaningful cushion for the DC tail risk. At $5,500 — roughly a 21% decline from here — you are in Buffett's "become aggressive" zone: a Graham-floor entry that matches NVR's 2008 worst-case precedent.

The waiting strategy here is not pessimism about the business. It is discipline about price. If you already own NVR, the right call is to hold — the buyback machine compounds your per-share value through any trough. If you are putting fresh capital to work, the right posture is to watch two things: a price drop below $6,200 (one cyclical panic away), or resolution of the DC federal workforce overhang (which would raise the ceiling to $7,150 and make current prices acceptable).

Wonderful business. Merely fair price. That is not a reason to hate it. It is a reason to be patient.
