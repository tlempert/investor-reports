The question is whether the moat's half-life shortens under Abel, and I find the evidence says it does not — because the moat is structural (float, scale, brand, decentralization), not personal.

Bull-Bear Balance

The Bull Case, aggregated: Seven experts lean BUY or outright recommend it. Lynch anchors the bull camp most forcefully: $816B in financial assets alone against a $1.035T market cap means the operating businesses — BNSF, BHE, the manufacturing conglomerate, Dairy Queen, See's, the lot — are being valued at roughly $219B, which is less than 5x pre-tax operating earnings. Weighting: I weight the bull case at 65% and the bear case at 35%. Here is why. Burry's opportunity cost argument assumes the cash will remain idle indefinitely. But Berkshire has deployed cash in enormous tranches before — $26B for Burlington Northern, the Bank of America warrants, the Japan trading houses. The $373B cash pile is either the greatest dry powder position in corporate history or the greatest confession of idea bankruptcy. I lean toward the former because Abel was Buffett's hand-picked successor trained inside the culture for two decades, because the decentralized structure means the operating businesses do not depend on Omaha for daily decisions, and because the Historian's Fairfax/Markel analogue is persuasive. 3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

When a company is a collection of separate businesses, the right tool is sum-of-parts (SOTP) valuation. You treat the conglomerate like a filing cabinet full of individual companies, value each drawer separately, then add them up.

Here is why this matters: conglomerates often trade at a "discount to intrinsic value" because the stock market assigns one blunt multiple to the whole thing. A railroad, an insurance company, and a candy business deserve very different multiples. When you unpack them individually — using the earnings multiple a standalone railroad fetches, the price-to-float multiple an insurer commands, the market value of a public equity portfolio — you frequently discover the stock is cheaper than it appears.

The method:

  1. Operating businesses — apply sector-appropriate earnings multiples to each segment's pre-tax earnings.
  2. Investment portfolio — use current market value (it is marked to market on the balance sheet).
  3. Cash — count it dollar for dollar.
  4. Float — this is the key Berkshire-specific adjustment: insurance float is essentially an interest-free loan from policyholders that Berkshire gets to invest. It has real economic value but does not appear as equity.

Add those four layers, divide by shares outstanding, compare to the stock price. If the sum exceeds the price by a meaningful margin — say 10% or more — you have a candidate for purchase.

4. How They Make Money

Berkshire has three distinct money engines running simultaneously:

Engine 1 — Insurance Float. Geico and the reinsurance businesses collect premiums before they pay claims. That gap — $176 billion of it — sits in Berkshire's hands earning investment returns. If underwriting breaks even (which Berkshire usually manages), this $176B is a costless pool of investable capital. It is a structural advantage almost impossible to replicate.

Engine 2 — Operating Businesses. Berkshire owns roughly 60 operating companies that generate ~$47.4 billion in annual earnings. The biggest contributors: insurance underwriting ($24.7B pre-tax), BNSF railroad ($7.2B), Berkshire Hathaway Energy, and a constellation of manufacturers, retailers, and service businesses. These businesses throw off cash regardless of what the stock market does.

Engine 3 — Investment Portfolio. A $267 billion equity portfolio anchored by Apple (25.8% of the portfolio). Berkshire does not trade; it holds. Dividends and occasional sales generate additional cash that gets recycled into buybacks or new acquisitions. Note: Berkshire buys back its own stock when Buffett believed it traded below intrinsic value — a form of capital allocation that creates value without fanfare.

Zero stock-based compensation. Every dollar of earnings belongs to shareholders, not to executives diluting you from behind.
