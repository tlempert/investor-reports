Adobe is a textbook example of what I call a narrative-suppressed clean analytical situation — with a binary litigation overlay on top of it.

Here is what that means.

The fundamentals are clean. Adobe generates $10.3 billion in free cash flow on $23.8 billion in revenue. Its return on invested capital — the single best measure of whether a business is truly excellent — has risen every year for five consecutive years: 33.5%, 40.6%, 44.7%, 55.4%, 62.1%. Ninety-six percent of its revenue is recurring subscriptions. Its gross margins are 89%. These numbers do not describe a company under siege. They describe a machine.

But the stock is priced as if the machine is dying. Adobe trades at 14.5x earnings — half the median valuation of its software peers, which trade at 28.6x. The market is saying: "This business looks great today, but it will be much worse tomorrow." The reason is a dominant narrative: generative AI tools like Midjourney, Canva, and OpenAI's image generators are going to make professional creative software obsolete, the way smartphones made digital cameras obsolete.

The narrative is not crazy. It is just imprecise. The Canva migration is real — but it is concentrated in the lowest-margin 25% of Adobe's revenue (individual prosumers, students, hobbyists). The enterprise core — the 75% of revenue from marketing departments, film studios, ad agencies, and Fortune 500 content teams — is actually accelerating. Revenue re-accelerated to +12% in the most recent quarter, right in the middle of the alleged disruption. ROIC went up, not down.

The binary overlay. On top of the narrative suppression, there is a genuine legal wildcard: class action lawsuits alleging Adobe trained its AI image model (Firefly) on customer-uploaded artwork without consent. If a court agrees, Adobe's entire "commercially safe, IP-indemnified AI" positioning — which is what separates it from OpenAI and Midjourney in the eyes of corporate legal departments — collapses. This is a binary outcome. It does not bend; it breaks or it doesn't. That makes it different from the narrative risk, which is continuous and gradual.

The task, therefore, is not standard DCF analysis. The task is: separate what the narrative says from what the data says, quantify the binary risk honestly, and decide whether the price compensates you for carrying both.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

When you encounter a narrative-suppressed situation, here is the reusable framework — five steps that work on any company where the story and the numbers disagree.

Step 1: Identify the disconfirming fact. Find the single most stubborn piece of evidence that contradicts the dominant narrative. For Adobe, it is the ROIC trend. A business being destroyed by disruption shows falling returns on capital. Adobe's ROIC has risen monotonically for five straight years through the very period the disruption is supposedly occurring. You cannot simultaneously be under existential AI disruption AND post record returns on capital. One of those things is wrong. The data, not the narrative, deserves the benefit of the doubt.

Step 2: Segment the narrative's target. Narratives are usually right about something — they are just imprecise about which part of the business is affected. Slice the revenue base and ask: where is the narrative actually landing? Adobe has two very different customer types. Prosumers (individual creatives, students, hobbyists) are genuinely migrating to Canva and CapCut. But prosumers are 25% of revenue and the lowest-margin segment. Enterprise teams (marketing departments, film studios, agencies) are sticky, accelerating, and have entirely different switching costs. The narrative conflates these two populations into one. That conflation is where the mispricing lives.

Step 3: Ask whether the multiple already compensates you. At 14.5x P/E versus a peer median of 28.6x, the market has already applied a 50% discount to Adobe. The question is not "is the narrative true?" — some of it clearly is. The question is "has the narrative already been paid for?" When a stock is priced at half its peers' multiple while growing revenue at 12% with 62% ROIC, the market is not being cautious. It is being panicked.

Step 4: Isolate the binary risk and size it separately. The Firefly copyright litigation is not a "how much?" risk — it is a "yes or no?" risk. It cannot be discounted like a probability-weighted cash flow; it is more like an option that either expires worthless or triggers a step-change collapse in the investment thesis. The right response is not to ignore it or pretend to know the outcome. The right response is to size the position as if the binary could go wrong — owning enough to matter if it goes right, but not so much that a loss is catastrophic. This is why the Silicon Council's recommendation is 4-6% of portfolio, not 10%.

Step 5: Watch the canary, not the cage. In a narrative-suppressed situation, you do not resolve the disagreement in your head — you wait for data to tell you who is right. For Adobe, the canary is enterprise net revenue retention. If enterprise customers renew and expand their contracts, the disruption narrative is wrong about the core business. If enterprise NRR turns negative, the Historian's "point of no return" triggers and you exit. The thesis is falsifiable. That is what makes it investable rather than speculative.

This five-step pattern — disconfirming fact, segmentation, multiple compensation test, binary isolation, canary identification — applies to any company where sentiment and fundamentals have diverged dramatically.

It is explained by fear. And fear, when it is already fully priced in while the underlying machine keeps compounding, is the definition of a buying opportunity.
