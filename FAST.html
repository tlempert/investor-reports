Fastenal (FAST) — A Feynman-Style Explanation

1. What This Company Actually Does

Fastenal sells the bolts, screws, and industrial supplies that factories need to keep their machines running — the thousands of tiny, unglamorous parts that hold the physical world together. This is a clean analytical problem with a mild cyclical overlay.

Clean analytical means the standard toolkit applies: measure the moat, calculate the return on invested capital, run a discounted cash flow, compare to a bond. There is no geopolitical override here, no drug trial pending, no narrative bubble requiring you to guess how long sentiment runs. Fastenal is a durable, well-run distribution business with 32%+ ROIC for a decade and no accounting tricks.

The cyclical overlay matters because 76% of Fastenal's revenue serves the manufacturing sector. When factories slow down and PMI (the manufacturing activity index) contracts, Fastenal's volume contracts with it. This means you must be careful not to treat peak earnings as normal earnings, and you must remember that the current 40x price-to-earnings ratio is being paid against earnings that might be near a cycle high.

The practical implication: the moat analysis and the quality analysis are settled questions. The only debate worth having is whether the price is right.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

For clean analytical companies with cyclical exposure, the right mental model has two steps.

Step one: separate the business from the cycle. Ask yourself: does this company have a real, durable advantage that survives through multiple industrial cycles? If yes, it deserves a "quality floor" multiple — one above the Graham no-growth baseline, because the earnings are not zero-growth. You are not buying a commodity widget manufacturer. You are buying a franchise that compounds at 32% ROIC.

Step two: normalize the earnings. Don't pay a high multiple on peak earnings. Manufacturing activity has peaks and valleys. If you pay 40 times earnings at the top of the cycle, and the multiple then contracts to 25x while earnings themselves dip 15%, you can lose 40% of your investment in a business you correctly identified as excellent. The business can be right and the investment can still be wrong.

The reusable rule: great businesses at bad prices are just bad prices. You are not buying a lottery ticket — you are buying a stream of future cash flows. If those cash flows yield you 2.3% when a Treasury bond yields 4.3%, you are being paid less to take more risk. Math does not forgive quality.

The patience discipline: stay on the watchlist, build conviction in the business, and wait for the cycle to deliver the price. It usually does.

4. How They Make Money

Fastenal's revenue model is deceptively simple. A factory needs thousands of different bolts, safety gloves, cutting tools, and janitorial supplies to keep running every day. Ordering all of these from different suppliers, managing inventory, and sending purchasing-department staff to the supply closet every hour is genuinely expensive. Fastenal's offer: let us handle all of that.

In practice, Fastenal places an employee directly inside the customer's facility — this is called an "Onsite." That employee manages a network of vending machines and automated bin-scanning devices branded FASTVend and FASTBin. When a machine operator takes safety gloves from a vending machine, the system automatically replenishes the order. The customer pays Fastenal for the supplies used, on monthly terms. They also pay, implicitly, with access to their factory floor — because leaving means firing the Fastenal employee and rebuilding their own purchasing infrastructure.

Revenue today: $8.2 billion, with fasteners down to 30.5% of the mix (declining) and safety products up to 22.2% (growing). The fastener was the door into the factory. Once inside, Fastenal cross-sells everything else.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

The moat has three interlocking parts.

Physical presence is the foundation. Amazon can sell you a bolt online. Amazon cannot put a person inside your plant who knows your engineers by name, understands your production schedule, and restocks your vending machine before the line shuts down. To remove Fastenal, you must fire that person, strip out the vending hardware, rebuild your supplier relationships, and hire a purchasing team. That costs months of disruption and real money. Fastenal has done this at 2,215 large manufacturing sites. Amazon has done it at zero.

Scale density compounds over time. Fastenal operates 1,595 distribution points across North America. That means same-day delivery is often possible because a local branch is nearby. A startup competitor would need to build that infrastructure from scratch — billions of dollars and years of time, in a business with 15% operating margins. The infrastructure itself is the barrier.

Data is the emerging hidden moat. Fastenal's 128,000+ installed vending and bin devices generate proprietary real-time consumption data across thousands of factories. They know what parts fail, when demand spikes before a plant expansion, and which customers are about to need more safety gear. No competitor has this database. No one can buy it.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The primary risk is not competitive — the moat is strong. The risk is that you are paying a price that requires perfection. Fastenal's 76% manufacturing exposure means earnings follow the industrial cycle, and a prolonged manufacturing contraction (PMI below 48 for several quarters) could push revenue growth to near-zero or negative while simultaneously causing the market to question whether 40x earnings is an appropriate multiple for a cyclical distributor. You could see a 15-20% earnings decline compounded by a de-rating from 40x to 25x — a combination that could cut the stock price nearly in half, from $45 to roughly $22, with the underlying business remaining intact. That is not a scenario where Fastenal is broken. It is a scenario where you paid the wrong price for a good business, which is the most common way disciplined investors lose money.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Imagine a house that is genuinely excellent: the best-built home on the block, great neighborhood, solid rental income, no structural issues. A fair buyer values it at $400,000. A competing buyer, impressed by the history and the neighborhood, pays $850,000 — not because they miscalculated the rental income, but because they believe the neighborhood will keep appreciating and they can't imagine this house ever being available at $400,000 again.
