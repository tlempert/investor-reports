Could I give a smart rival $1 billion and destroy Palantir? No. And here is why the number matters: the Ontology is not software, it is organizational memory. Understanding Palantir: A Feynman Explanation

PLTR at $143.10 | April 28, 2026

1. What This Company Actually Does

Palantir builds the operating system for decisions that cannot afford to be wrong.

Start with a concrete image. The Pentagon wants to know: given everything we know — satellite feeds, signals intercepts, troop movement reports, logistics databases — what does the enemy do next? The problem is not that there is too little data. The problem is that there is far too much data, it lives in incompatible systems, it is classified at different levels, and the person who needs the answer has about four minutes.

Palantir's solution — called Gotham, for government customers — creates a single model of reality that pulls those silos together, applies AI and human analyst judgment on top, and produces an actionable output. Not a dashboard. Not a spreadsheet. A living model of the world, continuously updated, that lets a human operator act confidently on incomplete information.

On the commercial side, the product is called Foundry. A pharmaceutical company has drug trial data in one system, FDA submission documents in another, and manufacturing batch records in a third. Foundry weaves them into what Palantir calls an Ontology — a structured map of how every data asset in the enterprise relates to every other. Once an organization's data lives in the Palantir Ontology, the company can deploy AI tools, run simulations, trace decisions to their source, and comply with regulators without rebuilding anything from scratch. AIP — the AI Platform — then layers large-language-model intelligence on top of that Ontology. The combination is what made Swiss Re's payback period 7.3 months.

Revenue today: $4.5 billion. Government (Gotham and Apollo) accounts for 54%; Commercial (Foundry and AIP) for 46%. All of it on zero debt, zero acquisitions, and margins that just doubled.

2. What Kind of Problem This Is

Here is the twist that makes Palantir unusual and important to understand clearly.

The fundamentals are clean. ROIC went from negative 59% in 2022 to positive 18% in 2025 — a trajectory that, in the history of software companies, is nearly unheard of. Operating margin doubled in a single year. Both business segments now print 66% contribution margins. Free cash flow is $2.1 billion on entirely organic growth. Revenue is growing at 56% annually, and the forward guide is 61%. Every expert in our twelve-person council who examined the business itself — the product, the moat, the operations, the flywheel — confirmed: this is a genuine platform business in genuine acceleration.

So why is the verdict WAIT?

Because the price is $143, and even the most optimistic discounted cash flow analysis produces a fair value around $75. The conservative DCF says $21. Owner yield — what the business earns in free cash flow relative to what you pay — is 0.41%. The 10-year Treasury pays 4.3%. You are being offered a 390-basis-point penalty to own PLTR instead of a government bond, with the promise that the gap closes because the business gets much, much bigger.

This is the "great business at any price" trap. It is subtle and it catches sophisticated investors, because they are not wrong about the business. They are wrong about the relationship between business quality and stock returns. Quality and price are two separate inputs. A great business at the wrong price produces poor investment returns. A mediocre business at the right price produces excellent returns. Conflating the two — believing that identifying a great company is the same as making a great investment — is the most expensive mistake in finance.

Palantir is a Narrative/Momentum problem precisely because the narrative (AI supercycle, defense modernization, Ontology lock-in) is built on real facts. Real revenue. Real margins. Real product. The narrative is not fiction. But the market has priced in an outcome — Palantir as the $200 billion AI-platform-of-record for global enterprise — that has not happened yet and may not happen on the timeline the price requires. The fundamentals earn the company a strong moat rating. The price embeds a 2032 outcome at 2026 prices.

3. How to Think About This Kind of Problem

This is a reusable mental model worth building carefully, because you will encounter it again.

The Cisco Lesson. In the year 2000, Cisco Systems was the most valuable company on earth, briefly surpassing $500 billion in market capitalization. It was not a fraud. The internet was genuinely transforming global communications, and Cisco's routers genuinely were the backbone of that transformation. Revenue was real. Margins were strong. Growth was accelerating. The narrative — "Cisco is the plumbing of the internet, and the internet will be everything" — was correct in almost every detail.

Cisco's stock fell 80% from peak to trough. Over the next fifteen years, revenue compounded steadily and the business performed roughly as bulls expected. But investors who bought in 2000 did not recover their money for two decades, because they paid for a 2015 outcome at 2000 prices.

The lesson is not "narratives are dangerous." Narratives can be right. The lesson is: the price at which you buy determines your return, not the quality of the business you buy. When the price already embeds the bull case, you lose even when you are right about the business.

The bull-case-already-in-price trap works like this. At $143, Palantir's market capitalization is roughly $343 billion. To justify that price on discounted cash flows, you need Palantir to generate roughly $20 billion in annual free cash flow by the early 2030s — a ten-fold increase from today's $2.1 billion — and then apply a quality multiple. That is not impossible. It requires Palantir to capture a dominant share of a market that is still forming, to hold margins at current levels through that growth, and to execute without a significant competitive disruption for seven years. Each of those individually is plausible. All of them simultaneously is the bull case — and the price has already priced it in. You do not get paid for the bull case coming true. You only get paid for outcomes that exceed what the price embeds.

The reusable principle: sometimes the answer is "right business, wrong price." The discipline is to hold them as separate questions. First: is this a great business? Second: is this a great price for a great business? The first is about moats, execution, and competitive dynamics. The second is about mathematics. Both must pass. Palantir passes the first test decisively and fails the second.

One more frame worth carrying forward: the gap between public market price and private market value. A strategic acquirer — Microsoft, Oracle, a defense prime — looking at Palantir's actual cash flows would pay 10 to 15 times revenue for the franchise. That is $45 to $65 billion. The public market is paying $343 billion. The $280 billion difference is "option value" — the probability-weighted value of Palantir becoming the AI platform of record for global enterprise. When public price is 5 to 7 times private market value, history says the public is wrong more often than the private is.

4. How They Make Money

Palantir's revenue model is elegant in its simplicity, even if the products themselves are not.

Government customers (54% of revenue, $2.4 billion) pay multi-year contracts for access to Gotham and Apollo. Gotham is the intelligence and military decision-support platform — the one that originated with CIA and expanded across DOD, which now represents $2.5 billion of Palantir's federal contract base. Apollo is the deployment infrastructure that gets Palantir software running in classified environments, including air-gapped networks that never touch the public internet. These contracts are sticky by design: once classified operations are running on Palantir infrastructure, switching is a years-long project requiring new security certifications, new training, and new integrations.

Commercial customers (46% of revenue, $2.1 billion) pay subscription fees for Foundry and AIP. The bootcamp model — Palantir's proprietary sales motion — runs a prospective customer through an intensive deployment workshop where their own data is loaded into Foundry and live results are produced within days. This converts proof-of-concept relationships to paid contracts at unusually high rates. Once a company's Ontology is built in Foundry, they expand within the platform rather than replacing it. Top-20 customer average revenue grew 45% year-over-year, to $93.9 million per customer — that is expansion, not churn.

AIP (the AI Platform) is the new layer that makes existing Foundry and Gotham customers more valuable. It does not exist as a separate product line — it is integrated into what customers already use. This is strategically important: AIP adoption does not require a new sale. It deepens the existing relationship.

5. Why They're Hard to Kill

The durability of Palantir's competitive position comes from three interlocking factors that compound on each other.

The Ontology lock-in is the deepest one. When a company builds its data model in Palantir Foundry, that Ontology becomes the authoritative representation of how the business understands itself — which machines are connected to which supply chains, which patients are linked to which trials, which transactions map to which compliance categories. Migrating that Ontology to a competitor is not a technical project. It is an organizational reconstruction. Swiss Re's 170% ROI and 7.3-month payback did not come from a feature — it came from rebuilding how Swiss Re thinks about its own data. You do not undo that casually.

The defense classification moat is a second category entirely. A significant portion of Palantir's government business runs on classified networks inside classified facilities. The act of being trusted with that data — and maintaining that trust across administrations — is not replicable by a new entrant in any reasonable timeframe. Palantir has been in that world since 2004. The institutional relationships, the cleared personnel, the established security frameworks are twenty years of accumulated infrastructure that competitors cannot simply buy.

The third factor is behavioral: Palantir's customers are trained. Analysts at intelligence agencies, portfolio managers at hedge funds, supply chain operators at manufacturers — they have built workflows around Palantir tools. The switching cost is not just technical and organizational. It is human. Retraining humans is expensive, disruptive, and politically difficult inside institutions. That is a moat that does not show up in any balance sheet line.

6. The One Thing That Could Go Wrong

The most likely path to losing money in Palantir is not a product failure, a moat breach, or a competitive disruption. It is multiple compression — and it is worth understanding the math precisely.

Palantir currently trades at roughly 200 times earnings. That means the market is paying $200 for every $1 of current earnings, betting that future earnings will be dramatically higher. The historical average for a high-quality software company is 30 to 50 times earnings. For a franchise business with durable moats — a Buffett-quality compounder — you might pay 60 to 80 times in a hot market.

Here is what multiple compression looks like in practice. Suppose Palantir executes perfectly over the next three years: revenue grows 60% annually, margins hold at 36%, free cash flow reaches $5 billion by 2028. That is the bull case, running clean. If the market simultaneously re-rates the stock from 200x earnings to 60x earnings — a re-rating that could happen simply because AI narrative excitement fades, or interest rates stay elevated, or retail investors rotate elsewhere — the stock falls materially even as the business thrives.

The Cisco investors lived this. Cisco's revenue in 2005 was higher than in 2000. Its business was fine. But investors who bought in 2000 at 200x earnings still lost money for fifteen years, because the multiple compressed from 200x to 30x while earnings grew. The business won; the 2000 stock buyer lost.

This is why insider behavior matters here. Peter Thiel has sold over $300 million of Palantir stock at current prices. Karp's cancellation of his own sale plan when the stock dropped is a real bull signal — founders do not cancel planned sales without believing in the price. But when the largest outside insiders are selling at the same price the retail narrative buyer is buying, that is a disagreement being expressed in the most direct possible currency. Insiders have no information advantage about the business. They have, arguably, an information advantage about what the business is worth relative to the current price.

7. The Price Tag Problem

Imagine a neighborhood with beautiful houses. One of them is, by any objective measure, the finest house on the block — the best architecture, the most durable construction, the most thoughtful design, the best location within the neighborhood. You want to buy it. The seller names a price that is six and a half times what a rigorous appraisal would produce, and three times what an optimistic appraisal would produce. Do you buy it?

The answer is not about the quality of the house. The answer is about the price relative to what you get. The finest house in the neighborhood at six and a half times appraised value is a bad investment, regardless of how much better it is than the other houses.

Palantir is that house. The appraised value — calculated multiple ways by twelve independent experts — lands between $50 and $75. The asking price is $143. It was $180 a few months ago, and even at $143, after a 20% decline year-to-date, the price still embeds a future that must unfold without error for investors to break even.

The entry point that makes Palantir a compelling investment is below $75. Below $50, the franchise quality at any reasonable growth assumption produces a return that would make a careful investor genuinely excited. At $143, even granting every optimistic assumption — 60% revenue growth sustained, margins holding, AIP becoming the enterprise AI platform of record — the math barely breaks even.

This does not mean Palantir the business fails. Palantir the business may do exactly what bulls expect. The question is always: at what price does that outcome become an investment return rather than just a true story about a great company?

The discipline is to separate admiration for the business from the decision about the stock. You can believe Palantir is the most important enterprise software company of the next decade and still wait at $143. Q1 earnings arrive May 4. If the report disappoints, the multiple may compress toward fair value and create the entry. If it beats, the narrative sustains and the wait continues. The business is not going away. The price will eventually reflect reality.

The big money, as Munger has said, is not in the buying or the selling. It is in the waiting.
